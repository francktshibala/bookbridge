A CHRISTMAS CAROL

A Christmas Carol in Prose, Being a Ghost Story of Christmas by Charles Dickens

PREFACE

In this spooky little book, I've tried to conjure up an idea that won't make my readers upset with themselves, each other, the holiday season, or me. I hope it haunts their homes in a good way, and nobody wants to get rid of it. Your faithful friend and servant, C.D. December, 1843.

STAVE I: MARLEY'S GHOST

Marley was dead: to begin with. There's absolutely no doubt about that. The record of his burial was signed by the clergyman, the clerk, the undertaker, and the chief mourner. Scrooge signed it, and his signature was respected in the business world for anything he chose to invest in. Old Marley was as dead as a doornail. Remember, I'm not saying that I personally know why a doornail is considered particularly dead. I might have thought a coffin-nail was the deadest piece of iron in the trade. But the wisdom of our ancestors is in that comparison; and I won't argue with it, or we're all in trouble. So, I'll emphasize again that Marley was as dead as a doornail.

Scrooge knew he was dead, right? Of course, he did. How could it be otherwise? Scrooge and he had been partners for I don't know how many years. Scrooge was his sole executor, his sole administrator, his only heir, his only friend, and his only mourner. And yet, Scrooge wasn't so terribly affected by the sad event that he didn't make a great business deal on the day of the funeral and celebrated it with a definite bargain.

Bringing up Marley's funeral reminds me to make it clear that Marley was definitely dead. This must be understood, or nothing wonderful can come from the story I'm about to tell. If we weren't completely sure that Hamlet's Father was dead before the play started, him strolling at night in an easterly wind on his own castle walls wouldn't be any more remarkable than any other middle-aged man foolishly going out after dark in a windy place—like Saint Paul's Churchyard, for instance—just to surprise his son.

Scrooge never removed Old Marley's name from the business sign. Years later, it still read "Scrooge and Marley" above the warehouse door. The firm was known as Scrooge and Marley. Sometimes people new to the business called him Scrooge, and sometimes Marley, but he answered to both. It was all the same to him.

Oh, but he was a tight-fisted hand at the grindstone, Scrooge! A squeezing, wrenching, grasping, scraping, clutching, greedy old sinner! Hard and sharp as flint, from which no steel had ever struck out a generous spark; secretive, and self-contained, and as isolated as an oyster. The cold within him froze his old features, nipped his pointed nose, shriveled his cheek, stiffened his walk; made his eyes red, his thin lips blue, and his voice was sharp and grating. His head and eyebrows were frosty, and his chin was bristly. He carried his own low temperature around with him; he chilled his office in the dog days of summer and didn't warm it one degree at Christmas. External heat and cold had little influence on Scrooge. No warmth could warm him, no cold weather chill him. No wind that blew was more bitter than he, no falling snow was more determined, no pelting rain less likely to be persuaded. Bad weather didn't know what to do with him. The heaviest rain, snow, hail, and sleet could only boast of one advantage over him—they often came down generously, and Scrooge never did.

Nobody ever stopped him on the street to say, with a cheerful look, "My dear Scrooge, how are you? When will you come to see me?" No beggars asked him for a little money, no children inquired what time it was, no man or woman ever asked him for directions in his entire life. Even the dogs of blind men seemed to recognize him; when they saw him coming, they would drag their owners into doorways and up alleys; then wag their tails as if to say, "Being blind is better than having an evil eye, dark master!" But what did Scrooge care! It was exactly what he liked. Keeping his distance from all human sympathy was his idea of a good time.

Once upon a time—on Christmas Eve, of all good days in the year—old Scrooge was busy in his office. It was cold, bleak, biting weather: foggy too: and he could hear people outside in the court, wheezing up and down, beating their hands upon their chests, and stamping their feet on the pavement stones to warm them. It had just turned three o'clock, but it was already quite dark—it hadn't been light all day—and candles were flaring in the windows of the neighboring offices, like bright spots on the thick brown air. The fog was so thick outside that, even though the court was narrow, the buildings across the way were mere shadows. To see the dingy cloud come drooping down, obscuring everything, one might have thought nature was brewing up a storm on a grand scale. The door of Scrooge's office was open so he could keep an eye on his clerk, who was copying letters in a dismal little cell, a sort of tank, lit by a very small coal fire that looked like one coal. Scrooge had a very small fire too, but his clerk's fire was so much smaller that it looked like one coal. But he couldn't replenish it, for Scrooge kept the coal-box in his own room; and so surely as the clerk came in with the shovel, the master predicted that it would be necessary for them to part. Wherefore the clerk put on his white comforter, and tried to warm himself at the candle; in which effort, not being a man of a strong imagination, he failed.

Scrooge kept a close eye on his clerk, who was working in a tiny, dismal office that felt more like a cage, copying letters. Scrooge had a small fire going for himself, but the clerk's fire was so tiny it barely counted as one. The clerk couldn't add more coal because Scrooge, who kept the coal supply in his own room, would likely fire him if he tried. So, the clerk wrapped himself in his scarf and attempted to warm up by the candle, a task at which he failed due to his lack of imagination.

"A merry Christmas, uncle! God save you!" came a cheerful voice. It was Scrooge's nephew, who had arrived so suddenly that Scrooge hadn't noticed him until he spoke. "Bah!" Scrooge replied, "Humbug!" The nephew, flushed and lively from his walk in the cold, couldn't believe his uncle meant it. "Merry Christmas! What reason do you have to be merry? You're poor enough," Scrooge snapped back.

The nephew, undeterred, countered, "And what reason do you have to be so gloomy? You're rich enough." Scrooge, lacking a good comeback, just repeated, "Bah! Humbug."

"Don't be so grumpy, uncle!" said the nephew. Scrooge retorted, "How can I be anything else when I'm surrounded by such foolishness? Merry Christmas! What's Christmas to you but a time for paying bills with no money, getting older without getting richer, and having every expense of the past year come back to haunt you? If I had my way," Scrooge fumed, "everyone who goes around saying 'Merry Christmas' should be cooked with their own pudding and buried with a holly stake through their heart!"

"Uncle!" his nephew protested.

"Nephew!" Scrooge replied sharply, "celebrate Christmas in your own way, and let me celebrate it in mine."

"But you don't celebrate it," the nephew pointed out.

"Then leave me alone," Scrooge snapped. "What good has it ever done you?"

"There are many things that have done me good without making me richer," the nephew argued. "I've always thought of Christmas as a time of kindness, forgiveness, and charity. It's the one time of the year when people open their hearts and think of others as equals, not as strangers on different paths. And for that reason, even though it hasn't made me wealthier, I believe it has done me good, and I say, God bless it!"

The clerk, moved by these words, clapped before quickly realizing his mistake and pretending to tend to the fire. "Make another sound," Scrooge threatened, "and you'll spend Christmas unemployed!"

Turning to his nephew, he added sarcastically, "You're quite the speaker. Ever thought about going into politics?"

"Don't be angry, uncle. Come have dinner with us tomorrow." Scrooge flatly refused, suggesting it would be a cold day in hell before he accepted such an invitation.

"Why?" his nephew pressed. "Why?"

"Why did you get married?" Scrooge countered.

"Because I fell in love."

"Because you fell in love!" Scrooge mocked, as if love were the most ridiculous reason of all. "Good afternoon!"

"But uncle, you never visited me before I got married. Why use that as an excuse not to visit now?"

"Good afternoon," Scrooge repeated, refusing to engage further. "I want nothing from you; I ask nothing of you; why can't we be friends?"

"Good afternoon," Scrooge said again, showing his nephew the door. Despite his uncle's coldness, the nephew wished him a Merry Christmas and a Happy New Year as he left, without showing any anger.

He even offered season's greetings to the clerk, who, despite being cold, was warmer in spirit than Scrooge and returned the wishes with genuine warmth.

"There's another one," Scrooge muttered to himself, hearing the clerk's merry wishes. "My clerk, earning so little, with a family to support, and he talks about a Merry Christmas. I might as well be in an asylum."

Just then, two cheerful gentlemen entered, having been let in by the clerk. They were there on business, holding books and papers, and they greeted Scrooge respectfully. "Scrooge and Marley's, I believe," one of them said, checking his list. "Do I have the pleasure of speaking to Mr. Scrooge or Mr. Marley?"

"Mr. Marley has been dead for seven years," Scrooge informed them. "He died seven years ago, this very night."

Every night. "We're sure your generosity is well represented by your surviving partner," said the gentleman, showing his credentials. It certainly was; they had been two peas in a pod. At the dreaded word "generosity," Scrooge frowned, shook his head, and handed the credentials back. "During this festive season, Mr. Scrooge," the gentleman began, picking up a pen, "it's especially important that we provide for the poor and destitute, who are suffering a lot right now. Thousands need basic necessities; hundreds of thousands lack simple comforts, sir."

"Are there no prisons?" Scrooge asked.

"Plenty of prisons," the gentleman replied, setting the pen down.

"And the workhouses? Are they still open?"

"They are. Still," the gentleman said, "I wish I could say they weren't."

"So, the Treadmill and the Poor Law are still going strong?" Scrooge noted.

"Both very busy, sir."

"Oh! I was worried, from what you initially said, that something had stopped them from doing their useful work," Scrooge said. "I'm very glad to hear they're still operational."

"Given that they hardly provide any cheer or comfort to the masses," the gentleman continued, "a few of us are trying to raise funds to buy the poor some food, drink, and means of staying warm. We choose this time because it's when need is most acutely felt, and abundance is celebrated. How much can I put you down for?"

"Nothing!" Scrooge replied.

"You wish to remain anonymous?"

"I wish to be left alone," Scrooge said. "Since you ask what I want, that's my answer. I don't celebrate Christmas myself, and I can't afford to make idle people celebrate. I support the institutions I've mentioned—they're expensive enough; and those who are struggling must go there."

"Many can't go there; and many would rather die."

"If they would rather die," Scrooge said, "they should do so and decrease the surplus population. Besides—excuse me—I'm not aware of that."

"But you might be aware," the gentleman pointed out.

"It's not my business," Scrooge returned. "A man should focus on his own business and not meddle in others'. Mine keeps me fully occupied. Good afternoon, gentlemen!"

Realizing it was futile to continue, the gentlemen left. Scrooge went back to work, feeling better about himself and in a lighter mood than usual. Meanwhile, the fog and darkness thickened, causing people to run about with torches, offering to guide horses and carriages. The ancient church tower, with its old bell always seeming to spy on Scrooge, disappeared into the mist, ringing the hours and quarters as if shivering with cold up there. The cold intensified. On the main street, workers were fixing the gas pipes and had lit a large fire in a brazier, around which a group of shabby men and boys warmed their hands, their eyes sparkling with joy. The water plug, left alone, froze over, turning into ice with a grudge. The brightness of shop windows, decorated with holly sprigs and berries, made pale faces glow as they passed. The trades of butchers and grocers seemed like a magnificent celebration, making it hard to believe they had anything to do with mundane transactions. The Lord Mayor, from his grand Mansion House, ordered his cooks and butlers to celebrate Christmas as befits a Lord Mayor's household; and even the small tailor, recently fined for disorderly conduct, was preparing tomorrow's pudding in his attic, while his thin wife and baby went out to buy beef. The fog grew thicker, the cold more biting. If Saint Dunstan had used such cold against the Evil Spirit instead of his usual methods, the spirit would have roared with pain. A young singer, his nose red and raw from the cold, tried to sing a Christmas carol at Scrooge's door but fled in terror when Scrooge threatened him, leaving the door to the mercy of the fog and frost. Finally, it was time to close the office. Scrooge reluctantly got up from his stool, acknowledging the clerk's expectation, who quickly snuffed out his candle and put on his hat.

"You'll want the whole day off tomorrow, I suppose?" Scrooge grumbled.

"If it's convenient, sir."

"It's not convenient," Scrooge snapped, "and it's not fair. If I deducted half a crown from your pay, you'd think yourself ill-treated, wouldn't you?" The clerk gave a weak smile.

"And yet," Scrooge continued, "you don't think I'm being ill-treated when I pay a day's wages for no work." The clerk mentioned it was only once a year.

"A poor excuse for picking a man's pocket every 25th of December!" Scrooge complained, buttoning his coat up to his chin. "But I suppose you must have the whole day. Just be here even earlier the next morning." The clerk promised he would, and Scrooge left with a growl. The office was closed quickly, and the clerk, with his scarf trailing behind him, headed home.

Wearing just his jacket, since he didn't have a heavy coat, he slid down a hill on Cornhill, joining a line of boys doing the same, twenty times, to celebrate Christmas Eve. Then, he ran all the way home to Camden Town as fast as he could, ready to play blindman's-buff. Scrooge had his usual lonely dinner in his usual dreary pub; after reading all the newspapers and spending the rest of the evening with his bank account, he went to bed. He lived in an apartment that had once belonged to his late business partner. It was a set of gloomy rooms in a dark, old building that seemed so out of place, one could imagine it had wandered there when it was new, playing hide-and-seek with the other buildings, and couldn't find its way back. It was now old and bleak, with Scrooge as its only resident, the other rooms being rented out as offices. The courtyard was so dark that even Scrooge, who knew it well, had to feel his way around. The fog and frost clung to the ancient gateway so thickly, it was as if the spirit of the weather itself was sitting there, lost in thought.

Now, it's true that there was nothing special about the door knocker, except its size. And it's also true that Scrooge, who had lived there for years, had seen it every day without a second thought. Scrooge wasn't one to let his imagination get away from him, not by any stretch. Keep in mind, Scrooge hadn't thought of Marley, his partner who had died seven years ago, since earlier that day. So, it's puzzling how Scrooge, putting his key in the door, saw not the knocker, but Marley's face on it. Marley's face, with a dismal light around it like a spoiled lobster in a dark cellar, wasn't angry or scary but looked just as Marley used to, with glasses perched on its forehead. The hair seemed to move as if stirred by breath or heat, and though the eyes were wide open, they were utterly still. Its pale color made it terrifying, but the fear seemed more than the face itself could intend. As Scrooge stared, it turned back into just a knocker. To say he wasn't shocked, or that he didn't feel a chill he hadn't known since he was a boy, would be a lie. But he grabbed the key, turned it firmly, walked in, and lit his candle. He hesitated before shutting the door, even peeked behind it as if half expecting to see Marley's ghost, but there was nothing there except the hardware for the knocker, so he dismissed it with a "Bah!" and slammed the door, which echoed like thunder through the building. Scrooge wasn't the type to be scared of echoes. He locked the door and made his way upstairs, trimming his candle as he went. The staircase was so wide, you could have easily moved a hearse up it. It was so dark, a handful of street lamps wouldn't have made much difference, but Scrooge didn't mind. He liked the dark because it was cheap.

Before he went to bed, he checked his rooms to make sure everything was as it should be, prompted by the memory of Marley's face. Living room, bedroom, storage room—everything in order. No one hiding anywhere; a small fire in the fireplace; a spoon and basin ready; and a little pot of gruel on the stove (Scrooge had a cold). Everything was in its place. Satisfied, he locked himself in, a double precaution he didn't usually take. Ready for bed, he took off his tie, put on his robe and slippers, and his nightcap, and sat down by the fire to eat his gruel. The fire was barely enough to warm him on such a cold night. He had to sit very close and focus on it to get any warmth from the small amount of fuel. The fireplace, old and built by...

A long time ago, a Dutch merchant had decorated the room with unique Dutch tiles that depicted scenes from the Bible. There were images of Cain and Abel, Pharaoh's daughters, the Queen of Sheba, angels descending on clouds as soft as feather beds, Abraham, Belshazzar, Apostles sailing in butter-boats, and hundreds of other figures meant to captivate the mind. Yet, Marley's face, even seven years after his death, overshadowed them all, like an ancient prophet's rod that swallows everything else. If those tiles had initially been blank, capable of reflecting the scattered pieces of his thoughts, each one would have shown Marley's face. "Humbug!" Scrooge muttered, pacing the room. After a few laps, he sat down again. As he leaned back, his eyes landed on an old bell in the room, once connected to a chamber at the top of the building for a now-forgotten purpose. To his astonishment and a sudden, unexplainable fear, he watched the bell start to swing. It began so softly that it hardly made a sound, but soon it rang out loudly, followed by every bell in the house. This eerie concert lasted what felt like an hour but was probably only a minute. The bells stopped as suddenly as they had started. Then, a clanking noise echoed from below, as if someone were dragging a heavy chain across the cellar floor. Scrooge remembered stories of ghosts in haunted houses dragging chains. The cellar door burst open, and the noise grew louder, coming up the stairs and heading straight for his door. "It's still humbug!" Scrooge declared. "I won't believe it." Yet, his face paled when, without any hesitation, the noise crossed through the heavy door and into the room. As it entered, the dying fire flared up as if to say, "I know him; Marley's Ghost!" before dying down again. There stood Marley, looking exactly as he had in life, with his pigtail, waistcoat, tights, and boots, the tassels and his coat-skirts bristling, and his hair as wild as ever. The chain wrapped around him was made of cash-boxes, keys, padlocks, ledgers, deeds, and heavy steel purses. His body was transparent, allowing Scrooge to see through to the buttons on his coat. Scrooge had heard rumors that Marley lacked a heart, but he hadn't believed them until now. Yet, even with the ghost standing before him, Scrooge remained skeptical, resisting the evidence of his own senses. "What do you want with me?" Scrooge asked, his voice as sharp and cold as ever. "Much!" came Marley's unmistakable reply. "Who are you?" "Ask me who I was." "Who were you then?" Scrooge demanded, a bit sarcastically. "In life, I was your partner, Jacob Marley." "Can you sit down?" Scrooge asked, eyeing him doubtfully. "I can." "Then do it." Scrooge was unsure if a ghost so transparent could actually sit in a chair and worried about the awkwardness if it turned out to be impossible. But Marley sat down as if it were the most natural thing in the world. "You don't believe in me," the Ghost noted. "I don't," Scrooge admitted. "What proof would you need of my reality beyond your own senses?" "I don't know," Scrooge said. "Why do you doubt your senses?" "Because," Scrooge explained, "a little thing can throw them off. A slight stomach upset can make them lie. You might be nothing more than an undigested piece of beef, a dab of mustard, a crumb of cheese, a fragment of an undercooked potato. There's more of gravy than of grave about you, whatever you are!" Scrooge wasn't used to making jokes, and he certainly didn't feel like joking then. He was trying to distract himself and suppress his fear, as the ghost's voice sent shivers down his spine. To sit and stare into those fixed, glazed eyes without saying a word would be unbearable. The ghost seemed to bring its own chilling atmosphere, which Scrooge couldn't feel himself but was evident from the way Marley's hair and clothing fluttered as if heated by an oven's blast. "You see this toothpick?" Scrooge quickly changed the subject, hoping to divert the ghost's piercing gaze. "I do," the Ghost replied. "You're not looking at it," Scrooge pointed out. "But I see it," said the Ghost, "regardless." "Well!" Scrooge retorted, "I have only to swallow this, and I'll be haunted by a legion of goblins, all of my own making. Humbug, I tell you! Humbug!" He saw the air filled with ghosts, rushing around in restless haste, moaning as they moved. Each one was chained like Marley's Ghost; some, possibly guilty governments, were chained together; none were free. Scrooge recognized many of them from their lives. He knew one old ghost, wearing a white waistcoat with a huge iron safe chained to its ankle, crying over its inability to help a miserable woman with a baby on a doorstep. Their shared agony was that they wanted to intervene for good in human affairs but had forever lost the power to do so.

Whether these spirits dissolved into mist, or the mist hid them, Scrooge couldn't tell. But they and their cries faded away, and the night returned to normal. Scrooge closed the window and checked the door the Ghost had used. It was still double-locked, just as he had locked it, and the bolts hadn't been touched. He tried to say "Humbug!" but only got the first syllable out. Overwhelmed by the night's events, the day's fatigue, his peek into the unseen world, the ghost's dull conversation, or the late hour, he desperately needed rest; he went straight to bed, clothes on, and fell asleep instantly.

STAVE II: THE FIRST OF THE THREE SPIRITS

When Scrooge woke up, it was so dark he could barely tell the window from the walls of his room. Trying to see through the darkness, he listened as the nearby church clock struck every quarter hour. To his astonishment, it chimed right up to twelve, then stopped. Twelve! It was past two when he went to bed. The clock must be wrong. Maybe an icicle was jamming it. Twelve! He checked his watch, which confirmed it was indeed twelve. "It can't be," Scrooge thought, "that I've slept through a whole day and into another night. It can't be that something's happened to the sun, and it's noon!"

Worried by this thought, he got out of bed and made his way to the window. He had to wipe the frost away with his robe sleeve to see outside, and even then, he saw very little. All he could tell was that it was still very foggy and cold, and there was no sound of people bustling about, which there certainly would have been if night had taken over the day. This was a relief because his financial dealings would be in trouble if days didn't exist to count by.

Scrooge went back to bed, thinking hard but getting nowhere. The more he thought, the more confused he became, and trying not to think only made him think more. Marley's Ghost was really bothering him. Every time he convinced himself it was all a dream, his mind snapped back to the same question, "Was it a dream or not?"

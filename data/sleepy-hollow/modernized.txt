The Legend of Sleepy Hollow

by Washington Irving

Found among the papers of the late Diedrich Knickerbocker.

A pleasing land of drowsy head it was,
Of dreams that wave before the half-shut eye;
And of gay castles in the clouds that pass,
Forever flushing round a summer sky.
CASTLE OF INDOLENCE.

In the heart of one of those spacious coves that indent the eastern shore of the Hudson, at the wide part of the river known as the Tappan Zee, where the ancient Dutch navigators always carefully reduced their sails and asked for St. Nicholas's protection when crossing, there lies a small market town or rural port. Some call it Greensburgh, but it's more widely and correctly known as Tarry Town. This name, we're told, was given by the good housewives of the surrounding area because of their husbands' strong tendency to hang around the village tavern on market days. Whether this is true or not, I can't say for sure. I only mention it to be precise and authentic. Not far from this village, maybe about two miles away, there is a little valley or lap of land among high hills, which is one of the quietest places in the whole world. A small brook glides through it, with just enough murmur to lull one to sleep; and the occasional whistle of a quail or tapping of a woodpecker is almost the only sound that ever breaks the uniform tranquility.

I remember that, when I was a young boy, my first attempt at squirrel-shooting was in a grove of tall walnut trees that shades one side of the valley. I had wandered into it at noon, when all nature is especially quiet, and was startled by the roar of my own gun, as it broke the silence around and echoed angrily. If ever I should wish for a retreat where I might escape from the world and its distractions, and quietly dream away the rest of a troubled life, I know of none more promising than this little valley.

From the idle restfulness of the place, and the peculiar character of its inhabitants, who are descendants of the original Dutch settlers, this secluded glen has long been known as Sleepy Hollow, and its young men are called the Sleepy Hollow Boys throughout all the neighboring country. A drowsy, dreamy influence seems to hang over the land, and to fill the very air. Some say that the place was enchanted by a High German doctor, during the early days of the settlement; others, that an old Indian chief, the prophet or wizard of his tribe, held his gatherings there before the country was discovered by Henry Hudson. It's certain that the place is still under the influence of some enchanting power, that casts a spell over the minds of the people, causing them to walk in a constant daydream. They are prone to all kinds of marvelous beliefs, are subject to trances and visions, and often see strange sights, and hear music and voices in the air. The whole neighborhood is full of local tales, haunted spots, and twilight superstitions; shooting stars and bright meteors appear more often across the valley than in any other part of the country, and the nightmare, with her whole ninefold, seems to make it her favorite playground.

The main spirit that haunts this magical area, taking command over all the air's forces, is the ghost of a headless horseman. Some say this ghost is a Hessian soldier who lost his head to a cannonball in an unnamed battle of the Revolutionary War. He's often seen by locals, rushing through the night as if carried by the wind. His roaming isn't limited to the valley but sometimes extends to nearby roads and particularly around a church not far away. In fact, some of the most reliable local historians, who've carefully gathered information about this ghost, claim that since the soldier was buried in the churchyard, the ghost rides out to the battlefield at night searching for his head. They say the reason he moves so fast, like a blast of wind through the Hollow, is because he's trying to return to the churchyard before sunrise.

This legend has inspired many spooky stories in this shadowy region, and the ghost is known to everyone around the firesides as the Headless Horseman of Sleepy Hollow.

Interestingly, the tendency to see visions isn't just found in the valley's natives but affects everyone who stays there for a while. No matter how skeptical they were before, soon they start to feel the valley's enchanting effect, becoming imaginative, dreaming, and seeing ghosts.

I praise this tranquil place highly because it's in these secluded Dutch valleys scattered across New York State that life remains unchanged, untouched by the constant movement and changes happening elsewhere in this busy country. They're like calm spots next to a fast stream, where things move slowly, unaffected by the surrounding currents. Even though it's been years since I visited Sleepy Hollow, I bet I'd still find the same trees and families living there, undisturbed in its protected environment.

In this secluded spot of nature, there lived, around thirty years ago, a man named Ichabod Crane. He came to Sleepy Hollow to teach the local children. He was from Connecticut, a state known for producing both pioneers and educators. The name Crane suited him well. He was very tall and thin, with long limbs, hands that seemed too big for his sleeves, and feet large enough to be shovels. His body was loosely put together. His head was small and flat on top, with big ears, large green eyes, and a long nose, making him look like a weather-vane showing the wind direction. On a windy day, seeing him walk with his clothes flapping around him, one might think he was a symbol of famine or a scarecrow come to life.

His school was a simple, one-room log building with windows that were partly glass and partly patched with old workbook pages. It was cleverly locked up when empty, using a twisted branch in the door handle and stakes against the shutters, making it easy to enter but hard to leave, a design likely inspired by an eel trap. The school was in a lonely yet pleasant spot at the bottom of a wooded hill, near a brook and a large birch tree. The sound of his students studying could be heard on a quiet summer day, occasionally interrupted by Ichabod's stern voice or the sound of the birch rod disciplining a latecomer. Ichabod believed in the saying, "Spare the rod and spoil the child," and his students were certainly not spoiled.

However, Ichabod was not a harsh teacher. He was fair, easing the load on the weaker students and being tougher on the stronger ones. The smaller, more sensitive kids were treated gently, while the tougher, stubborn ones received harsher discipline. He saw this as fulfilling his duty to their parents, always assuring the punished child they would be grateful for the lesson later in life.

After school, Ichabod would spend time with the older students and escort the younger ones home if they had attractive sisters or mothers known for their cooking. It was important for him to maintain good relationships with his students. His income from teaching was minimal, barely enough to cover his food expenses, as he ate a lot despite his thin frame. To supplement his income, he followed the local custom of staying with the families of his students, moving from one home to another each week, carrying all his belongings in a handkerchief.

To make sure this wasn't too hard on the wallets of his rural customers, who often saw the cost of education as a heavy burden and thought of schoolmasters as lazy, he found various ways to make himself both useful and likable. He occasionally helped the farmers with the easier tasks on their farms, like making hay, fixing fences, taking horses to water, driving cows from the pasture, and chopping wood for the winter. He also dropped the strict demeanor and absolute control he had in his little kingdom, the school, and became incredibly gentle and friendly. He won over the mothers by doting on the children, especially the youngest; and like the brave lion that once held the lamb so nobly, he would sit with a child on one knee and rock a cradle with his foot for hours on end.

Besides his other jobs, he was the singing teacher of the area, earning many coins by teaching the young people how to sing psalms. It was a source of great pride for him to lead the choir from the front of the church gallery on Sundays, where he felt he outshone the preacher. It's certain his voice stood out above all others in the congregation; and some unique quavers can still be heard in that church, and even half a mile away, across the millpond, on a quiet Sunday morning, believed to have originated from Ichabod Crane's own singing. So, through various small strategies, in that clever way often referred to as "by any means necessary," the respected teacher managed quite well and was considered by those who didn't understand the effort involved in intellectual work to have a very easy life.

The schoolmaster usually holds some importance among the women in a rural area; seen as a kind of leisurely, gentlemanly figure, with far superior taste and skills than the local men, and second in knowledge only to the preacher. Therefore, his presence often causes a bit of excitement at a farmhouse tea, leading to the serving of extra cakes or sweets, or maybe even the display of a silver teapot. Our scholar was particularly favored by the smiles of all the local young women. Imagine how he stood out among them in the churchyard, between services on Sundays; picking grapes for them from the wild vines that climbed the nearby trees; entertaining them with readings of all the tombstone epitaphs; or walking with a group of them along the banks of the nearby millpond, while the shyer local guys hung back, jealous of his superior charm and ease.

Because of his somewhat nomadic life, he also acted as a traveling news carrier, bringing all the local gossip from one house to another, so his arrival was always welcomed with pleasure. He was also regarded by the women as a man of great learning, for he had read several books all the way through and knew Cotton Mather’s “History of New England Witchcraft” inside out, a work in which he deeply and strongly believed.

He was truly a strange mix of slight cleverness and easy belief. His love for the incredible and his ability to absorb it were both remarkable, and living in this enchanted area only made them stronger. No story was too unbelievable for him to accept. He often enjoyed, after letting his students go for the day, lying on the lush clover near the small brook by his schoolhouse. There, he would read old Mather's terrifying tales until the evening turned the pages to mist before his eyes. Then, as he made his way through swamps, streams, and eerie woods to the farmhouse where he was staying, every natural sound at that magical time stirred his imagination—the call of the whip-poor-will from the hill, the ominous croak of the tree toad signaling a storm, the mournful hoot of the screech owl, or the rustle of birds startled from their sleep. The fireflies, shining brightest in the darkest spots, sometimes shocked him when one particularly bright one flew across his path. And if a large, clumsy beetle hit him, he was terrified, thinking it was a witch's curse. His only way to calm his mind or ward off evil spirits was to sing hymns. The people of Sleepy Hollow would often listen in wonder to his singing, which floated from the distant hill or down the shadowy road.

Another way he found scary joy was by spending long winter evenings with the old Dutch women as they sat by the fire, spinning, with apples roasting and popping on the hearth. He listened to their amazing stories of ghosts, goblins, haunted fields, brooks, bridges, houses, and especially the headless horseman, or the Galloping Hessian of the Hollow, as they sometimes called him. He entertained them with his own stories of witchcraft, ominous omens, and eerie phenomena in the sky from Connecticut's early days. He would scare them with theories about comets and shooting stars and the shocking fact that the world actually spins, leaving them upside down half the time!

But as cozy as these moments were, sitting by a warm, crackling fire where no ghost dared appear, the fear during his walk home afterward was a high price to pay. What terrifying shapes and shadows crossed his path in the dim light of a snowy night! How eagerly he looked for any ray of light from a distant window across the fields! How often he was scared by a snow-covered shrub that looked like a ghost blocking his way! How he trembled at the sound of his own steps on the frosty ground, afraid to look back in case some strange creature was following him! And how many times did he panic at a sudden gust of wind, thinking it was the Galloping Hessian on a nightly raid!

All these fears, though, were just night-time terrors, creations of his mind in the dark. Even though he had encountered many ghosts and been targeted by Satan in various forms during his lonely walks, daylight always ended these fears. He could have lived a happy life despite the Devil and all his minions, if not for one creature that brings more trouble to men than all the ghosts, goblins, and witches combined—a woman.

Among the music students who gathered one evening each week for his lessons in singing psalms was Katrina Van Tassel, the daughter and only child of a well-off Dutch farmer. She was a vibrant young woman of eighteen; as plump as a partridge; ripe, melting, and with cheeks as rosy as one of her father’s peaches. She was famous not just for her beauty, but also for her great expectations. She was a bit of a flirt, which could be seen even in her clothing, which mixed old and new styles to best highlight her beauty. She wore pure yellow gold jewelry that her great-great-grandmother had brought from Saardam, the enticing bodice from olden times, and a teasingly short skirt to show off the prettiest foot and ankle in the area.

Ichabod Crane was very tender-hearted towards women; it's no surprise that he quickly took a liking to such an attractive young woman, especially after visiting her at her family home. Old Baltus Van Tassel was the very image of a successful, content, and generous farmer. True, he rarely paid attention to anything outside his own farm, but within its boundaries, everything was cozy, happy, and in good order. He was happy with his wealth but not arrogant about it; he prided himself more on the generous amount rather than the style of his living. His home was on the Hudson River, in one of those green, sheltered, fertile spots that Dutch farmers love so much. A great elm tree spread its branches over it, under which bubbled a spring of the softest and sweetest water, collected in a barrel-made well, then flowing through the grass to a nearby brook that murmured among alders and dwarf willows. Right by the farmhouse was a huge barn, big enough to be a church, bursting at every window and crack with the farm's bounty. Inside, the sound of threshing could be heard from dawn to dusk; swallows and martins flew around the eaves, and rows of pigeons, some looking up as if checking the weather, others with heads tucked in or cooing and strutting around their mates, basked in the sun on the roof. Fat pigs lounged in their pens, occasionally joined by groups of piglets venturing out to sniff the air. A majestic group of white geese swam in a nearby pond, leading fleets of ducks; flocks of turkeys strutted around the farmyard, and Guinea fowls wandered about, fussing like cranky housewives with their grumpy cries. In front of the barn, a proud rooster strutted, the epitome of a husband, warrior, and gentleman, flapping his shiny wings and crowing proudly, sometimes scratching the earth and then generously calling his hungry family to enjoy a tasty find.

Ichabod's mouth watered as he looked at this rich promise of a lavish winter diet. In his hungry imagination, he saw every pig running around stuffed with pudding and an apple in its mouth; the pigeons were comfortably settled into a pie, covered with a crust blanket; the geese swam in their own gravy; and the ducks nestled together in dishes, like cozy couples, with plenty of onion sauce. In the pigs, he envisioned the future slices of tender bacon and juicy ham; not a turkey but appeared ready for the oven, with its gizzard tucked under its wing, and maybe even wearing a necklace of tasty sausages; and even the proud rooster was imagined lying on his back in a dish, claws up, as if asking for mercy he never sought in life.

As Ichabod dreamed of all this, and as his eyes roamed over the fat meadows, the rich fields of wheat, rye, buckwheat, and corn, and the orchards heavy with ripe fruit that surrounded the Van Tassel's comfortable home, his heart longed for the girl who would inherit all this. His imagination soared with thoughts of how easily it could all be turned into cash, and the money used to buy vast stretches of wild land and build grand homes in the wilderness. Indeed, his active mind already saw his dreams coming true, picturing the blooming Katrina with a family of children, riding atop a wagon loaded with household goods, pots and kettles swinging below; and there he was, riding a steady mare with a colt trailing behind, heading off to Kentucky, Tennessee, or someplace even further away!

When he entered the house, his heart was completely won over. It was one of those large farmhouses with high, but gently sloping roofs, built in the style passed down from the first Dutch settlers. The low, extending eaves formed a porch along the front, which could be enclosed in bad weather. Under this porch were hung flails, harnesses, various farming tools, and fishing nets for use in the nearby river. Benches were built along the sides for summer use; and a large spinning wheel at one end, and a churn at the other, showed the porch's many possible uses. From this porch, the amazed Ichabod entered the hall, the heart of the mansion and its main living area. Here, rows of shiny pewter on a long dresser dazzled him. In one corner stood a huge bag of wool, ready to be spun; in another, a quantity of linsey-woolsey fresh from the loom; ears of corn, and strings of dried apples and peaches, hung in colorful festoons along the walls, mixed with the bright red of peppers; and a slightly open door gave him a glimpse into the best parlor, where the claw-footed chairs and dark mahogany tables shone like mirrors; fireplace tools sparkled from their hiding spot among asparagus ferns; mock-oranges and conch shells decorated the mantelpiece; strings of various-colored bird eggs were hung above it; a large ostrich egg was suspended from the center of the room, and a corner cupboard, intentionally left open, displayed vast treasures of old silver and well-mended china.

From the moment Ichabod saw these wonders, he lost his peace of mind. His only goal was to win the love of the unmatched daughter of Van Tassel. However, he faced more real challenges than the knights of old, who only had to deal with giants, enchanters, fiery dragons, and such, which they overcame as easily as cutting into a Christmas pie, and then the lady would simply agree to marry them. Ichabod, on the other hand, had to navigate the whims and caprices of a country coquette, constantly facing new difficulties and obstacles; and he had to deal with a host of real-life rivals, the many rustic admirers who guarded every approach to her heart, watching each other jealously but ready to unite against any new challenger.

Among these, the most daunting was a loud, boisterous man named Abraham, or Brom Van Brunt as per the Dutch shortening, a local hero famous for his strength and daring. He was broad-shouldered and very strong, with short curly black hair, and a face that was rough but not unkind, showing a mix of fun and arrogance. Thanks to his physical prowess, he was universally known as BROM BONES. He was an excellent horseman, unmatched in races and cockfights. His physical strength gave him a leading role in local life, deciding all disputes with a confidence that left no room for argument. He was always up for a fight or fun, but his mischief was more playful than harmful, and despite his rough exterior, he had a good sense of humor. He had a group of close friends who saw him as a role model, and together they roamed the countryside, looking for excitement or trouble. In winter, he was known for wearing a fur cap with a fox’s tail, and at country gatherings, the sight of this familiar emblem among a group of hard riders meant trouble was near. Sometimes, his group could be heard racing past farmhouses at midnight, yelling and whooping like a band of wild riders, and the startled people would say, “There goes Brom Bones and his gang!” The locals viewed him with a mix of respect, admiration, and affection, and whenever there was a wild prank or fight, they were sure Brom Bones was involved.

This wild hero had set his sights on the beautiful Katrina as the target of his rough affections, and although his flirting was more like a bear's clumsy attempts at tenderness, it was rumored she didn’t entirely reject him. Indeed, his advances made other suitors back off, not wanting to challenge him in love matters. So, when his horse was seen tied to Van Tassel’s fence on a Sunday night, a clear sign he was inside courting, or “sparking,” all other suitors gave up in despair and looked elsewhere for love.

Ichabod Crane had to face a formidable rival, and considering everything, a stronger man would have backed out of the competition, and a smarter man would have given up hope. However, Ichabod had a great mix of adaptability and persistence; he was flexible yet resilient. He could bend without breaking. Even under pressure, once it was gone, he would snap back to his original form, standing tall and proud.

Going head-to-head with his rival would have been crazy; after all, this rival was not someone to be easily beaten in love, much like the passionate Achilles. So, Ichabod made his moves in a subtle and smooth way. Pretending to be just the singing teacher, he visited the farmhouse often. He wasn't worried about the parents interfering, which is often a problem for lovers. Balt Van Tassel was laid-back and loved his daughter more than anything, even letting her do as she pleased. His wife was too busy with the house and her poultry, thinking that animals needed more looking after than girls. So, while she was busy inside or spinning on the porch, Balt would relax with his pipe, watching a little wooden soldier battle the wind on the barn roof. Meanwhile, Ichabod would woo the daughter by the spring or during evening walks, a time perfect for romance.

I can't claim to understand how to win a woman's heart. To me, it's always been a mystery. Some women seem to have just one way to their heart, while others have many, and winning them over can be done in countless ways. It's a big achievement to win the first type, but even more so to keep the second type interested, as a man must defend his position constantly. Winning over many is impressive, but keeping a flirt's attention is truly heroic. Unfortunately for the mighty Brom Bones, once Ichabod started making his moves, Brom's chances began to fade. His horse wasn't seen tied up outside on Sunday nights anymore, and a serious rivalry developed between him and the teacher of Sleepy Hollow.

Brom, who had a bit of noble roughness to him, wanted to settle things through a fight, like the straightforward knights of old. But Ichabod knew he was outmatched and avoided direct confrontation. He had heard Brom boast about beating him up, and Ichabod was too cautious to give him a chance. This passive approach frustrated Brom, leaving him no choice but to use his sense of humor to play pranks on Ichabod. Ichabod became the target of Brom's jokes and his friends. They disrupted his singing classes, broke into the school at night, and turned everything upside down, making Ichabod think it was haunted. Even more irritating for Ichabod, Brom made fun of him in front of Katrina, and even trained his dog to mock Ichabod's singing.

For a while, things continued as they were, without really changing the balance between the competing forces. One beautiful autumn afternoon, Ichabod, deep in thought, sat on his high stool from which he usually oversaw his small educational domain. He held a ruler in his hand, a symbol of absolute authority; the birch rod, a tool for punishment, hung on three nails behind his seat, always a threat to those who misbehaved. On his desk lay various confiscated items from his idle students, such as half-eaten apples, toy guns, spinning tops, bug boxes, and many tiny paper fighting cocks. It seemed a severe punishment had just been given out because all his students were either focused on their books or whispering to each other while keeping an eye on Ichabod; a quiet buzz filled the classroom. This quiet was suddenly broken by the arrival of a Black man wearing a simple cotton jacket and pants, with a hat that looked like something Mercury might wear, riding a scruffy, semi-wild young horse, which he controlled with a rope for a bridle. He rode up to the school door with an invitation for Ichabod to attend a fun party or "quilting frolic" that evening at Mynheer Van Tassel’s. After delivering his message with a sense of importance and an attempt at formal language, common on such errands, he crossed the brook and hurried away, full of the urgency of his task.

The once quiet classroom was now filled with noise and activity. The students rushed through their lessons, skipping parts without worry and those lagging behind received a quick hit to motivate them or help them with difficult words. Books were carelessly tossed aside, inkstands knocked over, benches upended, and the whole school was let out an hour early, pouring out like a horde of little demons, screaming and playing on the green, happy for their early release.

Ichabod took an extra half hour getting ready, sprucing up his only suit of rusty black and fixing his hair using a piece of broken mirror in the schoolhouse. Wanting to impress his lady as a true gentleman, he borrowed a horse from the farmer he lived with, a quick-tempered old Dutchman named Hans Van Ripper. Mounted on this horse, Ichabod set off like a knight in search of adventure. But first, let me describe our hero and his horse. The horse, named Gunpowder, was an old plow horse, more known for its bad temper than anything else. It was thin and scruffy, with a neck like a sheep's and a head like a hammer; its mane and tail were full of knots and burrs. One eye was blind and looked ghostly, but the other shone with mischief. Despite its appearance, Gunpowder must have been a spirited horse, given its name and having been a favorite of its owner, Van Ripper, who was known for riding hard and probably passed some of his fiery spirit to the horse. For, even in its decrepit state, it seemed more devilish than any young horse around.

Ichabod was just the right person to ride such a horse. He rode with short stirrups, making his knees stick up high; his elbows jutted out like a grasshopper's. He held his whip straight up, like a scepter, and as they moved along, his arms flapped like wings. A small wool hat sat on his small forehead, and his coat flapped nearly to the horse's tail. The sight of Ichabod and Gunpowder leaving Hans Van Ripper's farm was truly a sight rarely seen in broad daylight.

It was, as I've mentioned, a beautiful autumn day; the sky was clear and calm, and nature was dressed in the rich, golden colors we often link with plenty. The forests had turned to shades of sober brown and yellow, while some of the more delicate trees had been touched by the frost into vivid colors of orange, purple, and scarlet. Flocks of wild ducks started to appear high in the sky. The sound of squirrels could be heard from the groves of beech and hickory-nuts, and the thoughtful whistle of the quail came at intervals from the nearby stubble field.

The small birds were enjoying their goodbye feasts. In their celebration, they flitted, chirping and playing from bush to bush, and tree to tree, whimsical from the sheer abundance and variety around them. There was the honest cock robin, a favorite target of young hunters, with its loud, complaining note; and the twittering blackbirds flying in dark clouds; and the golden-winged woodpecker with his crimson crest, his broad black gorget, and magnificent feathers; and the cedar bird, with its red-tipped wings and yellow-tipped tail and its small feathered cap; and the blue jay, that noisy show-off, in his bright light blue coat and white underclothes, screaming and chattering, nodding and bobbing and bowing, and pretending to be friendly with every singer of the grove.

As Ichabod rode slowly on his way, his eyes, always alert to signs of food abundance, delightedly surveyed the riches of the joyful autumn. He saw vast amounts of apples everywhere; some still hanging heavily on the trees; some collected into baskets and barrels for the market; others piled up in rich mounds for the cider-press. Further on, he saw large fields of Indian corn, with its golden ears peeking from their leafy covers, promising cakes and hasty-pudding; and the yellow pumpkins lying beneath them, showing their round bellies to the sun, hinting at the most luxurious pies; and then he passed the fragrant buckwheat fields smelling of the beehive, and as he saw them, gentle dreams of delicious slapjacks, well buttered, and topped with honey or treacle by the delicate, dimpled hand of Katrina Van Tassel filled his mind.

Thus, filling his mind with many sweet thoughts and "sugared suppositions," he traveled along the sides of a range of hills that overlook some of the most beautiful scenes of the mighty Hudson. The sun slowly moved its broad disk down in the west. The vast expanse of the Tappan Zee was still and glassy, except where now and then a gentle wave extended the blue shadow of the distant mountain. A few amber clouds were in the sky, motionless without a breath of air to stir them. The horizon was a fine golden tint, gradually changing into a pure apple green, and from that into the deep blue of the mid-sky. A slanting ray lingered on the wooded tops of the cliffs that overhung parts of the river, adding depth to the dark gray and purple of their rocky sides. A sloop was drifting in the distance, moving slowly with the tide, her sail limp against the mast; and as the reflection of the sky shone along the still water, it looked as if the vessel was hanging in the air.

It was toward evening that Ichabod arrived at the Van Tassel's grand home, which was filled with the most distinguished people from the surrounding area. There were old farmers, a lean and tough-looking group, wearing homespun coats and breeches, blue stockings, huge shoes, and impressive pewter buckles. Their energetic, aged wives wore tightly fitted caps, short gowns with long waists, homespun petticoats, and had scissors and pincushions, with colorful calico pockets visible on the outside. The young women, almost as old-fashioned as their mothers except for a straw hat, a bright ribbon, or maybe a white dress, showed signs of city influence. The sons wore short coats with big brass buttons and often tied their hair back, especially if they could get an eel-skin for it, since it was believed to make hair stronger.

However, Brom Bones was the center of attention, arriving on his beloved horse Daredevil, a spirited and mischievous animal that only he could control. He was known for liking unruly animals that were full of tricks, putting the rider at constant risk, because he thought a calm, well-trained horse was not suitable for a spirited young man.

I would love to spend more time describing the incredible sights that greeted Ichabod when he entered the Van Tassel's living room. Not just the attractive young women and their vibrant dresses, but the true wonder of a Dutch country tea-table during the rich autumn season. There were piles of cakes of so many kinds, only known to skilled Dutch housewives! You had the solid doughnut, the soft oly koek, and the crunchy cruller; there were sweet cakes and short cakes, ginger cakes and honey cakes, and all sorts of cakes. Then there were apple pies, peach pies, and pumpkin pies; slices of ham and smoked beef; and delicious preserved fruits like plums, peaches, pears, and quinces; not to forget broiled shad and roasted chickens; along with bowls of milk and cream, all mixed together just as I've listed them, with the teapot warmly steaming in the center--bless it! I lack the breath and time to give this feast the praise it deserves, and I'm too eager to continue with my story. Fortunately, Ichabod Crane was in no rush and thoroughly enjoyed every delicacy.

He was a grateful soul, whose heart grew as full as his stomach with good food, and whose mood lifted with eating, just as some people's do with drinking. He couldn't help but look around with wide eyes as he ate, silently laughing at the thought that he might one day own all this incredible wealth and luxury. Then, he imagined how quickly he'd leave the old schoolhouse behind; he'd dismiss Hans Van Ripper and all the stingy patrons with a snap of his fingers, and he'd boldly send any wandering teacher away who dared to call him a peer!

Old Baltus Van Tassel moved among his guests with a face beaming with happiness and good humor, as round and jolly as the harvest moon. His way of making people feel welcome was quick but meaningful, involving a handshake, a pat on the back, a hearty laugh, and a warm invitation to "go ahead, and help yourselves."

And then, the sound of music from the common room, or hall, called everyone to dance. The musician was an old gray-headed man, who had been the traveling one-man band of the neighborhood for over fifty years. His instrument was as worn and old as he was. Most of the time, he played on two or three strings, moving his head with every stroke of the bow; he would bow almost to the ground and stamp his foot whenever a new couple started dancing.

Ichabod was as proud of his dancing skills as he was of his singing. Not a single part of him was still; watching his lanky body move all over the room, you'd think St. Vitus, the patron saint of dance, was there in person. He was the envy of all the black people; who, having come from the farm and the neighborhood, of all ages and sizes, stood making a pyramid of shining black faces at every door and window, watching the scene with joy, their white eyes rolling, and showing wide grins from ear to ear. How could the teacher, so lively and happy, be anything but? The woman he loved was his dance partner, responding to his flirtatious looks with smiles; while Brom Bones, hit hard by love and jealousy, sat sulking alone in a corner.

When the dance ended, Ichabod was drawn to a group of the wiser folks, who, along with Old Van Tassel, were sitting at one end of the porch, smoking and chatting about the old days, sharing long tales about the war.

This neighborhood, at the time I'm talking about, was one of those lucky places filled with history and famous people. The British and American lines had been close by during the war. So, it had seen a lot of raiding and was crawling with refugees, cowboys, and all sorts of warriors. Enough time had passed for each storyteller to spice up his story a bit. They added some fiction and, in their fuzzy memories, made themselves the hero of every adventure.

There was the story of Doffue Martling, a big Dutchman with a blue beard. He almost captured a British frigate with an old iron cannon from a mud fortification. But his cannon exploded on the sixth shot. And there was an old gentleman, too important to name lightly. During the battle of White Plains, he was so skilled in defense that he deflected a musket-ball with his small sword. He even claimed he felt it buzz along the blade and bounce off the hilt. He would show the slightly bent sword hilt as proof. There were several others who were just as brave in battle. Each one was convinced they played a big role in ending the war successfully.

But these stories were nothing compared to the ghost stories that followed. The neighborhood is full of such legendary tales. Local tales and superstitions flourish in these secluded, long-settled places. But they get lost in the hustle and bustle of most of our towns. Also, ghosts don't get much of a chance in most of our villages. They've barely had time to settle into their graves before their friends move away. So, when they come out at night, they have no one they know to visit. This might be why we hardly hear about ghosts except in our old Dutch communities.

However, the main reason for all the supernatural stories here was definitely because of Sleepy Hollow's closeness. The air coming from that haunted place seemed to carry a contagion. It filled the area with dreams and fantasies that affected everyone. Several people from Sleepy Hollow were at Van Tassel's. As usual, they were sharing their incredible legends. There were scary stories about funeral processions, and cries and wails heard near the big tree where the unfortunate Major André was captured. Some also talked about the woman in white who haunted the dark glen at Raven Rock. She was often heard screaming on winter nights before a storm, having died there in the snow. But most of the stories were about Sleepy Hollow's favorite ghost, the Headless Horseman. Lately, he had been heard patrolling the area. It was said he tied his horse among the graves in the churchyard every night.

The secluded location of this church has always made it a popular spot for troubled spirits. It's perched on a hill, surrounded by locust trees and tall elms. Its simple, whitewashed walls stand out modestly, like a beacon of purity amidst the shadows. A gentle slope leads down to a shimmering lake, framed by tall trees. Through them, glimpses of the Hudson's blue hills can be seen. The peaceful, sunlit graveyard might make you think the dead could rest easy here. Next to the church is a large, wooded valley with a brook rushing through it, over rocks and fallen trees. Near the church, there used to be a wooden bridge over a dark part of the stream. The road and bridge were covered by dense trees, creating a gloomy atmosphere even during the day and a terrifying darkness at night. This was a favorite spot for the Headless Horseman and where he was often seen. There's a story about old Brouwer, who didn't believe in ghosts. He encountered the Horseman coming back from Sleepy Hollow and had to ride behind him. They raced through the wilderness until they reached the bridge. Then, the Horseman turned into a skeleton, threw Brouwer into the brook, and disappeared over the treetops with a thunderclap.

This tale was soon followed by an even more incredible story from Brom Bones, who scoffed at the idea of the Galloping Hessian. He claimed that one night, coming back from Sing Sing, he was caught up by the Horseman. He challenged him to a race for a bowl of punch and was winning, thanks to his horse Daredevil. But as they neared the church bridge, the Hessian disappeared in a burst of fire.

All these stories, whispered in the dark, made a deep impression on Ichabod. He shared his own stories from Cotton Mather and talked about strange events in Connecticut and scary sights in Sleepy Hollow.

The party eventually ended. The farmers took their families home in wagons, their departure echoing through the roads and hills. Some girls rode behind their boyfriends, their laughter fading into the night until everything was quiet. Ichabod stayed back to talk with the heiress, hoping he was making progress. What happened during their talk is unknown. But something must have gone wrong because he left looking defeated. Maybe the girl was just leading him on to win over his rival. Only Heaven knows. Ichabod left looking guilty, as if he'd been caught stealing, not winning a lady's heart. He didn't even enjoy the countryside views he usually loved. He went straight to the stable, woke his horse with a few rough shoves, and prepared to leave. The horse, dreaming of endless food, was rudely awakened.

It was the very witching time of night that Ichabod, heavy-hearted and crestfallen, made his way home. He traveled along the sides of the lofty hills above Tarry Town, which he had crossed so cheerfully in the afternoon. The hour was as dismal as he was. Far below him, the Tappan Zee spread its dusky and indistinct waters. Here and there, the tall mast of a sloop could be seen, quietly anchored under the land. In the dead silence of midnight, he could even hear the barking of a watchdog from the opposite shore of the Hudson. But it was so vague and faint that it only highlighted how far away he was from this faithful companion of man. Now and then, the long-drawn crowing of a cock, accidentally awakened, would sound far off from some farmhouse among the hills. But it was like a dreamy sound in his ear. No signs of life occurred near him, except occasionally the melancholy chirp of a cricket or perhaps the guttural sound of a bullfrog from a nearby marsh, as if it were sleeping uncomfortably and suddenly turned in its bed.

All the stories of ghosts and goblins he had heard in the afternoon now crowded his memory. The night grew darker; the stars seemed to sink deeper in the sky, and driving clouds occasionally hid them from his view. He had never felt so lonely and dismal. He was also approaching the very place where many of the ghost stories had taken place. In the center of the road stood an enormous tulip-tree, towering like a giant above all the other trees in the area and serving as a kind of landmark. Its limbs were gnarled and fantastic, large enough to be trunks for ordinary trees, twisting down almost to the earth and then rising again. It was associated with the tragic story of the unfortunate André, who had been captured nearby, and was universally known as Major André’s tree. The common people looked at it with a mix of respect and superstition, partly out of sympathy for the fate of its ill-starred namesake and partly because of the tales of strange sights and sad cries told about it.

As Ichabod approached this frightening tree, he began to whistle. He thought his whistle was answered, but it was just a blast sweeping sharply through the dry branches. As he got a bit closer, he thought he saw something white hanging in the middle of the tree. He stopped whistling and looked more closely, only to see that it was a spot where the tree had been struck by lightning, exposing the white wood. Suddenly, he heard a groan—his teeth chattered, and his knees knocked against the saddle. It was just the sound of one huge bough rubbing against another as they swayed in the breeze. He passed the tree safely, but new dangers awaited him.

About two hundred yards from the tree, a small brook crossed the road and flowed into a marshy, thickly-wooded glen known as Wiley’s Swamp. A few rough logs, laid side by side, served as a bridge over this stream. On the side of the road where the brook entered the wood, a group of oaks and chestnuts, tangled thick with wild grapevines, cast a deep shadow over it. To cross this bridge was the toughest challenge. It was at this exact spot that the unfortunate André was captured, and it was under the cover of those chestnuts and vines that the sturdy yeomen hid and surprised him. This place has been considered haunted ever since, and the feelings of the schoolboy who has to pass it alone after dark are filled with fear.

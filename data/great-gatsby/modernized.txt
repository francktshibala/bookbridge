                                  I

In my younger and more impressionable years, my father gave me a piece of advice that I've been pondering ever since.

"Whenever you're tempted to judge someone," he told me, "just remember that not everyone in this world has had the opportunities that you've had."

He didn’t say much more, but we’ve always had a way of communicating deeply in a subtle manner, and I understood he meant much more than he said. As a result, I tend to withhold judgment, a practice that has allowed me to understand many complex personalities but also made me the target of quite a few tedious individuals. The unconventional mind quickly recognizes and gravitates towards this trait when it appears in someone normal, leading to my unjust accusation of being a politician in college because I was privy to the hidden sorrows of wild, unknown men. Most of these confidences were uninvited—often I pretended to be asleep, distracted, or showed a feigned indifference when I sensed that someone was about to share something deeply personal; for the confessions of young men, or at least how they articulate them, tend to be derivative and full of deliberate omissions. Keeping an open mind is a practice of endless optimism. I still worry about missing out on something if I forget that, as my father elitely suggested, and I elitely echo, a sense of basic decency is distributed unevenly at birth.

And, after boasting about my tolerance, I must admit it has its limits. Behavior may be based on solid ground or shaky foundations, but beyond a certain point, I don’t care what it’s based on. When I returned from the East last fall, I wished the world could be disciplined and morally upright indefinitely; I was done with wild adventures and special insights into the human condition. Only Gatsby, the man this book is named after, was exempt from my judgment—Gatsby, who embodied everything I naturally disdain. If personality is a series of successful acts, then there was something magnificent about him, a keen sensitivity to life’s possibilities, as if he were connected to one of those delicate instruments that detect earthquakes thousands of miles away. This sensitivity had nothing to do with the weak impressionability often praised as the “creative temperament”—it was an exceptional capacity for hope, a romantic readiness I have never found in anyone else and likely never will again. No—Gatsby turned out fine in the end; it’s what preyed on Gatsby, the malign influence that followed his dreams, that temporarily made me lose interest in the fleeting sorrows and brief joys of men.

My family has been prominent and affluent in this Midwestern city for three generations. The Carraways are somewhat of a clan, and there's a tradition claiming we're descended from the Dukes of Buccleuch, but the real founder of my line was my grandfather's brother. He arrived here in 1851, hired someone to take his place in the Civil War, and started the wholesale hardware business that my father runs today.

I never met this great-uncle, but I'm told I resemble him—especially according to the rather stern painting in my father's office. I graduated from New Haven in 1915, a quarter-century after my father, and soon after, I joined the delayed European migration known as World War I. I found the experience exhilarating to the point that I returned home feeling restless. The Midwest, once the heart of my world, now felt like the fringe of civilization—so I decided to head East and get into the bond business. It seemed everyone I knew was in it, so I figured it could support one more bachelor. My aunts and uncles deliberated over my decision as if choosing a prep school for me, and with serious, uncertain expressions, they finally said, "Why—yes," Father agreed to fund me for a year, and after several delays, I moved East for good, I thought, in the spring of 1922.

The sensible choice was to find a place in the city, but it was a warm season, and I had just left a region of expansive lawns and welcoming trees. So, when a colleague suggested we rent a house together in a commuter town, it seemed like an excellent idea. He found the house, a weathered cardboard bungalow at eighty dollars a month, but at the last minute, his company transferred him to Washington, and I ended up moving to the countryside by myself. I had a dog—at least for a few days until he ran away—and an old Dodge, along with a Finnish woman who took care of the house, cooking breakfast and murmuring Finnish proverbs to herself by the electric stove.

It felt isolating for a day or so until one morning, a man, who had arrived more recently than I, stopped me on the road.

“How do you get to West Egg village?” he asked, looking lost.

I directed him. And as I continued on my way, I no longer felt alone. I was a guide, a pathfinder, an original settainer. He had casually granted me the freedom of the neighborhood.

And so, with the sunshine and the great bursts of leaves growing on the trees, just like scenes rush by in fast movies, I felt that familiar conviction that life was starting anew with the summer.

There was so much to read, for one thing, and so much vibrant health to be absorbed from the fresh, invigorating air. I bought a dozen books on banking, credit, and investment securities, and they stood on my shelf in red and gold, gleaming like freshly minted money, promising to reveal the shining secrets known only to legends like Midas, Morgan, and Maecenas. And I had every intention of reading many other books as well. I was quite the literary type in college—one year, I even wrote a series of very solemn and obvious editorials for the Yale News—and now, I was determined to reintegrate all such pursuits into my life and become once again that most limited of all specialists, the "well-rounded man." This isn't just a witty remark—after all, life is much more successfully understood from a single viewpoint.

It happened by chance that I rented a house in one of the most peculiar communities in North America. It was located on that narrow, lively island which stretches directly east of New York—and where, among other natural wonders, there are two unusual land formations. Twenty miles from the city, a pair of enormous eggs, identical in contour and separated only by a narrow bay, project into the most domesticated body of salt water in the Western hemisphere, the vast, calm barnyard of Long Island Sound. They are not perfect ovals—like the egg in the Columbus story, they are both flattened at the contact end—but their physical resemblance must be a constant source of amazement to the gulls flying overhead. To those of us on the ground, a more fascinating observation is their complete difference in every detail except shape and size.

I lived in West Egg, the less trendy of the two, though calling it "less fashionable" barely scratches the surface of the strange and somewhat ominous differences between them. My house was right at the edge, a mere fifty yards from the Sound, wedged between two massive estates that rented for twelve or fifteen thousand a season. The one to my right was a monumental structure by any measure—it was an exact replica of a certain Hôtel de Ville in Normandy, with a tower on one side, looking brand new under a sparse covering of raw ivy, and a marble swimming pool, surrounded by more than forty acres of lawn and garden. It was Gatsby’s mansion. Or, since I didn’t know Mr. Gatsby at the time, it was a mansion owned by a gentleman of that name. My own place was an eyesore, but a minor one, and it went unnoticed, so I enjoyed a view of the water, a partial view of my neighbor’s lawn, and the comforting closeness of millionaires—all for eighty dollars a month.

Across the bay, the white mansions of the chic East Egg sparkled along the waterfront, and the story of that summer really starts on the evening I drove over there to have dinner with the Tom Buchanans. Daisy was my second cousin once removed, and I’d known Tom in college. And just after World War I, I spent two days with them in Chicago.

Her husband, among various physical feats, had been one of the most formidable football players ever at New Haven—a national figure in a way, one of those men who achieve such remarkable, focused excellence at twenty-one that everything afterward seems like a letdown. His family was incredibly wealthy—even in college, his lavish spending was criticized—but now he had moved East in a way that was astonishing: for example, he had brought a string of polo ponies from Lake Forest. It was hard to believe that someone from my own generation could afford to do that.

Why they moved to the East, I can't say. They had spent a year in France without any clear purpose, then aimlessly moved from one place to another, drawn to locales where polo was played and wealth congregated. Daisy claimed over the phone that this relocation was permanent, but I had my doubts—I couldn't see into Daisy's heart, but I sensed that Tom would perpetually wander, nostalgically searching for the excitement of a bygone football game he could never relive.

Thus, on a warm, breezy evening, I found myself driving to East Egg to visit two old friends whom I barely knew. Their residence was even more grand than I had anticipated, a cheerful red-and-white Georgian Colonial mansion with a view of the bay. The lawn stretched from the beach to the front door for a quarter of a mile, leaping over sundials and brick paths, through vibrant gardens—eventually, as if propelled by its own momentum, climbing up the house's sides in bright vines. The front was marked by a row of French windows, now radiating with reflected gold, wide open to the warm, breezy afternoon, and there stood Tom Buchanan in riding attire, legs apart on the front porch.

He had changed since his New Haven days. Now, he was a robust, straw-haired man of thirty, with a somewhat harsh mouth and a disdainful demeanor. Two bright, arrogant eyes dominated his face, giving him the look of someone always aggressively leaning forward. Not even the dandyish flair of his riding outfit could conceal the immense power of his physique—he seemed to stretch those shiny boots to their limits, and the movement of his shoulder under his slim coat revealed a mass of muscle shifting beneath. His was a physique built for immense force—a merciless physique.

His speaking voice, a gruff husky tenor, added to the impression of fractiousness he conveyed. There was a touch of paternal contempt in it, even toward people he liked—and there were men at New Haven who had despised him deeply.

“Now, don’t think my opinion on these matters is final,” he seemed to say, “just because I’m stronger and more of a man than you are.” We were in the same senior society, and while we were never close, I always had the impression that he approved of me and wanted me to like him with some harsh, defiant wistfulness of his own.

We talked for a few minutes on the sunny porch.

"I've got a nice place here," he said, his eyes darting around restlessly.

Turning me around by one arm, he moved a broad flat hand along the front vista, encompassing in its sweep a sunken Italian garden, a half acre of deep, pungent roses, and a snub-nosed motorboat that bobbed on the tide offshore.

"It belonged to Demaine, the oil man." He turned me around again, politely and abruptly. "We'll go inside."

We walked through a tall hallway into a bright, rosy-colored space, delicately connected to the house by French windows at both ends. The windows were open, shining white against the vibrant grass outside that seemed to extend a bit into the house. A breeze flowed through the room, sending curtains fluttering in at one end and out the other like pale flags, twisting them up toward the frosted wedding-cake of the ceiling, before rippling over the wine-colored rug, casting a shadow on it as the wind does on the sea.

The only completely still object in the room was an enormous couch on which two young women were perched as though on an anchored balloon. They were both dressed in white, their dresses rippling and fluttering as if they had just been blown back in after a brief flight around the house. I must have stood there for a few moments, listening to the whip and snap of the curtains and the groan of a picture on the wall. Then there was a boom as Tom Buchanan closed the rear windows, and the trapped wind faded away around the room, causing the curtains, the rugs, and the two young women to gently settle to the floor.

The younger of the two was unknown to me. She lay stretched out at her end of the couch, completely still, with her chin raised slightly, as if she were balancing something on it that was quite likely to fall. If she noticed me from the corner of her eye, she gave no sign of it—indeed, I was almost surprised into murmuring an apology for having disturbed her by entering.

The other girl, Daisy, made an attempt to rise—she leaned slightly forward with a conscientious expression—then she laughed, an absurd, charming little laugh, and I laughed too and came forward into the room.

“I’m p-paralyzed with happiness.”

She laughed again, as if she said something very witty, and held my hand for a moment, looking up into my face, promising that there was no one in the world she so much wanted to see. That was a way she had. She hinted in a murmur that the surname of the balancing girl was Baker. (I’ve heard it said that Daisy’s murmur was only to make people lean toward her; an irrelevant criticism that made it no less charming.)

At any rate, Miss Baker's lips fluttered, she nodded at me almost imperceptibly, and then quickly tipped her head back again—the object she was balancing had obviously tottered a little and given her something of a fright. Again, a sort of apology rose to my lips. Almost any display of complete self-sufficiency draws a stunned admiration from me.

I looked back at my cousin, who began to ask me questions in her low, thrilling voice. It was the kind of voice that the ear follows up and down, as if each speech is an arrangement of notes that will never be played again. Her face was sad and lovely with bright things in it, bright eyes and a bright passionate mouth, but there was an excitement in her voice that men who had cared for her found difficult to forget: a singing compulsion, a whispered "Listen," a promise that she had done fun, exciting things just a while ago and that there were fun, exciting things hovering in the next hour.

I told her how I had stopped off in Chicago for a day on my way East, and how a dozen people had sent their love through me.

"Do they miss me?" she exclaimed with ecstasy.

"The whole town is in despair. Every car has the left rear wheel painted black as a symbol of mourning, and there's a constant wail all night along the north shore."

"How wonderful! Let's go back, Tom. Tomorrow!" Then she added, seemingly out of nowhere: "You really should see the baby."

"I'd love to."

"She's asleep. She's three years old. Haven't you ever seen her?"

"Never."

"Well, you really should see her. She's—"

Tom Buchanan, who had been pacing anxiously around the room, stopped and placed his hand on my shoulder.

"What are you up to, Nick?"

"I work in finance."

"Which firm?"

I told him.

"Never heard of them," he said with finality.

This irritated me.

"You will," I replied curtly. "You will if you stick around the East."

"Oh, I'll stay in the East, don't you worry," he said, glancing at Daisy and then back at me, as if he were on the lookout for something more. "I'd be a complete idiot to live anywhere else."

At this point, Miss Baker exclaimed, "Absolutely!" with such suddenness that I jumped—it was the first word she had spoken since I entered the room. It evidently surprised her as much as it did me, for she yawned and with a series of quick, skillful movements, stood up into the room.

"I'm stiff," she complained, "I've been lying on that sofa for as long as I can remember."

"Don't look at me," Daisy retorted, "I've been trying to get you to New York all afternoon."

"No, thanks," said Miss Baker to the four cocktails just brought in from the kitchen. "I'm absolutely in training."

Her host looked at her incredulously.

"You are!" He finished his drink as if it were just a tiny sip left at the bottom of his glass. "How you manage to accomplish anything is a mystery to me."

I glanced at Miss Baker, curious about what exactly she "accomplished." I found myself enjoying the view. She was slender, with a modest figure, carrying herself with an upright posture that she emphasized by leaning back at the shoulders like a young cadet. Her gray eyes, faded slightly from the sun, met mine with a polite, mutual interest from a face that was attractive yet marked by a subtle dissatisfaction. It struck me then that I had seen her, or at least a photo of her, somewhere before.

"You live in West Egg," she said with a hint of disdain. "I know someone there."

"I don't know a single—"

"You must know Gatsby."

"Gatsby?" Daisy asked, puzzled. "What Gatsby?"

Before I could reply that he was my neighbor, dinner was announced; wedging his tense arm imperatively under mine, Tom Buchanan compelled me from the room as though he were moving a checker to another square.

Slenderly, languidly, their hands set lightly on their hips, the two young women preceded us out onto a rosy-colored porch, open toward the sunset, where four candles flickered on the table in the diminished wind.

“Why candles?” objected Daisy, frowning. She snuffed them out with her fingers. “In two weeks it’ll be the longest day of the year.” She looked at us all radiantly. “Do you always watch for the longest day of the year and then miss it? I always watch for the longest day of the year and then miss it.”

"We should plan something," yawned Miss Baker, settling into her chair as though she were slipping into bed.

"Okay," said Daisy. "What should we plan?" She looked at me, clueless: "What do people plan?"

Before I could reply, her eyes locked with an awed expression on her little finger.

"Look!" she complained; "I hurt it."

We all looked—the knuckle was black and blue.

"You did it, Tom," she said accusingly. "I know you didn't mean to, but you did do it. That's what I get for marrying a brute of a man, a great, big, hulking physical specimen of a—"

“I hate that word ‘hulking,’” objected Tom irritably, “even as a joke.”

“Hulking,” Daisy persisted.

Sometimes she and Miss Baker would speak simultaneously, subtly and with a playful inconsequence that never quite became idle talk. It was as cool as their white dresses and their detached gazes, devoid of any desire. They were present, accepting Tom and me, making only a courteous effort to amuse or be amused. They understood that soon dinner would end, and shortly after, the evening would also conclude and be casually dismissed. This was starkly different from the West, where an evening was rushed from one stage to the next, either in a continually thwarted anticipation or in sheer anxiety about the moment itself.

"You make me feel uncivilized, Daisy," I admitted over my second glass of corky but rather impressive red wine. "Can't you talk about crops or something?"

I didn't mean anything specific by this comment, but it was picked up in an unexpected manner.

"Civilization's falling apart," Tom erupted violently. "I've become a terrible pessimist about things. Have you read The Rise of the Coloured Empires by this man Goddard?"

"Why, no," I answered, rather surprised by his tone.

"Well, it's a great book, and everyone should read it. The idea is if we're not careful, the white race will be—will be completely overwhelmed. It's all based on science; it's been proven."

"Tom's diving into some serious topics," said Daisy, with an expression of unreflective sadness. "He reads complex books filled with big words. What was that word we—"

"Well, these books are all about science," insisted Tom, glancing at her impatiently. "This author has figured out everything. It's up to us, who are the dominant group, to be vigilant or these other groups will take over."

"We've got to keep them in check," whispered Daisy, winking ferociously toward the blazing sun.

"You should consider moving to California—" began Miss Baker, but Tom interrupted her by shifting heavily in his chair.

"This idea is that we're Nordics. I am, and you are, and you are," After a brief pause, he included Daisy with a subtle nod, and she winked at me again. "—And we've produced all the things that make up civilization—oh, science and art, and all that. Do you see?"

There was something pathetic in his focus, as if his self-satisfaction, more intense than before, was no longer enough for him. When, almost immediately, the phone rang inside and the butler left the porch, Daisy seized the brief interruption and leaned towards me.

"I'll tell you a family secret," she whispered excitedly. "It's about the butler's nose. Do you want to hear about the butler's nose?"

"That's why I came over tonight."

"Well, he wasn't always a butler; he used to polish silver for some folks in New York who had enough silverware for two hundred guests. He polished from morning till night, until it finally started to affect his nose—"

"Things went from bad to worse," suggested Miss Baker.

“Yes. Things went from bad to worse, until finally he had to give up his position.”

For a moment, the last sunshine fell with romantic affection upon her glowing face; her voice compelled me forward breathlessly as I listened—then the glow faded, each light deserting her with lingering regret, like children leaving a pleasant street at dusk.

The butler came back and murmured something close to Tom’s ear, whereupon Tom frowned, pushed back his chair, and without a word went inside. As if his absence quickened something within her, Daisy leaned forward again, her voice glowing and singing.

"I love to see you at my table, Nick. You remind me of a—of a rose, an absolute rose. Doesn’t he?" She turned to Miss Baker for confirmation: "An absolute rose?"

This was untrue. I am not even faintly like a rose. She was only improvising, but a stirring warmth flowed from her, as if her heart was trying to reach out to you, hidden within one of those breathless, thrilling words. Then suddenly, she threw her napkin on the table and excused herself, heading inside the house.

Miss Baker and I exchanged a brief look, intentionally empty of meaning. I was about to speak when she sat up alertly and said "Sh!" in a warning tone. A subdued, impassioned murmur was audible from the next room, and Miss Baker leaned forward without embarrassment, trying to listen. The murmur hovered on the edge of making sense, dipped down, rose excitedly, and then stopped altogether.

"This Mr. Gatsby you mentioned is my neighbor—" I began.

"Don't talk. I want to hear what happens."

"Is something happening?" I asked innocently.

"You mean you don't know?" said Miss Baker, genuinely surprised. "I thought everyone knew."

"I don't."

"Why—" she said hesitantly. "Tom's seeing someone else in New York."

"Got a girlfriend?" I repeated, confused.

Miss Baker nodded.

"She could at least have the decency not to call him during dinner. Don't you think?"

Almost before I had grasped her meaning, there was the flutter of a dress and the crunch of leather boots, and Tom and Daisy were back at the table.

"It couldn't be helped!" cried Daisy with tense excitement.

She sat down, glanced searchingly at Miss Baker and then at me, and continued: "I looked outside for a minute, and it's very romantic out there. There's a bird on the lawn that I think must be a nightingale that came over on the Cunard or White Star Line. He's singing away—" Her voice sang: "It's romantic, isn't it, Tom?"

"Very romantic," he said, and then, looking miserable, he turned to me: "If it's still light after dinner, I want to take you down to the stables."

The phone rang inside, startlingly, and as Daisy decisively shook her head at Tom, the topic of the stables, in fact, all topics, disappeared into thin air. Among the scattered pieces of the last five minutes at the table, I remember the candles being lit again, pointlessly, and I was aware of my desire to look directly at everyone, yet to avoid meeting anyone's gaze. I couldn't guess what Daisy and Tom were thinking, but I doubt if even Miss Baker, who seemed to have adopted a certain resilient skepticism, could completely dismiss the piercing, metallic urgency of our unexpected fifth guest from her mind. To some, the situation might have seemed intriguing—my own impulse was to immediately call the police.

Needless to say, the horses were not brought up again. Tom and Miss Baker, with several feet of twilight between them, walked back into the library, as though attending a vigil beside a very real presence, while, trying to appear pleasantly interested and slightly hard of hearing, I followed Daisy through a series of connecting verandas to the front porch. In its deep shadow, we sat down side by side on a wicker settee.

Daisy cupped her face in her hands as if to cherish its beautiful contours, and her gaze slowly drifted into the soft twilight. I could tell she was overwhelmed by intense feelings, so I tried to calm her by asking some gentle questions about her daughter.

"We're not very close, are we, Nick?" she suddenly remarked. "Even though we're cousins. You didn't come to my wedding."

"I hadn't returned from the war yet."

"That's true." She hesitated. "Well, I've had a really tough time, Nick, and I'm pretty skeptical about everything."

Clearly, she had her reasons. I waited, but she didn't elaborate, and after a moment, I awkwardly circled back to the topic of her daughter.

"I guess she talks, and—eats, and all that."

“Oh, yes.” She looked at me absently. “Listen, Nick; let me tell you what I said when she was born. Would you like to hear?”

“Very much.”

“It’ll show you how I’ve gotten to feel about—things. Well, she was less than an hour old and Tom was God knows where. I woke up out of the anesthesia with an utterly abandoned feeling, and asked the nurse right away if it was a boy or a girl. She told me it was a girl, and so I turned my head away and wept. ‘All right,’ I said, ‘I’m glad it’s a girl. And I hope she’ll be a fool—that’s the best thing a girl can be in this world, a beautiful little fool.’

"You see, I think everything's terrible anyhow," she continued with conviction. "Everybody thinks so—the most enlightened people. And I know. I've traveled everywhere, seen everything, and experienced it all." Her eyes darted around defiantly, much like Tom's, and she laughed with exhilarating disdain. "Sophisticated—God, I'm sophisticated!"

The moment her voice stopped, no longer capturing my attention or belief, I sensed the fundamental dishonesty in her words. It unsettled me, as if the entire evening had been some kind of ruse to extract a specific reaction from me. I waited, and sure enough, soon she gave me a smug look with her beautiful face, as though she had just proclaimed her membership in a rather exclusive secret society to which she and Tom belonged.

Inside, the crimson room was alive with light. Tom and Miss Baker were seated at opposite ends of the long couch, and she was reading aloud to him from the Saturday Evening Post—the words, soft and monotone, blended into a calming melody. The lamplight, shining on his boots and dim on the autumn-leaf yellow of her hair, danced along the paper as she turned a page with a graceful movement of her slender arms.

When we entered, she silenced us for a moment with a raised hand.

“To be continued,” she announced, tossing the magazine onto the table, “in our very next issue.”

Her body made its presence known with a restless shift of her knee, and she rose to her feet.

"Ten o'clock," she noted, as if reading the time from the ceiling. "Time for this good girl to hit the hay."

"Jordan's competing in the tournament tomorrow," Daisy explained, "over at Westchester."

"Oh—you're Jordan Baker."

I knew now why her face was familiar—its pleasing contemptuous expression had looked out at me from many glossy magazine photos of the sporting life at Asheville and Hot Springs and Palm Beach. I had heard some story about her too, a critical, unpleasant story, but what it was I had forgotten long ago.

"Good night," she said softly. "Wake me at eight, won't you."

"If you'll get up."

"I will. Good night, Mr. Carraway. See you soon."

"Of course you will," confirmed Daisy. "In fact, I think I'll play matchmaker. Come over often, Nick, and I'll sort of—oh—set you up. You know—accidentally trap you in linen closets and push you out to sea in a boat, and all that sort of thing—"

"Good night," called Miss Baker from the stairs. "I haven't heard a word."

"She's a nice girl," said Tom after a moment. "They shouldn't let her travel around the country like this."

"Who shouldn't?" inquired Daisy coldly.

"Her family."

"Her family consists of one aunt who's practically ancient. Besides, Nick's going to take care of her, right, Nick? She'll be spending plenty of weekends out here this summer. I believe the home environment will be very beneficial for her."

Daisy and Tom exchanged a silent glance.

"Is she from New York?" I asked quickly.

"From Louisville. We spent our carefree youth there together. Our beautiful, innocent—"

"Did you give Nick a little heart-to-heart talk on the veranda?" demanded Tom suddenly.

“Did I?” She looked at me. “I can’t seem to remember, but I think we talked about the Nordic race. Yes, I’m sure we did. It sort of crept up on us and first thing you know—”

“Don’t believe everything you hear, Nick,” he advised me.

I said lightly that I had heard nothing at all, and a few minutes later I got up to go home. They came to the door with me and stood side by side in a cheerful square of light. As I started my car, Daisy peremptorily called: “Wait!

"I forgot to ask you something, and it's important. We heard you were engaged to a girl out West."

"That's right," Tom agreed warmly. "We heard that you were engaged."

"It's false. I'm too poor."

“But we heard it,” insisted Daisy, surprising me by opening up again in a flower-like way. “We heard it from three people, so it must be true.”

Of course, I knew what they were referring to, but I wasn’t even vaguely engaged. The fact that gossip had spread the news was one of the reasons I had come East. You can’t stop hanging out with an old friend because of rumors, and on the other hand, I had no intention of being rumored into marriage.

Their interest rather touched me and made them seem less distantly wealthy—nevertheless, I was confused and a little disgusted as I drove away. It seemed to me that the thing for Daisy to do was to rush out of the house, child in arms—but apparently, she had no such intentions. As for Tom, the fact that he “had some woman in New York” was really less surprising than that he had been depressed by a book. Something was making him nibble at the edge of stale ideas as if his robust physical egotism no longer nourished his peremptory heart.

Already it was deep summer on roadhouse roofs and in front of roadside garages, where new red gas pumps sat out in pools of light, and when I reached my estate at West Egg I parked the car under its shed and sat for a while on an abandoned grass roller in the yard. The wind had blown off, leaving a loud, bright night, with wings beating in the trees and a persistent organ sound as the full bellows of the earth blew the frogs full of life. The silhouette of a moving cat wavered across the moonlight, and, turning my head to watch it, I saw that I was not alone—fifty feet away a figure had emerged from the shadow of my neighbor’s mansion and was standing with his hands in his pockets regarding the silver pepper of the stars. Something in his leisurely movements and the secure position of his feet upon the lawn suggested that it was Mr. Gatsby himself, come out to determine what share was his of our local heavens.

I decided to call to him. Miss Baker had mentioned him at dinner, and that would do for an introduction. But I didn’t call to him, for he gave a sudden intimation that he was content to be alone—he stretched out his arms toward the dark water in a curious way, and, far as I was from him, I could have sworn he was trembling. Involuntarily I glanced seaward—and distinguished nothing except a single green light, minute and far away, that might have been the end of a dock. When I looked once more for Gatsby he had vanished, and I was alone again in the unquiet darkness.

About halfway between West Egg and New York, the road hastily merges with the railroad and runs alongside it for a quarter of a mile, as if to distance itself from a certain desolate stretch of land. This is the valley of ashes—a surreal farm where ashes grow like wheat into ridges and hills and grotesque gardens; where ashes take the shapes of houses and chimneys and rising smoke and, finally, with a transcendent effort, of ash-grey men, who move dimly and already crumbling through the powdery air. Occasionally, a line of grey cars inches along an invisible track, emits a ghastly creak, and comes to a halt, and immediately the ash-grey men swarm up with leaden spades and stir up an impenetrable cloud, which hides their obscure operations from your view.

But above the grey land and the spasms of bleak dust that drift endlessly over it, you notice, after a moment, the eyes of Doctor T. J. Eckleburg. The eyes of Doctor T. J. Eckleburg are blue and gigantic—their retinas are one yard high. They look out of no face but, instead, from a pair of enormous yellow spectacles that pass over a nonexistent nose. Evidently, some whimsical eye doctor placed them there to boost his practice in the borough of Queens, and then either sank into eternal blindness himself or forgot about them and moved away. But his eyes, dimmed slightly by many paintless days under sun and rain, watch over the solemn dumping ground.

The valley of ashes is bordered on one side by a small foul river, and when the drawbridge is raised to let barges through, the passengers on waiting trains can gaze at the dismal scene for as long as half an hour. There is always a stop there of at least a minute, and it was because of this that I first met Tom Buchanan’s mistress.

The fact that he had one was insisted upon wherever he was known. His acquaintances resented the fact that he showed up in popular cafés with her and, leaving her at a table, wandered around, chatting with whoever he knew. Though I was curious to see her, I had no desire to meet her—but I did. I went up to New York with Tom on the train one afternoon, and when we stopped by the ash-heaps he jumped to his feet and, grabbing my elbow, literally forced me from the car.

“We’re getting off,” he insisted. “I want you to meet my girl.”

I think he’d had quite a bit to drink at lunch, and his determination to have me join bordered on aggression. The arrogant assumption was that on Sunday afternoon I had nothing better to do.

I followed him over a low whitewashed railroad fence, and we walked back a hundred yards along the road under Doctor Eckleburg’s persistent stare. The only building in sight was a small block of yellow brick sitting on the edge of the wasteland, a sort of compact Main Street serving it, and adjacent to absolutely nothing. One of the three shops it contained was for rent and another was an all-night diner, approached by a trail of ashes; the third was a garage—Repairs. George B. Wilson. Cars bought and sold.—and I followed Tom inside.

The interior was unprosperous and bare; the only car visible was the dust-covered wreck of a Ford which crouched in a dim corner. It had occurred to me that this shadow of a garage must be a front, and that lavish and romantic apartments were hidden upstairs, when the owner himself appeared in the door of an office, wiping his hands on a rag. He was a blond, spiritless man, anemic, and faintly handsome. When he saw us, a damp gleam of hope sprang into his light blue eyes.

“Hello, Wilson, old man,” said Tom, slapping him jovially on the shoulder. “How’s business?”

"I can't complain," answered Wilson, his tone lacking conviction. "When are you going to sell me that car?"

"Next week; my guy is on it as we speak."

"Works pretty slow, doesn't he?"

“No, he doesn’t,” said Tom coldly. “And if you feel that way about it, maybe I should just sell it somewhere else.”

“I didn’t mean it like that,” Wilson hurried to explain. “I just meant—”

His voice trailed off, and Tom looked around the garage with impatience. Then, I heard footsteps on the stairs, and soon the substantial figure of a woman obscured the light from the office door. She was in her mid-thirties, slightly plump, but she carried her weight with a sensuous grace that some women possess. Her face, above a dark blue spotted crepe-de-chine dress, held no traditional beauty, but there was an undeniable vitality to her, as if her body was charged with a constant, smoldering energy. She smiled slowly and, walking through her husband as though he were invisible, shook hands with Tom, meeting his gaze directly. Then, she licked her lips and, without turning to face her husband, spoke to him in a soft, rough voice:

"Grab some chairs, will you? So people can sit down."

"Oh, sure," Wilson agreed quickly, heading toward the small office, blending right in with the gray color of the walls. A white ashen dust covered his dark suit and his light hair, just as it covered everything around—except his wife, who moved closer to Tom.

"I need to see you," Tom said seriously. "Catch the next train."

"All right."

"I'll meet you by the magazine rack on the lower level."

She nodded and stepped away from him just as George Wilson came out with two chairs from his office door.

We waited for her down the road and out of sight. It was a few days before the Fourth of July, and a thin, scruffy Italian child was lining up fireworks along the railroad track.

"Terrible place, isn't it," said Tom, sharing a look of disapproval with Doctor Eckleburg.

"Awful."

"It does her good to get away."

"Doesn't her husband object?"

"Wilson? He thinks she goes to see her sister in New York. He's so clueless he doesn't even know he's alive."

So Tom Buchanan, his girl, and I went up together to New York—or not quite together, for Mrs. Wilson sat discreetly in another car. Tom deferred that much to the sensibilities of those East Eggers who might be on the train.

She had changed her dress to a brown figured muslin, which stretched tight over her rather wide hips as Tom helped her to the platform in New York. At the newsstand, she bought a copy of Town Tattle and a movie magazine, and in the station drugstore, some cold cream and a small flask of perfume. Upstairs, in the solemn echoing drive, she let four taxis drive away before she selected a new one, lavender-colored with grey upholstery, and in this we slid out from the mass of the station into the glowing sunshine. But immediately she turned sharply from the window and, leaning forward, tapped on the front glass.

“I want to get one of those dogs,” she said earnestly. “I want to get one for the apartment. They’re nice to have—a dog.”

We reversed toward a grey-haired man who strikingly resembled John D. Rockefeller. In a basket hanging from his neck were a dozen very young puppies of an unclear breed.

“What kind are they?” Mrs. Wilson asked excitedly, as he approached the car window.

“All kinds. What kind do you want, lady?”

"I'd like to get one of those police dogs; I don't suppose you have that kind?"

The man peered doubtfully into the basket, plunged in his hand, and drew one up, wriggling, by the back of the neck.

"That's no police dog," said Tom.

"No, it's not exactly a police dog," said the man, his voice tinged with disappointment. "It's more of an Airedale." He ran his hand over the dog's coarse, brown fur. "Look at that coat. Impressive, right? That's a dog that won't easily get sick on you."

"I think it's adorable," said Mrs. Wilson, her enthusiasm evident. "How much is it?"

"That dog?" He gazed at it with admiration. "That dog will cost you ten dollars."

The Airedale—clearly, there was an Airedale involved in this somehow, even though its paws were shockingly white—changed owners and settled comfortably into Mrs. Wilson's lap, where she caressed its weather-resistant fur with delight.

"Is it a boy or a girl?" she inquired gently.

"That dog? That dog's a boy."

"It's a pain," said Tom decisively. "Here's your money. Go and buy ten more dogs with it."

We drove over to Fifth Avenue, warm and soft, almost pastoral, on the summer Sunday afternoon. I wouldn't have been surprised to see a great flock of white sheep turn the corner.

"Hold on," I said, "I have to leave you here."

"No, you don't," Tom cut in swiftly. "Myrtle will be upset if you don't come up to the apartment. Won't you, Myrtle?"

"Come on," she encouraged. "I'll call my sister Catherine. She's considered very beautiful by those who should know."

"Well, I'd like to, but—"

We continued our journey, veering back through the park toward the West Hundreds. At 158th Street, the cab halted in front of a segment of a long, white row of apartment buildings. Casting a majestic look of return around the neighborhood, Mrs. Wilson, with her dog and shopping in tow, entered with an air of dignity.

"I'm going to invite the McKees over," she declared as we ascended in the elevator. "And, of course, I need to call my sister as well."

The apartment was on the top floor—a compact living room, a small dining room, a tiny bedroom, and a bathroom. The living room was crammed with a set of tapestry-covered furniture that was far too large, making it a constant challenge to navigate without tripping over the elaborate scenes of ladies frolicking in the gardens of Versailles. The sole piece of art was an excessively enlarged photo, which at first glance seemed to depict a chicken perched on an indistinct rock. From a distance, though, the chicken transformed into a bonnet, and the cheerful face of a robust elderly woman looked down upon the room. Scattered on the table were several outdated issues of Town Tattle, alongside a copy of Simon Called Peter and a few of Broadway's minor gossip magazines. Mrs. Wilson's initial concern was for the dog. A hesitant elevator operator fetched a box filled with straw and some milk, to which he also added, without being asked, a can of large, tough dog biscuits—one of which slowly disintegrated in the milk dish throughout the afternoon. Meanwhile, Tom retrieved a bottle of whiskey from a locked cabinet.

I've only been drunk twice in my life, and the second time was that afternoon; so everything that happened is a bit blurry, even though the apartment was bathed in cheerful sunlight until after eight o'clock. Sitting on Tom's lap, Mrs. Wilson called several people on the phone; then we ran out of cigarettes, and I went to buy some at the drugstore on the corner. When I returned, they had both vanished, so I discreetly sat down in the living room and read a chapter of Simon Called Peter—either it was awful, or the whiskey was playing tricks on me because it made no sense.

Just as Tom and Myrtle (we started calling each other by our first names after the first drink) came back, guests began to arrive at the apartment.

Catherine, Myrtle's sister, was a worldly, slender woman around thirty, with a bold, choppy bob of red hair and a complexion so powdered it was almost milky white. Her eyebrows had been plucked and redrawn at a more daring angle, but nature's attempt to revert to their original state gave her face a fuzzy look. She moved with a constant clinking sound as countless ceramic bracelets jangled on her arms. She entered with such a sense of ownership and surveyed the furniture so possessively that I wondered if she lived there. But when I asked, she laughed heartily, echoed my question, and told me she lived with a girlfriend at a hotel.

Mr. McKee was a pale, effeminate man from the apartment below. He had just shaved, evidenced by a white spot of shaving cream on his cheekbone, and he greeted everyone in the room with utmost respect. He mentioned he was in the "artistic game," and I later understood he was a photographer, responsible for the vague enlargement of Mrs. Wilson's mother that loomed like a ghost on the wall. His wife was shrill, lethargic, attractive, and dreadful. She boasted to me that her husband had taken her photo a hundred and twenty-seven times since their marriage.

Mrs. Wilson had changed her outfit a while ago and was now wearing an elaborate afternoon dress of cream-colored chiffon, which rustled continuously as she moved around the room. The dress seemed to transform her personality as well. The raw energy she radiated at the garage was now replaced with an imposing haughtiness. Her laughter, her movements, and her claims became increasingly exaggerated by the minute, and as she became more animated, the room seemed to shrink around her, until she appeared to be spinning on a noisy, groaning axis through the smoky air.

"My dear," she exclaimed to her sister in a high, affected yell, "most of these guys will scam you every chance they get. All they care about is money. I had a woman come over last week to check my feet, and when she handed me the bill, you would have thought she performed an appendectomy on me."

"What was the name of the woman?" asked Mrs. McKee.

"Mrs. Eberhardt. She goes around looking at people's feet in their own homes."

"I like your dress," remarked Mrs. McKee, "I think it's adorable."

Mrs. Wilson dismissed the compliment with a disdainful raise of her eyebrow.

"It's just some old thing," she said. "I just throw it on sometimes when I don't care how I look."

"But it looks amazing on you, if you catch my drift," continued Mrs. McKee. "If Chester could just capture you in that pose, I think he could really create something special."

We all watched in silence as Mrs. Wilson brushed a strand of hair from her eyes and returned our gaze with a dazzling smile. Mr. McKee observed her closely, tilting his head, then waved his hand slowly in front of his face.

"I should adjust the lighting," he said after a moment. "I want to highlight the contours of the face. And I'd try to capture all the hair at the back."

"I wouldn't dream of adjusting the lighting," exclaimed Mrs. McKee. "I think it's—"

Her husband said "Sh!" and we all turned our attention back to the topic at hand, at which point Tom Buchanan let out a loud yawn and stood up.

"You McKees should have something to drink," he said. "Grab some more ice and sparkling water, Myrtle, before everyone dozes off."

"I already told that guy about the ice." Myrtle raised her eyebrows in frustration at the laziness of the service staff. "These people! You have to constantly nag them."

She glanced at me and laughed without reason. Then she strutted over to the dog, kissed it passionately, and glided into the kitchen, suggesting that a team of chefs awaited her commands there.

"I've created some impressive things out on Long Island," Mr. McKee claimed confidently.

Tom stared at him, utterly uncomprehending.

"Two of them are hanging downstairs."

"Two what?" Tom demanded.

"Two paintings. One of them I've named Montauk Point—The Gulls, and the other, Montauk Point—The Sea."

Catherine, the sister, settled next to me on the couch.

"Do you also live on Long Island?" she asked.

"I reside in West Egg."

"Really? I was at a party there about a month ago. At a man named Gatsby's. Do you know him?"

"I live next door to him."

"Well, they say he's a nephew or a cousin of Kaiser Wilhelm's. That's where all his money comes from."

"Really?"

She nodded.

"I'm scared of him. I'd hate for him to have anything on me."

This intriguing news about my neighbor was cut short by Mrs. McKee suddenly pointing at Catherine:

"Chester, I think you could really make something of her," she exclaimed, but Mr. McKee just nodded indifferently and shifted his focus to Tom.

"I'd love to do more work on Long Island, if I could just get my foot in the door. All I'm asking for is a chance to prove myself."

"Ask Myrtle," said Tom, bursting into a short burst of laughter as Mrs. Wilson came in with a tray. "She'll hook you up with a letter of introduction, won't you, Myrtle?"

"Do what?" she asked, taken aback.

"You'll get McKee a letter of introduction to your husband, so he can do some studies of him." His lips moved silently for a moment as he came up with, "‘George B. Wilson at the Gas Station,' or something like that."

Catherine leaned close to me and whispered in my ear:

"Neither of them can stand the person they're married to."

"Can't they?"

"Can't stand them." She glanced at Myrtle and then at Tom. "What I'm saying is, why keep living with them if they can't stand them? If I were them, I'd get a divorce and marry each other right away."

"Doesn't she like Wilson either?"

The response to this was surprising. It came from Myrtle, who had overheard the question, and it was harsh and vulgar.

"You see," Catherine exclaimed with a victorious tone, lowering her voice once more. "It's actually his wife who's causing the separation. She's a Catholic, and they're against divorce."

Daisy wasn't Catholic, and I was somewhat taken aback by the complexity of the lie.

"When they do get married," Catherine went on, "they're planning to move out West for a bit until the situation cools down."

"It'd be more discreet to go to Europe."

"Oh, do you like Europe?" she exclaimed, surprised. "I just got back from Monte Carlo."

"Really."

"Just last year. I went over there with another girl."

"Stay long?"

"No, we just went to Monte Carlo and back. We traveled via Marseilles. We had over twelve hundred dollars when we started, but we were scammed out of it all in two days in the private rooms. Getting back was a nightmare, I can tell you. God, how I hated that town!"

The late afternoon sky bloomed in the window for a moment like the blue honey of the Mediterranean—then the sharp voice of Mrs. McKee called me back into the room.

“I almost made a mistake, too,” she declared vigorously. “I almost married a guy who’d been chasing me for years. I knew he wasn't right for me. Everyone kept telling me: ‘Lucille, that man’s not good enough for you!’ But if I hadn’t met Chester, he’d have snagged me for sure.”

“Yes, but listen,” said Myrtle Wilson, nodding her head up and down, “at least you didn’t marry him.”

"I know I didn't."

"Well, I married him," said Myrtle, with a hint of ambiguity. "And that's the difference between your situation and mine."

"Why did you, Myrtle?" demanded Catherine. "Nobody made you do it."

Myrtle reflected.

"I married him because I thought he was a gentleman," she finally said. "I thought he had some class, but he wasn't even worthy to lick my shoe."

"You were head over heels for him for a while," said Catherine.

"Crazy about him!" Myrtle exclaimed in disbelief. "Who said I was crazy about him? I was never any more crazy about him than I was about that guy over there."

She pointed suddenly at me, and everyone turned to look at me with suspicion. I tried to convey through my expression that I was not expecting any affection.

"The only time I was crazy was when I married him. I realized immediately it was a mistake. He borrowed someone's best suit for our wedding and didn't even tell me about it. Then, one day when he was out, the owner came to claim it: 'Oh, is this your suit?' I asked. 'This is the first I've heard of it.' But I handed it over and then spent the entire afternoon crying my eyes out."

"She really should leave him," Catherine continued, speaking to me. "They've been living above that garage for eleven years. And Tom's the first real romance she's ever had."

The bottle of whiskey—a second one—was now being passed around constantly by everyone there, except for Catherine, who claimed she "felt just as good sober." Tom called for the building manager and sent him out for some famous sandwiches, which were hearty enough to be a meal on their own. I wanted to leave and walk eastward toward the park through the soft twilight, but every time I tried, I got caught up in some intense, loud argument that yanked me back, as if with ropes, into my chair. Yet, high above the city, our line of yellow-lit windows must have added our bit of human mystery to the casual observer in the dimming streets, and I saw him too, looking up and wondering. I was both inside and outside, at once enchanted and repulsed by the endless diversity of life.

Myrtle moved her chair close to mine, and suddenly her warm breath carried to me the story of her first encounter with Tom.

It was on the two little seats facing each other, always the last ones left on the train. I was heading up to New York to visit my sister and spend the night. He was dressed in a formal suit and shiny leather shoes, and I couldn’t keep my eyes off him. Yet, every time he glanced my way, I had to act as though I was interested in the ad above his head. When we arrived at the station, he was right beside me, his crisp white shirt brushing against my arm. I jokingly threatened to call the police on him, but he knew I was bluffing. I was so thrilled that when I hopped into a cab with him, it barely registered that we weren’t boarding a subway train. All that ran through my mind, again and again, was ‘You can’t live forever; you can’t live forever.’

She turned to Mrs. McKee, filling the room with her forced laughter.

"My dear," she exclaimed, "I'm going to give you this dress as soon as I'm done with it. I need to buy a new one tomorrow. I have to make a list of everything I need to get. A massage and a hair styling, a new collar for the dog, one of those adorable ashtrays with a spring mechanism, and a wreath with a black silk ribbon for my mother’s grave that’ll last the whole summer. I need to write down a list so I don’t forget all the things I have to do."

It was nine o’clock—almost immediately afterward I looked at my watch and found it was ten. Mr. McKee was asleep on a chair, his fists clenched in his lap, like a photograph of a man of action. Taking out my handkerchief, I wiped from his cheek the spot of dried shaving cream that had bothered me all the afternoon.

The little dog was sitting on the table, looking with blind eyes through the smoke, and from time to time, groaning faintly. People disappeared, reappeared, made plans to go somewhere, and then lost each other, searched for each other, found each other a few feet away. Sometime toward midnight, Tom Buchanan and Mrs. Wilson stood face to face, discussing in impassioned voices whether Mrs. Wilson had any right to mention Daisy’s name.

“Daisy! Daisy! Daisy!” shouted Mrs. Wilson. “I’ll say it whenever I want to! Daisy! Dai—”

With a swift, precise movement, Tom Buchanan broke her nose with his open hand.

Then, the bathroom floor was covered in bloody towels, and women's voices were scolding, with a long, anguished cry of pain rising above the chaos. Mr. McKee woke up from his nap and began to walk towards the door in a daze. Halfway there, he turned around to stare at the scene—his wife and Catherine scolding and comforting as they navigated the cluttered room with first aid supplies, and the despairing figure on the couch, bleeding profusely, attempting to cover the tapestry scenes of Versailles with a copy of Town Tattle. Then, Mr. McKee turned back and continued out the door. Taking my hat from the chandelier, I followed him.

"Come to lunch sometime," he suggested, as we descended in the elevator, groaning.

"Where?"

"Anywhere."

"Keep your hands off the control," snapped the elevator attendant.

"I beg your pardon," said Mr. McKee with dignity, "I didn't realize I was touching it."

"All right," I agreed, "I'd be happy to."

... I was standing beside his bed, and he was sitting up between the sheets, dressed in his underwear, holding a large portfolio in his hands.

"Beauty and the Beast... Loneliness... Old Grocery Horse... Brooklyn Bridge..."

Then I found myself half asleep in the chilly lower level of Pennsylvania Station, gazing at the morning Tribune, and waiting for the four o'clock train.

There was music from my neighbor’s house through the summer nights. In his blue gardens, men and women came and went like moths among the whisperings and the champagne and the stars. At high tide in the afternoon, I watched his guests diving from the tower of his raft, or sunbathing on the hot sand of his beach while his two motorboats cut through the waters of the Sound, towing wakeboarders over cataracts of foam. On weekends, his Rolls-Royce transformed into a shuttle, ferrying parties to and from the city between nine in the morning and well past midnight, while his station wagon zipped around like a brisk yellow bug to meet all trains. And on Mondays, eight servants, including an extra gardener, worked all day with mops and scrubbing brushes and hammers and garden shears, repairing the damage from the night before.

Every Friday, five crates of oranges and lemons arrived from a fruit vendor in New York—every Monday, these same oranges and lemons left his back door in a pyramid of juiceless halves. There was a machine in the kitchen that could extract the juice of two hundred oranges in half an hour if a little button was pressed two hundred times by a butler’s thumb.

At least once every two weeks, a team of caterers arrived with several hundred feet of canvas and enough colored lights to turn Gatsby’s enormous garden into a Christmas display. On buffet tables, adorned with sparkling appetizers, spiced baked hams competed with salads of harlequin designs and pastry pigs and turkeys enchanted to a dark gold. In the main hall, a bar with a real brass rail was set up, stocked with gins and liquors and with cordials so long forgotten that most of his female guests were too young to recognize one from another.

By seven o'clock the orchestra has arrived, not just a modest five-piece group, but an entire ensemble of oboes, trombones, saxophones, violins, cornets, piccolos, and both low and high drums. The last swimmers have now returned from the beach and are getting dressed upstairs; the cars from New York are parked five deep in the driveway, and already the halls, salons, and verandas are vibrant with primary colors, hair styled in novel ways, and shawls that surpass the wildest dreams of Castile. The bar is bustling, and rounds of cocktails float through the garden outside, until the air buzzes with chatter and laughter, and fleeting innuendos and introductions that are forgotten immediately, and exuberant encounters between women who never learn each other’s names.

The lights grow brighter as the earth turns away from the sun, and now the orchestra plays music that seems to color the air yellow, and the opera of voices rises a notch. Laughter becomes more effortless by the minute, poured out generously, triggered by a single cheerful word. The groups change more rapidly, grow with new arrivals, dissolve and form in the same moment; there are already wanderers, confident girls who thread their way here and there among the more solid and stable, becoming for a brief, exhilarating moment the center of a group, and then, thrilled with their triumph, glide on through the ever-shifting sea of faces, voices, and colors under the kaleidoscopically changing lights.

Suddenly, one of these nomads, clad in shimmering opal, grabs a cocktail out of nowhere, downs it for courage, and with hands moving like she's from San Francisco, dances out alone onto the canvas platform. A brief silence falls; the orchestra leader adjusts his rhythm to accommodate her, and a burst of chatter follows as the mistaken rumor spreads that she is Gilda Gray’s understudy from the Follies. The party has officially started.

I believe that on the first night I went to Gatsby’s house, I was one of the few guests who had actually been invited. People were not invited—they just showed up. They hopped into cars that whisked them out to Long Island, and somehow they ended up at Gatsby’s door. Once there, they were introduced by someone who knew Gatsby, and from then on, they acted according to the unwritten rules of an amusement park. Sometimes they came and went without ever meeting Gatsby, drawn by the party’s allure with a naive enthusiasm that served as their entry pass.

I had actually received an invitation. A chauffeur in a robin’s-egg blue uniform crossed my lawn early that Saturday morning with a surprisingly formal note from his boss: the honor would be entirely Gatsby’s, it read, if I would attend his “little party” that night. He had seen me several times and had intended to visit me long before, but a peculiar combination of circumstances had prevented it—signed Jay Gatsby, in a regal handwriting.

Dressed in white flannels, I headed over to his place a little after seven and found myself somewhat awkwardly navigating through crowds of people I didn’t know—though here and there was a face I recognized from the commuter train. I was immediately struck by the number of young Englishmen scattered around; all well-dressed, all looking slightly famished, and all speaking in hushed, serious tones to affluent and confident Americans. I was convinced they were peddling something: stocks, insurance, or cars. They seemed painfully aware of the wealth surrounding them and were sure it was within reach if they just said the right things.

As soon as I arrived, I tried to find my host, but the few people I asked about his whereabouts looked at me with such surprise and denied knowing where he was so strongly that I retreated towards the cocktail table—the only spot in the garden where a single man could hang out without seeming lost and alone.

I was on my way to get completely drunk from sheer embarrassment when Jordan Baker emerged from the house and stood at the top of the marble steps, leaning slightly back and looking with disdainful interest down into the garden.

Welcome or not, I realized I needed to join someone before I started making friendly comments to the people walking by.

"Hello!" I shouted, moving toward her. My voice sounded unnaturally loud across the garden.

"I thought you might be here," she replied distractedly as I approached. "I remembered you lived next door to—"

She held my hand in a detached way, as a promise that she'd attend to me shortly, and listened to two girls in matching yellow dresses, who paused at the bottom of the steps.

"Hello!" they exclaimed in unison. "Too bad you didn't win."

That was about the golf tournament. She had been defeated in the finals just the week before.

"You don't recognize us," said one of the girls in yellow, "but we met you here around a month ago."

"You've changed your hair color since then," remarked Jordan, and I was taken aback, but the girls had nonchalantly moved on, and her comment seemed to be directed at the early evening moon, which, like the dinner, had probably been arranged by a catering service. With Jordan's slender golden arm linked with mine, we made our way down the steps and strolled around the garden. A tray of cocktails approached us through the dimming light, and we took seats at a table with the two girls in yellow and three men, each introduced to us as Mr. Mumble.

"Do you come to these parties often?" Jordan asked the girl next to her.

"The last one was the one where I met you," the girl replied, with a lively and confident tone. She turned to her friend: "That was the same for you, wasn't it, Lucille?"

It was for Lucille, too.

“I like to come,” Lucille said. “I never care what I do, so I always have a good time. When I was here last, I ripped my dress on a chair, and he asked me my name and address—within a week, I received a package from Croirier’s with a new evening dress in it.”

“Did you keep it?” asked Jordan.

"Absolutely, I did. I planned to wear it tonight, but it was too large in the chest and needed adjustments. It was a stunning gas blue with lavender beads. Cost me two hundred and sixty-five dollars."

"There's something odd about a guy who does something like that," the other girl said with keen interest. "He doesn't want any problems with anyone."

"Who doesn't?" I asked.

"Gatsby. Someone mentioned to me—"

The two girls and Jordan leaned in closer, sharing a secret.

"Someone mentioned to me they thought he once killed a man."

A thrill swept through us all. The three Mr. Mumbles leaned in, listening intently.

"I don't think that's quite it," Lucille countered skeptically; "It's more like he was a German spy during World War I."

One of the men nodded, affirming her guess.

“I heard that from a man who knew all about him, grew up with him in Germany,” he assured us positively.

“Oh, no,” said the first girl, “it couldn’t be that, because he served in the American army during World War I.” As our belief swung back to her, she leaned forward with enthusiasm. “You look at him sometimes when he thinks nobody’s watching him. I’ll bet he killed a man.”

She narrowed her eyes and shivered. Lucille shivered. We all turned and looked around for Gatsby. It was a testament to the romantic speculation he inspired that there were whispers about him from those who had found little that was necessary to whisper about in this world.

The first dinner—there would be another one after midnight—was now being served, and Jordan invited me to join her group, who were seated around a table on the other side of the garden. The group consisted of three married couples and Jordan’s date, a persistent college student prone to suggestive comments, clearly under the impression that Jordan would eventually succumb to his advances to some degree. Unlike the more scattered guests, this group had maintained a dignified unity, positioning themselves as the embodiment of the area's established nobility—East Egg looking down on West Egg, cautiously distancing itself from its vibrant extravagance.

“Let’s get out of here,” Jordan whispered after a somehow wasteful and mismatched half-hour; “this is way too formal for my taste.”

We stood up, and she mentioned that we were going to look for the host: I had never met him, she pointed out, and it was making me anxious. The college student gave a cynical, melancholy nod.

The bar, where we glanced first, was crowded, but Gatsby was not there. She couldn’t find him from the top of the steps, and he wasn’t on the veranda. On a chance, we tried an important-looking door and walked into a high Gothic library, paneled with carved English oak, likely transported whole from some ruin overseas.

A stout, middle-aged man, with enormous owl-eyed spectacles, was sitting somewhat drunk on the edge of a great table, staring with unsteady focus at the shelves of books. As we entered, he wheeled around excitedly and examined Jordan from head to foot.

“What do you think?” he demanded impetuously.

"About what?"

He gestured towards the bookshelves.

"About that. Actually, you don't need to bother checking. I already did. They're real."

"The books?"

He nodded.

"Totally real—they have pages and everything. I thought they'd just be some fancy cardboard. But no, they're completely real. Pages and—Here! Let me show you."

Taking our skepticism for granted, he rushed to the bookcases and returned with Volume One of the Stoddard Lectures.

“See!” he cried triumphantly. “It’s a genuine piece of printed matter. It fooled me. This guy’s a real Spielberg. It’s a triumph. What thoroughness! What realism! Knew when to stop, too—didn’t cut the pages. But what do you want? What do you expect?”

He snatched the book from me and replaced it hastily on its shelf, muttering that if one brick was removed the whole library was liable to collapse.

"Who invited you?" he demanded. "Or did you just show up? Someone invited me. Most people were invited."

Jordan looked at him attentively, with a bright cheer, but didn't reply.

"I was invited by a woman named Roosevelt," he continued. "Mrs. Claud Roosevelt. Do you know her? I met her somewhere last night. I've been drunk for about a week now, and I thought it might sober me up to sit in a library."

"Has it?"

"Just a bit, I think. I'm not sure yet. I've only been here for an hour. Did I mention the books? They're genuine. They're—"

"You've already told us."

We shook hands with him solemnly and stepped back outside.

The garden was alive with dancing; elderly men guiding young women in awkward, endless loops, stylish couples clinging to each other in a strained embrace, sticking to the shadows—and numerous single women dancing solo or momentarily freeing the band from the weight of the banjo or the drums. By midnight, the festivity had escalated. A renowned tenor had performed in Italian, and a famous alto had sung in jazz, with guests performing "stunts" throughout the garden between acts, as bursts of carefree laughter soared into the night sky. A pair of stage twins, who were actually the girls in yellow, performed a childish act in costume, and champagne was served in glasses larger than soup bowls. The moon climbed higher, casting a triangle of silver light on the Sound, quivering slightly to the metallic twang of the banjos on the lawn.

I was still with Jordan Baker. We sat at a table with a man around my age and a boisterous young woman, who burst into fits of laughter at the slightest joke. I was truly enjoying myself now. After consuming two large glasses of champagne, the scene transformed in my eyes into something meaningful, fundamental, and deep.

During a pause in the festivities, the man glanced at me and smiled.

"Your face looks familiar," he said courteously. "Were you in the First Division during World War I?"

"Why yes. I was in the Twenty-eighth Infantry."

"I served in the Sixteenth until June 1918. I knew I'd seen you somewhere before."

We chatted briefly about some damp, gray little towns in France. Clearly, he lived around here, because he mentioned that he had just bought a hydroplane and was planning to test it out in the morning.

"Want to join me, old sport? Just near the shore along the Sound."

"What time?"

"Whenever works best for you."

I was just about to ask his name when Jordan glanced around and smiled.

"Having a great time now?" she inquired.

"Much better." I turned again to my new acquaintance. "This is an unusual party for me. I haven’t even seen the host. I live over there—" I waved my hand at the invisible hedge in the distance, "and this man Gatsby sent over his chauffeur with an invitation."

For a moment he looked at me as if he failed to understand.

"I'm Gatsby," he said suddenly.

"What!" I exclaimed. "Oh, I'm sorry."

"I thought you knew, old sport. I'm afraid I'm not a very good host."

He smiled understandingly—much more than understandingly. It was one of those rare smiles with a quality of eternal reassurance in it, that you may come across four or five times in life. It faced—or seemed to face—the whole eternal world for an instant, and then concentrated on you with an irresistible bias in your favor. It understood you just as much as you wanted to be understood, believed in you as you would like to believe in yourself, and assured you that it had precisely the impression of you that, at your best, you hoped to convey. Precisely at that point it vanished—and I was looking at an elegant young man, a year or two over thirty, whose elaborate formality of speech just missed being absurd. Some time before he introduced himself, I’d got a strong impression that he was choosing his words with care.

Almost at the moment when Mr. Gatsby identified himself, a butler hurried toward him with the information that Chicago was calling him on the phone. He excused himself with a small bow that included each of us in turn.

“If you want anything just ask for it, old sport,” he urged me. “Excuse me. I will rejoin you later.”

When he had left, I immediately turned to Jordan—compelled to express my astonishment. I had anticipated Mr. Gatsby to be a flamboyant and overweight man in his middle years.

"Who is he?" I pressed. "Do you know?"

"He's just a man named Gatsby."

"Where is he from, I mean? And what does he do?"

"Now you've brought it up," she answered with a faint smile. "Well, he once told me he went to Oxford."

A vague background began to form behind him, but at her next comment, it disappeared.

"However, I don't believe it."

"Why not?"

"I don't know," she insisted, "I just don't think he went there."

Something in her tone reminded me of the other girl's "I think he killed a man," and it piqued my curiosity. I would have easily believed that Gatsby emerged from the swamps of Louisiana or from the lower East Side of New York. That made sense to me. But young men didn't—at least in my limited experience I thought they didn't—just appear out of thin air and buy a mansion on Long Island Sound.

"Anyway, he throws big parties," said Jordan, shifting the topic with a city slicker's aversion to the specific. "And I like big parties. They're so intimate. At small parties, there's no privacy."

Then came the boom of a bass drum, and the orchestra leader's voice suddenly cut through the echo of the garden.

"Ladies and gentlemen," he announced. "At Mr. Gatsby's request, we're going to perform Mr. Vladmir Tostoff's latest masterpiece, which made quite the splash at Carnegie Hall last May. If you keep up with the news, you'll remember it caused quite a stir." He grinned broadly, a hint of playful superiority in his tone, and added: "Quite the stir, indeed!" This prompted laughter from everyone.

He energetically wrapped up, "The piece is titled 'Vladmir Tostoff’s Jazz History of the World!'"

I couldn't quite grasp the essence of Mr. Tostoff’s piece, as my attention was captured by Gatsby, who stood alone on the marble steps, surveying the crowd with a look of satisfaction. His tan skin was smoothly stretched over his face, and his hair appeared impeccably groomed as if cut daily. There was nothing alarming about him. I speculated whether his abstaining from drinking set him apart from his guests, as he seemed to become more distinguished while the general merriment escalated. As the "Jazz History of the World" concluded, women were playfully leaning their heads on men's shoulders, others were teasingly falling back into men's arms or even into groups, confident they would be caught—yet no one leaned on Gatsby, no stylish haircut brushed against his shoulder, and no singing groups formed with Gatsby as a member.

"Excuse me."

Gatsby's butler had suddenly appeared beside us.

"Miss Baker?" he asked. "Excuse me, but Mr. Gatsby would like to speak to you alone."

"With me?" she exclaimed in surprise.

"Yes, madame."

She stood up slowly, her eyebrows lifting in astonishment, and followed the butler toward the house. I noticed that she wore her evening dress, and all her dresses, with the casual grace of sportswear—there was a lively spring in her step as if she had first learned to walk on golf courses during bright, fresh mornings.

I was alone and it was almost two in the morning. For some time, a mix of confusing and intriguing sounds had been coming from a long room filled with windows that overlooked the terrace. Dodging Jordan’s friend, a college student who was now deeply involved in a lively discussion with two chorus girls and who begged me to join them, I went inside.

The large room was crowded. One of the girls in yellow was playing the piano, and next to her stood a tall, red-haired young woman from a renowned chorus, singing. She had consumed a significant amount of champagne, and as she sang, she had come to the melancholy conclusion that everything was profoundly sad—she wasn't just singing; she was also crying. Whenever there was a break in the song, she filled it with gasping, broken sobs, and then resumed the lyrics in a shaky soprano voice. The tears streamed down her face—not smoothly, though, because when they met her heavily mascaraed eyelashes, they turned black and continued their journey in slow, dark streams. Someone jokingly suggested she should sing the notes on her face, which led her to throw up her hands, collapse into a chair, and fall into a deep, alcohol-induced sleep.

“She had a fight with a guy who claims he’s her husband,” a girl next to me explained.

I looked around. Most of the remaining women were now arguing with men claimed to be their husbands. Even Jordan's group, the quartet from East Egg, was torn apart by conflict. One of the men was speaking with intense curiosity to a young actress, and his wife, after trying to laugh off the situation with dignity and indifference, completely broke down and resorted to sneak attacks—at times, she would suddenly appear at his side like an angry spark, and hiss: "You promised!" into his ear.

The reluctance to leave wasn't just among the unfaithful men. The hall was currently occupied by two extremely sober men and their very upset wives. The wives were commiserating with each other in slightly elevated voices.

"Whenever he sees I'm enjoying myself, he wants to leave."

"Never heard anything so selfish in my life."

"We're always the first ones to leave."

"So are we."

"Well, we're nearly the last ones here," said one of the men, a bit embarrassed. "The band left half an hour ago."

Despite the wives' insistence that such hostility was unthinkable, the argument concluded with a brief scuffle, and both wives were carried out into the night, protesting vehemently.

As I waited for my hat in the hall, the door of the library opened, and Jordan Baker and Gatsby emerged together. He was finishing a conversation with her, but his eager demeanor quickly shifted to formality as several guests approached him to bid farewell.

Jordan's friends were calling out to her impatiently from the porch, but she stayed back for a moment to shake hands.

"I've just heard the most amazing thing," she whispered. "How long were we in there?"

"Why, about an hour."

“It was … simply amazing,” she repeated, lost in thought. “But I swore I wouldn’t tell it and here I am teasing you.” She yawned elegantly in my direction. “Please come and see me … Phone book … Under the name of Mrs. Sigourney Howard … My aunt …” She was on the move as she spoke—her brown hand gave a lively wave as she blended back into her group at the door.

Feeling somewhat embarrassed that on my first visit I had overstayed, I joined the last of Gatsby’s guests, who were gathered around him. I wanted to explain that I’d looked for him earlier in the evening and to apologize for not having recognized him outside.

“Don’t worry about it,” he urged me warmly. “Don’t give it another thought, old sport.” The familiar term was as comforting as the hand that reassuringly patted my shoulder. “And don’t forget we’re going up in the hydroplane tomorrow morning, at nine o’clock.”

Then the butler, over his shoulder:

"Philadelphia wants you on the phone, sir."

"All right, in a minute. Tell them I'll be right there... Good night."

"Good night."

"Good night." He smiled—and suddenly, it felt meaningful to have been among the last to leave, as if he had wanted it that way all along. "Good night, old sport... Good night."

But as I walked down the steps, I realized the evening was far from over. Fifty feet from the door, a dozen headlights cast light on a chaotic scene. In the ditch beside the road, perfectly upright but violently missing one wheel, sat a new coupe that had exited Gatsby's driveway not two minutes earlier. The sharp angle of a wall explained why the wheel had come off, which now drew the keen interest of half a dozen curious drivers. However, since their cars were blocking the road, a loud, jarring noise from those stuck behind had been growing for some time, contributing to the already intense confusion of the scene.

A man in a long coat had gotten out of the wrecked car and now stood in the middle of the road, looking from the car to the tire and from the tire to the onlookers with a pleasantly puzzled expression.

"See!" he exclaimed. "It went into the ditch."

The fact was infinitely astonishing to him, and I first recognized the unusual quality of wonder, and then the man—it was the recent visitor of Gatsby’s library.

"How did it happen?"

He shrugged his shoulders.

"I know absolutely nothing about mechanics," he said decisively.

"But how did it happen? Did you crash into the wall?"

"Don't ask me," said Owl Eyes, distancing himself from the entire situation. "I barely know anything about driving—practically nothing. It happened, and that's all I know."

"Well, if you're a bad driver, you really shouldn't be driving at night."

"But I wasn't even trying," he explained indignantly. "I wasn't even trying."

An awed silence fell upon the onlookers.

"Do you want to commit suicide?"

"You're lucky it was just a wheel! A terrible driver and not even trying!"

"You don't understand," explained the criminal. "I wasn't driving. There's another man in the car."

The shock that followed this declaration found voice in a sustained "Ah-h-h!" as the door of the coupe swung slowly open. The crowd—it was now a crowd—stepped back involuntarily, and when the door had opened wide there was a ghostly pause. Then, very gradually, part by part, a pale, dangling individual stepped out of the wreck, pawing tentatively at the ground with a large uncertain dancing shoe.

Blinded by the glare of the headlights and confused by the constant honking of the horns, the figure stood swaying for a moment before he noticed the man in the long coat.

"What's the problem?" he asked calmly. "Did we run out of gas?"

"Look!"

Several people pointed at the detached wheel—he stared at it for a moment, then looked up as if he thought it might have fallen from the sky.

"It came off," someone clarified.

He nodded in understanding.

"At first, I didn't notice we'd stopped."

A pause. Then, taking a deep breath and straightening his shoulders, he asked in a determined voice:

"Could you tell me where there's a gas station?"

At least a dozen men, some of them a bit wealthier than he was, explained to him that the wheel and the car were no longer connected in any tangible way.

"Back out," he suggested after a moment. "Put it in reverse."

"But the wheel's off!"

He hesitated.

"No harm in trying," he said.

The blaring horns had reached a climax, and I turned away, cutting across the lawn toward home. I glanced back once. A sliver of the moon was shining over Gatsby's house, making the night as splendid as before, and outlasting the laughter and the sound of his still illuminated garden. A sudden emptiness seemed to flow now from the windows and the grand doors, bestowing a sense of complete isolation on the figure of the host, who stood on the porch, his hand raised in a formal gesture of farewell.

Reading over what I have written so far, I realize I may have given the impression that the events of three separate nights, spread out over several weeks, were all that captivated me. On the contrary, they were just fleeting moments in a bustling summer, and, until much later, they captivated me far less than my own personal matters.

Most of the time, I was working. In the early morning, the sun cast my shadow westward as I hurried through the towering canyons of lower Manhattan to the Probity Trust. I was on a first-name basis with the other clerks and young bond salesmen, and joined them for lunch in dim, packed restaurants, where we ate small pork sausages, mashed potatoes, and drank coffee. I even had a brief fling with a girl from Jersey City who worked in the accounting department, but her brother started giving me hostile looks, so when she took her vacation in July, I let the relationship fade away quietly.

I usually had dinner at the Yale Club—for some reason, it was the most depressing part of my day—and then I went upstairs to the library to study investments and securities for a dedicated hour. The place was often buzzing with lively groups, but they never ventured into the library, making it an ideal spot for work. Afterward, if the evening was pleasant, I'd walk down Madison Avenue past the old Murray Hill Hotel, and over to 33rd Street towards the Pennsylvania Station.

I began to appreciate New York, the exhilarating, adventurous feel of it at night, and the satisfaction that the constant movement of men, women, and cars gives to the restless observer. I enjoyed walking up Fifth Avenue, picking out intriguing women from the crowd, and imagining that in a few minutes, I'd become a part of their lives, unnoticed and unjudged. Sometimes, in my imagination, I followed them to their apartments on the corners of secluded streets, and they turned and smiled back at me before they disappeared through a door into a welcoming darkness. In the magical twilight of the city, I sometimes felt a profound loneliness, and sensed it in others too—poor young office workers who lingered in front of windows waiting until it was time for a lonely dinner at some restaurant—these young workers in the twilight, squandering the most touching moments of night and life.

Again at eight o’clock, when the dark streets of the Forties were packed with rows of pulsing taxis, headed for the theater district, my heart would sink. People leaned close together in the taxis as they waited, and voices sang, laughter erupted from unheard jokes, and the glow of cigarettes drew mysterious patterns in the air. Imagining that I, too, was rushing towards fun and sharing in their private joy, I wished them well.

For a while, I lost sight of Jordan Baker, and then in midsummer, I found her again. At first, I was flattered to go places with her because she was a golf champion, and everyone knew her name. Then it became something more. I wasn’t actually in love, but I felt a sort of tender curiosity. The bored, haughty face that she showed the world concealed something—most pretenses hide something eventually, even if they don’t at first—and one day, I discovered what it was. When we were at a house party together up in Warwick, she left a borrowed car out in the rain with the top down and then lied about it—and suddenly, I remembered the story about her that had eluded me that night at Daisy’s. At her first major golf tournament, there was a controversy that almost made the news—a suggestion that she had moved her ball from a bad lie in the semifinal round. The incident nearly became a scandal—then faded away. A caddy retracted his statement, and the only other witness admitted he might have been mistaken. The incident and the name had stuck in my mind.

Jordan Baker instinctively avoided clever, shrewd men, and now I saw that this was because she felt safer in a realm where any deviation from a code of conduct would be considered impossible. She was incurably dishonest. She couldn’t stand being at a disadvantage and, given this reluctance, I suppose she had started resorting to tricks when she was very young to maintain that cool, insolent smile for the world while still meeting the demands of her hard, jaunty body.

It made no difference to me. Dishonesty in a woman is not something you deeply fault—I was casually sorry, and then I forgot. It was during that same house party that we had a peculiar conversation about driving. It started because she drove so close to some workers that our fender brushed a button on one man’s coat.

"You're a terrible driver," I protested. "You should either be more careful or not drive at all."

"I am careful."

"No, you're not."

"Well, other people are," she said casually.

"What does that have to do with anything?"

"They'll stay out of my way," she insisted. "It takes two to cause an accident."

"Suppose you met somebody just as careless as yourself."

"I hope I never will," she answered. "I can't stand careless people. That's why I like you."

Her grey, sun-strained eyes looked straight ahead, but she had intentionally changed the dynamic between us, and for a moment, I thought I was in love with her. However, I'm slow to act, governed by a set of internal rules that rein in my impulses, and I knew I first had to clear up the situation back home. I had been sending letters every week, signing them with "Love, Nick," and all I could think about was how, when that certain girl played tennis, a subtle shadow of sweat would appear on her upper lip. Nonetheless, there was a vague agreement that needed to be gently ended before I could consider myself truly free.

Everyone suspects themselves of possessing at least one of the cardinal virtues, and this is mine: I am one of the few honest people that I have ever known.

On Sunday morning, while church bells rang in the villages along the shore, the world and its mistress returned to Gatsby's house and twinkled hilariously on his lawn.

“He’s a bootlegger,” said the young ladies, navigating between his cocktails and his flowers. “One time he killed a man who had discovered he was a nephew to Von Hindenburg and second cousin to the devil. Pass me a rose, honey, and pour me a last drop into that crystal glass.”

Once I jotted down on the blank spaces of a schedule the names of those who visited Gatsby’s house that summer. It's an old schedule now, falling apart at its folds, and titled “This schedule in effect July 5th, 1922.” But I can still make out the faded names, and they will give you a clearer picture than my broad descriptions of those who enjoyed Gatsby’s hospitality and paid him the subtle compliment of knowing absolutely nothing about him.

From East Egg, then, came the Chester Beckers and the Leeches, and a man named Bunsen, whom I knew at Yale, and Doctor Webster Civet, who drowned last summer up in Maine. And the Hornbeams and the Willie Voltaires, and an entire family named Blackbuck, who always huddled in a corner and turned up their noses like goats at anyone who approached. And the Ismays and the Chrysties (or rather Hubert Auerbach and Mr. Chrystie’s wife), and Edgar Beaver, whose hair, they say, turned cotton-white one winter afternoon for no apparent reason at all.

Clarence Endive was from East Egg, as I remember. He came only once, in white knickerbockers, and had a fight with a homeless man named Etty in the garden. From farther out on the Island came the Cheadles and the O. R. P. Schraeders, and the Stonewall Jackson Abrams of Georgia, and the Fishguards and the Ripley Snells. Snell was there three days before he went to prison, so drunk out on the gravel drive that Mrs. Ulysses Swett’s car ran over his right hand. The Dancies came, too, and S. B. Whitebait, who was well over sixty, and Maurice A. Flink, and the Hammerheads, and Beluga the tobacco importer, and Beluga’s girls.

From West Egg came the Poles and the Mulreadys and Cecil Roebuck and Cecil Schoen and Gulick the State senator and Newton Orchid, who controlled Films Par Excellence, and Eckhaust and Clyde Cohen and Don S. Schwartz (the son) and Arthur McCarty, all connected with the movie industry in one way or another. And the Catlips and the Bembergs and G. Earl Muldoon, brother to that Muldoon who afterward strangled his wife. Da Fontano the promoter came there, and Ed Legros and James B. (“Rot-Gut”) Ferret and the De Jongs and Ernest Lilly—they came to gamble, and when Ferret wandered into the garden it meant he was broke and Associated Traction would have to see a good day's trading next day.

A man named Klipspringer was there so often that he became known as “the boarder”—I doubt if he had any other home. Of theatrical people there were Gus Waize and Horace O’Donavan and Lester Myer and George Duckweed and Francis Bull. Also from New York were the Chromes and the Backhyssons and the Dennickers and Russel Betty and the Corrigans and the Kellehers and the Dewars and the Scullys and S. W. Belcher and the Smirkes and the young Quinns, now divorced, and Henry L. Palmetto, who killed himself by jumping in front of a subway train in Times Square.

Benny McClenahan always showed up with four girls. They were never the same individuals, but they were so similar to each other that it felt like they had been there before. I've forgotten their names—Jaqueline, perhaps, or maybe Consuela, Gloria, Judy, or June, and their last names were either the poetic names of flowers and months or the more imposing ones of the great American tycoons, whose distant relatives they would admit to being if really pressed.

In addition to these, I remember that Faustina O’Brien visited at least once, as did the Baedeker girls and young Brewer, who lost his nose in World War I, and Mr. Albrucksburger and Miss Haag, his fiancée, and Ardita Fitz-Peters and Mr. P. Jewett, once the head of the American Legion, and Miss Claudia Hip, accompanied by a man rumored to be her chauffeur, and a prince of some sort, whom we called Duke, and whose name, if I ever knew it, I've since forgotten.

All these people visited Gatsby’s house that summer.

At nine o'clock, one morning late in July, Gatsby's stunning car navigated up the rocky drive to my door and emitted a burst of melody from its three-noted horn.

It was the first time he had called on me, though I had attended two of his parties, ridden in his hydroplane, and, at his eager invitation, made frequent use of his beach.

"Good morning, old sport. You're having lunch with me today, and I thought we'd ride up together."

He was perched on the dashboard of his car with that agility of movement that's so distinctly American—arising, I suppose, from not doing heavy lifting in youth and, even more, from the unstructured elegance of our fast-paced, erratic sports. This trait kept surfacing through his meticulous demeanor as a kind of restlessness. He could never stay completely still; there was always a foot tapping or a hand opening and closing with impatience.

He noticed me admiring his car.

“It’s pretty, isn’t it, old sport?” He jumped off to give me a better view. “Haven’t you ever seen it before?”

I’d seen it. Everybody had seen it. It was a rich cream color, bright with nickel, swollen here and there in its monstrous length with triumphant hatboxes and supper-boxes and toolboxes, and terraced with a labyrinth of windshields that mirrored a dozen suns. Sitting down behind many layers of glass in a sort of green leather conservatory, we started to town.

I had talked with him perhaps half a dozen times in the past month and found, to my disappointment, that he had little to say. So my first impression, that he was a person of some undefined consequence, had gradually faded and he had become simply the owner of an elaborate club next door.

And then came that unsettling ride. We hadn’t reached West Egg village before Gatsby started leaving his elegant sentences hanging and slapping himself indecisively on the knee of his caramel-colored suit.

“Look here, old sport,” he suddenly burst out, “what’s your opinion of me, anyhow?”

A bit overwhelmed, I started with the generalized evasions that such a question warrants.

“Well, I’m going to tell you something about my life,” he interrupted. “I don’t want you to get the wrong idea of me from all these stories you hear.”

So, he was aware of the bizarre accusations that flavored conversation in his halls.

“I’ll tell you the honest truth.” His right hand suddenly seemed to call for divine retribution to stand by. “I am the son of some wealthy people in the Middle West—all dead now. I was brought up in America but educated at Oxford, because all my ancestors have been educated there for many years. It’s a family tradition.”

He glanced at me sideways—and I understood why Jordan Baker had thought he was lying. He rushed through the phrase “educated at Oxford,” or mumbled it, or stumbled over it, as if it had troubled him before. And with this doubt, his entire statement crumbled, and I began to wonder if there wasn't something slightly sinister about him, after all.

“What part of the Midwest?” I asked casually.

“San Francisco.”

"I see."

"My family all passed away, and I inherited a significant amount of money."

His voice was solemn, as if the memory of that abrupt end to his family lineage still haunted him. For a moment, I thought he might be joking with me, but one look at him convinced me otherwise.

"After that, I lived like a young prince in all the capitals of Europe—Paris, Venice, Rome—amassing jewels, mainly rubies, hunting big game, dabbling in painting, just for myself, and trying to forget a profound sadness that had befallen me long ago."

With effort, I managed to hold back my disbelief-filled laughter. The phrases he used were so clichéd they conjured up nothing but the image of a caricatured figure, his costume coming apart at the seams, as he chased a tiger through the Bois de Boulogne.

"Then came the war, old sport. It was a welcome distraction, and I tried very hard to die, but it seemed I led a charmed life. I took a commission as a first lieutenant when it started. In the Argonne Forest, I led what was left of my machine-gun battalion so far ahead that we were isolated by a half-mile gap on either side, where the infantry couldn’t push forward. We held our position for two days and two nights, a hundred and thirty men with sixteen Lewis guns, and when the infantry finally caught up, they found the insignia of three German divisions among the heaps of the dead. I was promoted to major, and received decorations from every Allied nation—even Montenegro, tiny Montenegro, tucked away on the Adriatic Sea!"

Little Montenegro! He lifted up the words and nodded at them—with his smile. The smile understood Montenegro's tumultuous history and empathized with the valiant efforts of the Montenegrin people. It fully recognized the series of national events that had prompted this honor from Montenegro's passionate heart. My skepticism was now drowned in fascination; it was like quickly flipping through a dozen magazines.

"Just as much as you are."

"Don't talk so loud."

"You're acting like a little boy," I burst out impatiently. "Not only that, but you're being rude. Daisy's sitting in there all by herself."

He raised his hand to stop my words, looked at me with unforgettable reproach, and, opening the door cautiously, went back into the other room.

I walked out the back way—just as Gatsby had when he had made his nervous circuit of the house half an hour before—and ran for a huge black knotted tree, whose dense leaves formed a barrier against the rain. Once more it was pouring, and my irregular lawn, meticulously maintained by Gatsby’s gardener, was full of small muddy puddles and ancient marshes. There was nothing to see from under the tree except Gatsby’s enormous house, so I stared at it, like Kant at his church steeple, for half an hour. A brewer had built it early in the craze for period styles, a decade before, and there was a story that he’d agreed to pay five years’ taxes on all the neighboring cottages if the owners would thatch their roofs with straw. Perhaps their refusal discouraged him from his plan to establish a family legacy—he quickly lost interest. His children sold the house with the black wreath still on the door. Americans, while willing, even eager, to be underlings, have always resisted being seen as peasants.

After half an hour, the sun came out again, and the grocer’s car turned into Gatsby’s drive with the ingredients for his servants’ dinner—I was sure he wouldn’t eat a spoonful. A maid began to open the upper windows of his house, appeared briefly in each, and, leaning from the large central bay, spat thoughtfully into the garden. It was time I went back. While the rain continued, it had seemed like the murmur of their voices, rising and swelling a little now and then with gusts of emotion. But in the new silence, I felt that silence had descended within the house too.

I went in—after making every possible noise in the kitchen, short of knocking over the stove—but I don’t think they heard a thing. They were sitting at either end of the couch, looking at each other as if some question had been asked, or was hanging in the air, and every trace of awkwardness was gone. Daisy’s face was streaked with tears, and when I came in she jumped up and began wiping at it with her handkerchief before a mirror. But there was a change in Gatsby that was simply astonishing. He literally glowed; without a word or a gesture of triumph, a new well-being radiated from him and filled the small room.

"Oh, hello, old sport," he said, as if he hadn't seen me for years. I thought for a moment he was going to shake hands.

"It's stopped raining."

"Has it?" When he realized what I was talking about, that there were twinkle-bells of sunshine in the room, he smiled like a weatherman, like an ecstatic fan of recurring light, and repeated the news to Daisy. "What do you think of that? It's stopped raining."

"I'm glad, Jay." Her voice, filled with a poignant, mournful beauty, conveyed only her surprise and happiness.

"I want you and Daisy to come over to my house," he said, "I'd like to give her a tour."

"You're sure you want me to come along?"

"Absolutely," he agreed.

Daisy went upstairs to wash her face—too late, I thought with embarrassment about my towels—while Gatsby and I waited on the lawn.

"My house looks good, doesn't it?" he asked. "See how the whole front of it catches the light."

I agreed that it was splendid.

"Yes." His eyes scanned it, every arched door and square tower. "It took me just three years to earn the money that bought it."

"I thought you inherited your money."

“I did, old sport,” he said automatically, “but I lost most of it in the big panic—the panic of the war.”

I think he hardly knew what he was saying, for when I asked him what business he was in, he answered: “That’s my business,” before he realized that it wasn’t an appropriate reply.

“Oh, I’ve been involved in several ventures,” he corrected himself. “I was in the pharmaceuticals and then I was in the energy sector. But I’m not in either one now.” He looked at me with more attention. “Do you mean you’ve been considering what I suggested the other night?”

Before I could reply, Daisy emerged from the house, the two rows of brass buttons on her dress sparkling in the sunlight.

“That massive place over there?” she exclaimed, pointing.

“Do you like it?”

“I love it, but I don’t see how you live there all alone.”

“I always have it filled with interesting people, night and day. People who do interesting things. Celebrities.”

Instead of taking the shortcut along the Sound, we went down to the road and entered through the grand side gate. With enchanting murmurs, Daisy admired this aspect or that of the castle-like silhouette against the sky, admired the gardens, the sparkling scent of jonquils, and the frothy scent of hawthorn and plum blossoms, and the pale gold scent of kiss-me-at-the-gate. It was odd to reach the marble steps and find no movement of bright dresses in and out the door, and to hear no sound but bird songs in the trees.

And inside, as we explored Marie Antoinette music rooms and Restoration Salons, I felt as though there were guests hiding behind every couch and table, silently waiting for us to pass. When Gatsby closed the door of "the Merton College Library," I was almost sure I heard the owl-eyed man burst into ghostly laughter.

We went upstairs, through bedrooms draped in rose and lavender silk, alive with fresh flowers, past dressing rooms and pool rooms, and bathrooms with sunken baths—accidentally walking in on a disheveled man in pajamas doing liver exercises on the floor. It was Mr. Klipspringer, the "boarder." I had seen him roaming the beach that morning, looking lost. Finally, we arrived at Gatsby's own suite, a bedroom and bath, and an Adam study, where we sat and drank a glass of Chartreuse he pulled from a cupboard in the wall.

He never stopped watching Daisy, and I think he reassessed the value of everything in his house based on how much it seemed to impress her beloved eyes. Sometimes, he looked around at his possessions in a dazed manner, as if, in her real and astonishing presence, none of it mattered anymore. Once, he almost fell down a flight of stairs.

His bedroom was the simplest of them all—except for the dresser, which was adorned with a toiletry set made of pure, matte gold. Daisy picked up the brush with delight and smoothed her hair, at which point Gatsby sat down, covered his eyes, and began to laugh.

"It's the funniest thing, old sport," he said, bursting with laughter. "I can't—When I try to—"

He had visibly transitioned through two states and was now entering a third. After his initial embarrassment and his irrational joy, he was now filled with wonder at her presence. He had been obsessed with the idea for so long, had dreamt it through to the end, and had waited with bated breath, so to speak, at an unimaginable level of intensity. Now, in the aftermath, he was winding down like an over-wound clock.

Recovering himself in a minute, he opened for us two massive, modern cabinets that housed his extensive collection of suits, dressing gowns, and ties, along with his shirts, stacked neatly in rows, each a dozen high.

“I have a guy in England who picks out my clothes. He sends over a selection of items at the start of each season, spring and fall.”

He pulled out a stack of shirts and began tossing them, one by one, in front of us—shirts made of sheer linen, thick silk, and fine flannel, which unfolded as they landed and spread across the table in a multicolored chaos. As we looked on in admiration, he brought out more, and the soft, luxurious pile grew taller—shirts in stripes, scrolls, and plaids in coral, apple-green, lavender, and faint orange, with monograms in Indian blue. Suddenly, with a choked sound, Daisy bent her head into the shirts and began to cry stormily.

"They're such beautiful shirts," she sobbed, her voice muffled in the thick folds. "It makes me sad because I've never seen such—such beautiful shirts before."

After the house, we were to see the grounds and the swimming pool, and the hydroplane, and the midsummer flowers—but outside Gatsby's window, it began to rain again, so we stood in a row looking at the corrugated surface of the Sound.

“If it wasn’t for the mist, we could see your house across the bay,” said Gatsby. “You always have a green light that burns all night at the end of your dock.”

Daisy linked her arm through his abruptly, but he seemed lost in his thoughts, absorbed by what he had just said. Perhaps it had dawned on him that the monumental importance of that light had now vanished forever. Compared to the vast distance that had separated him from Daisy, it had seemed very close to her, almost within reach. It had seemed as close as a star to the moon. Now, it was once again just a green light on a dock. His collection of enchanted objects had decreased by one.

I began to walk around the room, examining various vague objects in the dim light. A large photograph of an elderly man dressed in yachting attire caught my attention, hanging on the wall above his desk.

"Who's this?"

"That? That's Mr. Dan Cody, old sport."

The name sounded vaguely familiar.

"He's passed away now. He was my best friend years back."

There was a small picture of Gatsby, also dressed for yachting, on the bureau—Gatsby with his head thrown back defiantly—captured apparently when he was about eighteen.

"I love it," exclaimed Daisy. "The hairstyle! You never told me you had that kind of hair—or a yacht."

"Look at this," said Gatsby quickly. "Here's a bunch of articles—about you."

They stood side by side looking at it. I was about to ask to see the rubies when the phone rang, and Gatsby answered it.

"Yes... Well, I can't talk now... I can't talk right now, old sport... I mentioned a small town... He should know what a small town means... Well, he's no good to us if he thinks Detroit is a small town..."

He hung up.

"Come here quick!" Daisy called from the window.

The rain continued to pour, but the darkness had split open in the west, revealing a pink and golden swell of foamy clouds above the sea.

"Look at that," she whispered, and then after a moment: "I'd love to just grab one of those pink clouds and put you in it and push you around."

I tried to leave then, but they wouldn't hear of it; perhaps my presence made them feel more comfortably alone.

"I know what we'll do," said Gatsby, "we'll have Klipspringer play the piano."

He left the room, calling "Ewing!" and came back shortly with a sheepish, slightly disheveled young man wearing shell-rimmed glasses and thinning blond hair. He was now appropriately dressed in a casual button-down shirt, left open at the neck, sneakers, and khaki pants of an indistinct color.

"Did we interrupt your workout?" Daisy asked politely.

"I was sleeping," exclaimed Mr. Klipspringer, caught in a wave of embarrassment. "That is, I had been sleeping. Then I got up..."

"Klipspringer plays the piano," said Gatsby, cutting him off. "Don't you, Ewing, old sport?"

"I don't play well. I don't—hardly play at all. I'm all out of practice—"

"We'll go downstairs," interrupted Gatsby. He flipped a switch. The grey windows disappeared as the house glowed full of light.

In the music room, Gatsby switched on a single lamp by the piano. He lit Daisy's cigarette with a shaking match and sat down with her on a couch far across the room, where the only light was what the polished floor reflected from the hallway.

When Klipspringer finished playing "The Love Nest," he turned around on the bench, peering through the dimness for Gatsby.

"I'm totally out of practice, you see. I told you I couldn't play. I'm completely out of prac—"

"Don't talk so much, old sport," commanded Gatsby. "Play!"

"In the morning, in the evening, ain't we got fun—"

Outside, the wind was loud, and there was a faint rumble of thunder along the Sound. All the lights were turning on in West Egg now; the electric trains, filled with passengers, were making their way home through the rain from New York. It was a time of profound human change, and excitement was brewing in the air.

"One thing's certain and nothing's more certain: the wealthy grow wealthier and the poor expand their families. In the meantime, in the in-between time—"

As I went over to say goodbye, I noticed that Gatsby's face had once again taken on a look of confusion, as if he was beginning to doubt the authenticity of his current joy. Nearly five years! There must have been instances, even that afternoon, when Daisy fell short of his expectations—not by her own doing, but because the immense vitality of his fantasy had surpassed her, had surpassed everything. He had invested himself in it with a creative passion, constantly embellishing it, adorning it with every attractive detail that came his way. No amount of vibrancy or novelty can compete with what a man can accumulate in his spiritual heart.

Watching him, he made a slight adjustment, visibly. He took her hand, and as she whispered something softly in his ear, he turned to her, overwhelmed with emotion. I believe it was her voice that captivated him the most, with its variable, fervent warmth, because it was something that couldn't be outdreamed—that voice was an eternal melody.

They had forgotten me, but Daisy glanced up and held out her hand; Gatsby didn’t recognize me at all now. I looked once more at them and they looked back at me, distantly, consumed by intense emotions. Then I left the room and walked down the marble steps into the rain, leaving them there together.

About this time, an ambitious young reporter from New York arrived one morning at Gatsby's door and asked him if he had anything to say.

"Anything to say about what?" Gatsby asked politely.

"Why—any statement to release?"

It became clear after a confusing five minutes that the man had heard Gatsby's name mentioned at his office in a context that he either wouldn't disclose or didn't fully grasp. Today was his day off, and with commendable initiative, he had rushed over "to see."

It was a shot in the dark, but the reporter's hunch was spot on. Gatsby's fame, fueled by the hundreds who had enjoyed his generosity and thus became experts on his history, had grown all summer until he was almost a sensation. Modern myths like the "secret tunnel to Canada" became associated with him, and there was a relentless rumor that he didn't actually live in a house, but in a boat designed to look like one, stealthily moving along the Long Island coast. Why these fabrications pleased James Gatz of North Dakota is not straightforward to explain.

James Gatz—that was his real, or at least his legal, name. He changed it at seventeen, precisely at the moment that marked the start of his career—when he saw Dan Cody's yacht anchor at the most dangerous spot on Lake Superior. It was James Gatz who was idling on the beach that afternoon in a torn green jersey and canvas pants, but it was already Jay Gatsby who borrowed a rowboat, rowed out to the Tuolomee, and warned Cody that a sudden wind could wreck his yacht in half an hour.

I guess he had been holding onto the name for quite some time, even then. His parents were aimless and unsuccessful farmers—his imagination never truly acknowledged them as his parents. The truth was that Jay Gatsby of West Egg, Long Island, emerged from his idealized vision of himself. He was a son of God—a phrase which, if it means anything, means just that—and he was destined for His Father's work, the pursuit of a grand, ostentatious, and gaudy beauty. So, he crafted exactly the kind of Jay Gatsby that a seventeen-year-old boy would dream up, and to this vision, he remained loyal till the end.

For over a year, he had been making his way along the south shore of Lake Superior as a clam-digger and a salmon-fisher or in any other role that provided him with food and shelter. His body, bronzed and toughened, adapted naturally to the strenuous yet laid-back work of the invigorating days. He became acquainted with women early on, and since they pampered him, he grew disdainful of them—of young virgins for their naivety, and of the others for their exaggerated reactions to things which, in his profound self-absorption, he considered trivial.

Yet, his heart was in a constant state of upheaval. The most bizarre and imaginative notions tormented him in his bed at night. A universe of indescribable brilliance unfolded in his mind while the clock ticked on the washstand and the moonlight drenched his crumpled clothes on the floor. Night after night, he expanded the tapestry of his fantasies until sleep enveloped a particularly vivid scene in its forgetful grasp. For a time, these daydreams served as an outlet for his imagination; they were a satisfactory glimpse into the illusion of reality, a promise that the foundation of the world was as stable as a fairy's wing.

Driven by an instinct towards his destined greatness, he had found his way, some months earlier, to the small Lutheran College of St. Olaf's in southern Minnesota. He lasted there for two weeks, disheartened by its blatant disregard for the drumbeat of his destiny, for destiny itself, and loathing the janitorial work that was supposed to fund his education. Then he drifted back to Lake Superior, and he was still searching for his purpose on the day Dan Cody’s yacht anchored in the shallow waters along the shore.

Cody was fifty years old then, a product of the Nevada silver fields, of the Yukon, of every rush for metal since seventy-five. The deals in Montana copper that made him many times a millionaire found him physically robust but on the verge of soft-mindedness, and, suspecting this, countless women tried to separate him from his wealth. The not-so-reputable ways in which Ella Kaye, the journalist, played Madame de Maintenon to his vulnerability and sent him to sea in a yacht, were widely reported by the sensationalist press in 1902. He had been coasting along all too welcoming shores for five years when he turned up as James Gatz’s destiny in Little Girl Bay.

To young Gatz, resting on his oars and looking up at the railed deck, that yacht represented all the beauty and glamour in the world. I suppose he smiled at Cody—he had probably discovered that people liked him when he smiled. At any rate, Cody asked him a few questions (one of them elicited the brand new name) and found that he was quick and extravagantly ambitious. A few days later, he took him to Duluth and bought him a blue coat, six pairs of white duck trousers, and a yachting cap. And when the Tuolomee left for the West Indies and the Barbary Coast, Gatsby left too.

He was employed in a vague personal capacity—while he remained with Cody, he was in turn steward, mate, skipper, secretary, and even jailor, for Dan Cody sober knew what lavish doings Dan Cody drunk might soon be about, and he provided for such contingencies by placing more and more trust in Gatsby. The arrangement lasted five years, during which the boat went three times around the Continent. It might have lasted indefinitely except for the fact that Ella Kaye came on board one night in Boston and a week later Dan Cody inhospitably died.

I remember the portrait of him up in Gatsby’s bedroom, a grey, florid man with a hard, empty face—the pioneer debauchee, who during one phase of American life reintroduced to the Eastern seaboard the brutal violence of the frontier brothel and saloon. It was indirectly because of Cody that Gatsby drank so little. Sometimes during lively parties, women would rub champagne into his hair; for himself, he developed the habit of avoiding alcohol.

And it was from Cody that he was supposed to inherit money—a legacy of twenty-five thousand dollars. He didn’t get it. He never grasped the legal maneuver that was used against him, but what remained of the millions went entirely to Ella Kaye. He was left with his uniquely fitting education; the vague outline of Jay Gatsby had filled out into the solidity of a man.

He shared all this with me much later, but I've included it here to debunk those initial, wildly inaccurate rumors about his background, which were far from the truth. Moreover, he shared it during a period of confusion, when I was at a point of believing everything and nothing about him. So, I take this moment, while Gatsby, metaphorically speaking, caught his breath, to dispel these misconceptions.

It was also a pause in my involvement with his affairs. For several weeks, I neither saw him nor heard his voice on the phone—I was mostly in New York, hanging out with Jordan and trying to win over her senile aunt—but eventually, I visited his house one Sunday afternoon. I had barely been there for two minutes when someone brought Tom Buchanan in for a drink. Naturally, I was startled, but what truly surprised me was that this hadn't happened sooner.

They were a group of three on horseback—Tom, a man named Sloane, and a beautiful woman in a brown riding outfit, who had visited before.

"I'm delighted to see you," said Gatsby, standing on his porch. "I'm delighted that you dropped by."

As if they cared!

"Sit right down. Have a cigarette or a cigar." He moved quickly around the room, pressing buttons. "I'll have a drink for you in just a minute."

He was deeply unsettled by Tom's presence. Yet, he would feel anxious until he had offered them something, understanding vaguely that this was their only reason for coming. Mr. Sloane wanted nothing. A lemonade? No, thanks. Some champagne? Not at all, thanks... I'm sorry—

"Did you enjoy your drive?"

"The roads are really good around here."

"I believe we've met before, Mr. Buchanan."

"Oh, yes," said Tom, with forced politeness, clearly not recalling the encounter. "So we did. I remember now."

"We'll all come to your next party, Mr. Gatsby," she suggested. "What do you think?"

"You're both coming to dinner with me," said the lady with excitement.

This invitation included me. Mr. Sloane stood up.

"Let's go," he said—but his invitation was clearly meant for her alone.

Suddenly, Mr. Sloane and the lady walked down the steps and got on their horses.

Tom and I shook hands, the rest of us exchanged a cool nod, and they trotted quickly down the drive, disappearing under the August foliage just as Gatsby, with hat and light overcoat in hand, came out the front door.

Tom was evidently perturbed at Daisy's going out alone, for on the following Saturday night he came with her to Gatsby's party. Perhaps his presence gave the evening its peculiar quality of oppressiveness—it stands out in my memory from Gatsby's other parties that summer. There were the same people, or at least the same sort of people, the same profusion of champagne, the same many-colored, many-keyed commotion, but I felt an unpleasantness in the air, a pervading harshness that hadn't been there before. Or perhaps I had merely grown used to it, grown to accept West Egg as a world complete in itself, with its own standards and its own great figures, second to nothing because it had no consciousness of being so, and now I was looking at it again, through Daisy's eyes. It is invariably saddening to look through new eyes at things upon which you have expended your own powers of adjustment.

They arrived at twilight, and as we walked out among the glittering crowd, Daisy's voice was weaving enchanting sounds in her throat.

"These things thrill me so," she whispered. "If you want to kiss me at any point during the evening, Nick, just give me a hint and I'll be more than happy to make it happen for you. Just drop my name. Or flash a green card. I'm handing out green—"

"She's beautiful," said Daisy.

"The man leaning in to speak with her is her director."

Daisy and Gatsby danced. I remember being surprised by his graceful, conservative foxtrot—I had never seen him dance before. Then they sauntered over to my house and sat on the steps for half an hour, while at her request I remained watchfully in the garden. “In case there’s a fire or a flood,” she explained, “or any act of God.”

Tom appeared from his oblivion as we were sitting down to dinner together. “Do you mind if I eat with some people over here?” he said. “A guy’s cracking some hilarious jokes.”

“Go ahead,” answered Daisy warmly, “and if you want to take down any addresses here’s my little gold pencil.” … She looked around after a moment and told me the girl was “ordinary but pretty,” and I knew that except for the half-hour she’d been alone with Gatsby she wasn’t having a good time.

We were at a particularly lively table. That was my fault—Gatsby had been called to the phone, and I'd enjoyed these same people only two weeks before. But what had amused me then felt toxic in the atmosphere now.

"How do you feel, Miss Baedeker?"

The girl addressed was trying, unsuccessfully, to lean against my shoulder. At this inquiry, she sat up and opened her eyes.

"What?"

A large and sluggish woman, who had been encouraging Daisy to join her for golf at the local club the next day, spoke up in defense of Miss Baedeker:

"Oh, she's fine now. Whenever she's had five or six cocktails, she always starts screaming like that. I tell her she should really stop drinking."

"I do leave it alone," affirmed the accused, his voice empty of conviction.

"We heard you yelling, so I said to Doc Civet here: 'Someone needs your help, Doc.'"

"She's really thankful, I'm sure," said another friend, his tone lacking any real gratitude, "but you got her dress all soaked when you dunked her head in the pool."

"Anything I hate is getting my head stuck underwater," mumbled Miss Baedeker. "They almost drowned me once over in New Jersey."

"Then you should stay away from it," countered Doctor Civet.

"Speak for yourself!" cried Miss Baedeker fiercely. "Your hand shakes. I wouldn't let you perform surgery on me!"

It was like that. Almost the last thing I remember was standing with Daisy and watching the movie director and his star. They were still under the white-plum tree, and their faces were touching except for a pale, thin ray of moonlight between. It occurred to me that he had been very slowly bending toward her all evening to achieve this closeness, and even as I watched, I saw him stoop one ultimate degree and kiss her cheek.

“I like her,” said Daisy, “I think she’s lovely.”

But the rest offended her—and undeniably because it wasn’t just a gesture but an emotion. She was appalled by West Egg, this unprecedented “place” that Broadway had spawned in a Long Island fishing village—appalled by its raw energy that clashed with the old euphemisms and by the too conspicuous destiny that pushed its residents along a shortcut from obscurity to oblivion. She saw something terrible in the very simplicity she failed to grasp.

I sat on the front steps with them while they waited for their car. It was dark here in front; only the bright door sent ten square feet of light volleying out into the soft black morning. Sometimes a shadow moved against a dressing-room blind above, gave way to another shadow, an indefinite procession of shadows, who rouged and powdered in an invisible glass.

“Who is this Gatsby anyhow?” demanded Tom suddenly. “Some big bootlegger?”

“Where’d you hear that?” I inquired.

"I didn't hear it. I imagined it. A lot of these new money folks are just major bootleggers, you know."

"Not Gatsby," I said firmly.

He was silent for a moment. The pebbles of the driveway crunched under his feet.

“Well, he definitely went all out to assemble this eclectic mix.”

A breeze ruffled the grey haze of Daisy’s fur collar.

“At least they're more interesting than the people we know,” she said, trying to sound enthusiastic.

"You didn't seem too interested."

"Well, I was."

Tom laughed and turned to me.

"Did you see Daisy's face when that girl asked her to cool off with a cold shower?"

Daisy began to sing along with the music in a husky, rhythmic whisper, infusing each word with a meaning it had never had before and would never have again. When the melody soared, her voice sweetly broke, following it in a way unique to contralto voices, and with each change, she sprinkled a bit of her warm human magic into the air.

"Lots of people show up who haven't been invited," she suddenly said. "That girl wasn't invited. They just barge in, and he's too polite to say anything."

"I'd like to know who he is and what he does," insisted Tom. "And I think I'll make a point of finding out."

"I can tell you right now," she answered. "He owned some pharmacies, a lot of pharmacies. He built them up himself."

The delayed limousine came rolling up the drive.

"Good night, Nick," said Daisy.

Her gaze shifted from me to the illuminated top of the steps, where "Three O'Clock in the Morning," a neat, sad little waltz of that year, was floating out the open door. Despite the laid-back vibe of Gatsby's party, it held romantic possibilities completely missing from her own world. What was in that song that seemed to beckon her back inside? What could unfold now in the unpredictable, late hours? Maybe some extraordinary guest would show up, someone incredibly unique and awe-inspiring, an authentically dazzling young woman who, with a single fresh look at Gatsby, a moment of magical connection, would erase those five years of unwavering devotion.

I stayed late that night. Gatsby asked me to hang around until he was available, and I waited in the garden until the inevitable swimming party had returned, exhilarated and shivering, from the dark beach, until the lights in the guest rooms above were turned off. When he finally descended the steps, his tan skin was unusually tight across his face, and his eyes were bright yet weary.

"She didn't enjoy it," he said right away.

"Of course she did."

"She didn't enjoy it," he insisted. "She didn't have a good time."

He was silent, and I sensed his indescribable despair.

"I feel so distant from her," he said. "It's difficult to get her to understand."

"You mean about the party?"

"The dance?" He waved away all the parties he had thrown with a flick of his wrist. "My friend, the dance doesn't matter."

He desired nothing less from Daisy than for her to confront Tom and declare: "I never loved you." Once she had erased four years with those words, they could then consider the more practical steps to be taken. One of which was that, once she was free, they would return to Louisville and get married from her home—just as if it were five years earlier.

"And she doesn't get it," he lamented. "She used to understand. We could sit for hours—"

He stopped abruptly and started pacing back and forth on a lonely path littered with fruit peels, thrown-away party favors, and trampled flowers.

"I wouldn't expect too much from her," I suggested cautiously. "You can't relive the past."

"Can't relive the past?" he exclaimed in disbelief. "Why, of course you can!"

He looked around him wildly, as if the past were hiding in the shadow of his house, just beyond his grasp.

“I’m going to fix everything just the way it was before,” he said, nodding with determination. “She’ll see.”

He talked a lot about the past, and I gathered that he wanted to reclaim something, maybe some version of himself, that had been lost in his love for Daisy. His life had been chaotic and disorganized since then, but if he could just get back to a certain starting point and go through everything slowly, he might discover what that lost element was...

One autumn night, five years before, they had been walking down the street as the leaves fell, arriving at a spot where no trees stood and the sidewalk glowed under the moonlight. They stopped and faced each other. It was a cool night filled with that mysterious excitement that comes with the seasonal shifts. The soft lights from the houses spilled into the darkness, and there was a lively movement among the stars. From the corner of his eye, Gatsby noticed that the sidewalk blocks formed a ladder leading to a secret place above the trees—he could reach it if he climbed alone, and once there, he could indulge in the essence of life, savoring the unparalleled nectar of wonder.

His heart raced as Daisy's pale face drew close to his. He realized that kissing this girl, binding his profound dreams to her fleeting existence, would mean his imagination could no longer soar as freely as the divine. So he waited, listening a moment longer to the celestial note that had been struck. Then he kissed her. With his touch, she bloomed for him like a flower, and the transformation was complete.

Throughout all he said, even amid his overwhelming sentimentality, I was reminded of something—an elusive rhythm, a fragment of forgotten words, that I had encountered long ago. For a moment, a phrase tried to form on my lips, and they parted as if burdened with more than a mere breath of surprise. Yet, they remained silent, and what I had nearly recalled was lost to expression forever.

It was when curiosity about Gatsby was at its peak that the lights in his house didn't come on one Saturday night—and, as mysteriously as it had started, his era as Trimalchio was over. Only gradually did I realize that the cars which turned expectantly into his driveway stayed for just a minute before driving away in disappointment. Wondering if he was sick, I went over to find out—an unfamiliar butler with a menacing face peered at me suspiciously from the door.

“Is Mr. Gatsby sick?”

"Nope." After a pause, he added "sir" in a slow, reluctant manner.

"I hadn't seen him around, and I was getting worried. Tell him Mr. Carraway stopped by."

"Who?" he demanded rudely.

"Carraway."

"Carraway. All right, I'll let him know."

Suddenly, he slammed the door shut.

My Finn told me that Gatsby had fired every servant in his house a week ago and replaced them with half a dozen others, who never went into West Egg village to be bribed by the local businesses, but ordered moderate supplies over the phone. The grocery delivery person reported that the kitchen looked like a mess, and the general opinion in the village was that the new people weren’t servants at all.

The next day, Gatsby called me on the phone.

“Going away?” I inquired.

"No, old sport."

"I hear you let go of all your staff."

"I needed people who wouldn't spread rumors. Daisy comes over quite often—in the afternoons."

So the entire assembly had collapsed like a house of cards at the disapproval in her eyes.

"They're some people Wolfsheim wanted to help out. They're all siblings. They used to manage a small hotel."

"I see."

He was calling at Daisy's request—would I join them for lunch at her house tomorrow? Miss Baker would be there too. Half an hour later, Daisy herself called and seemed relieved to hear that I was coming. Something was definitely going on. Yet, I found it hard to believe they would use this occasion for a confrontation—especially the intense confrontation Gatsby had hinted at in the garden.

The next day was scorching, likely the hottest, and possibly the last, of the summer. As my train came out of the tunnel into the sunlight, only the loud whistles from the National Biscuit Company broke the stifling silence at noon. The straw seats in the train felt like they were about to catch fire; the woman sitting next to me began to sweat lightly into her white blouse, and then, as her newspaper became damp under her fingers, she gave up hope and succumbed to the overwhelming heat with a desolate cry. Her purse fell to the floor.

“Oh, my!” she exclaimed.

I picked it up with a tired lean and handed it back to her, holding it at arm's length and by the very tips of the corners to show I had no interest in it—but everyone close by, including the woman, doubted my intentions just the same.

"Hot!" said the conductor to familiar faces. "Some weather! ... Hot! ... Hot! ... Hot! ... Is it hot enough for you? Is it hot? Is it ...?"

My monthly pass came back to me with a dark stain from his hand. That anyone should care in this sweltering heat about whose flushed lips he kissed, whose head left a damp spot on the pajama pocket over his heart!

Through the hall of the Buchanans' house, a slight breeze carried the sound of the phone ringing out to Gatsby and me as we waited at the door.

"The master's body?" the butler shouted into the phone. "I'm sorry, madame, but we can't provide it—it's far too hot to handle right now!"

What he actually said was: "Yes... Yes... I'll check."

He put down the phone and came toward us, slightly sweaty, to take our stiff straw hats.

"Madame expects you in the living room!" he exclaimed, unnecessarily pointing the way. In this heat, every unnecessary movement was a drain on everyone's energy.

The room, well-shaded by awnings, was dark and cool. Daisy and Jordan reclined on a huge couch, like silver idols pressing down their own white dresses against the cool air from the fans.

"We can't move," they said in unison.

Jordan's fingers, dusted white against their tan, lingered for a moment in mine.

"And Mr. Thomas Buchanan, the athlete?" I asked.

Simultaneously, I heard his voice, gruff, muffled, husky, on the hall phone.

Gatsby stood in the center of the crimson carpet and looked around with fascinated eyes. Daisy watched him and laughed, her sweet, exciting laugh; a tiny gust of powder rose from her chest into the air.

"The rumor is," whispered Jordan, "that that's Tom's girl on the phone."

We were silent. The voice in the hall grew louder, filled with irritation: "Fine, then, I won't sell you the car at all... I don't owe you anything... and as for you hassling me about it during lunch, I'm not putting up with that at all!"

"Hogging the phone," said Daisy with a cynical tone.

"No, he's not," I assured her. "It's a legitimate deal. I happen to know about it."

Tom burst through the door, his broad frame momentarily filling the doorway, and rushed into the room.

"Mr. Gatsby!" He extended his wide, flat hand, barely hiding his disdain. "I'm glad to see you, sir... Nick..."

"Whip us up some cold drinks," Daisy exclaimed.

As he exited the room once more, she stood up, approached Gatsby, and drew his face down, pressing her lips to his.

"You know I love you," she whispered.

"You forget we have company," Jordan remarked.

Daisy glanced around, uncertain.

"You'd kiss Nick as well."

"What a crude, tasteless girl!"

"I don't care!" Daisy exclaimed, starting to dance on the brick fireplace. Then, remembering the heat, she guiltily sat down on the couch just as a freshly dressed nanny, leading a little girl, entered the room.

"Blessed precious," she cooed, holding out her arms. "Come to your own mother who loves you."

The child, handed over by the nanny, rushed across the room and nestled shyly against her mother's dress.

"Did mommy get powder in your beautiful blonde hair? Stand up now, and say—How are you?"

Gatsby and I, in turn, leaned down and gently took the small, hesitant hand. Afterward, he kept staring at the child with astonishment. I don't think he had ever truly accepted she was real before.

"I got dressed before lunch," said the child, turning eagerly to Daisy.

"That's because your mom wanted to show you off." Her face bent into the single wrinkle of the small white neck. "You dream, you. You absolute little dream."

"Yes," admitted the child calmly. "Aunt Jordan's wearing a white dress too."

"How do you like mom's friends?" Daisy turned her around so that she faced Gatsby. "Do you think they're pretty?"

"Where's Daddy?"

"She doesn't resemble her father," Daisy clarified. "She takes after me. She has my hair and the shape of my face."

Daisy leaned back on the couch. The nurse stepped forward and extended her hand.

"Come, Pammy."

"Goodbye, sweetheart!"

With a hesitant look back, the well-behaved child clung to her nanny's hand and was led out the door, just as Tom returned, leading the way for four gin rickeys that clinked, brimming with ice.

Gatsby picked up his drink.

"They definitely seem relaxed," he said, his tension evident.

We drank deeply, eagerly.

“I read somewhere that the sun’s getting hotter every year,” said Tom in a friendly manner. “It seems that pretty soon the earth’s going to fall into the sun—or wait a minute—it’s just the opposite—the sun’s getting colder every year.

“Come outside,” he suggested to Gatsby, “I’d like you to have a look at the place.”

I went with them out to the veranda. On the green Sound, stagnant in the heat, one small sailboat crawled slowly toward the fresher sea. Gatsby’s eyes followed it momentarily; he raised his hand and pointed across the bay.

"I'm right across from you."

"So you are."

Our gaze traveled over the rose beds and the sun-drenched lawn, past the neglected remnants of the summer's peak along the shore. Gradually, the white sails of the boat glided against the serene blue boundary of the sky. Ahead, the rippled ocean stretched out, leading to the plentiful, blissful islands.

"There's entertainment for you," said Tom, nodding. "I'd like to be out there with him for about an hour."

We had lunch in the dining room, darkened too against the heat, and drowned our anxious cheerfulness with the cold beer.

"What'll we do with ourselves this afternoon?" cried Daisy, "and the day after that, and the next thirty years?"

"Don't be so gloomy," Jordan said. "Life starts all over again when it gets crisp in the fall."

"But it's so hot," Daisy insisted, on the verge of tears, "and everything's so confusing. Let's all go to the city!"

Her voice fought through the heat, pushing against it, shaping its chaos into something tangible.

"I've heard of turning a garage into a stable," Tom was saying to Gatsby, "but I'm the first person who ever turned a stable back into a garage."

"Who wants to go to the city?" demanded Daisy insistently. Gatsby's gaze drifted toward her. "Ah," she exclaimed, "you look so calm."

Their eyes locked, and they stared at each other, isolated in their own universe. With an effort, she looked away, down at the table.

"You always look so cool," she repeated.

She had told him that she loved him, and Tom Buchanan saw. He was astounded. His mouth opened slightly, and he looked at Gatsby, and then back at Daisy as if he had just recognized her as someone he knew a long time ago.

"You're like the guy in that ad," she went on innocently. "You know, the ad with the man—"

"All right," Tom interjected swiftly, "I'm totally up for heading into the city. Let's go—we're all going to the city."

He stood up, his gaze still darting between Gatsby and his wife. No one stirred.

"Come on!" His patience began to fray. "What's the issue, anyway? If we're heading to the city, let's get moving."

His hand, shaking from his effort to stay composed, brought the last of his glass of ale to his lips. Daisy's voice got us up and out onto the blazing gravel driveway.

"Are we just going to leave?" she objected. "Just like this? Aren't we going to let anyone have a cigarette first?"

"Everybody smoked all through lunch."

"Oh, let's just enjoy ourselves," she pleaded with him. "It's too hot to argue."

He remained silent.

"Suit yourself," she said. "Come on, Jordan."

They went upstairs to prepare, leaving us three men standing there, idly kicking the hot pebbles beneath our feet. A silver crescent of the moon was already visible in the western sky. Gatsby began to speak, hesitated, but not before Tom turned sharply to face him, anticipation in his gaze.

"Do you keep your horses nearby?" Gatsby asked, forcing the words out.

"About a quarter of a mile down the road."

"Oh."

A pause.

"I don't see the point of going into the city," Tom burst out fiercely. "Women get these ideas in their heads—"

"Should we bring something to drink?" Daisy called from an upstairs window.

"I'll grab some whiskey," Tom replied. He went inside.

Gatsby turned to me stiffly:

"I can't say anything in his house, old sport."

"She's got an indiscreet voice," I remarked. "It's full of—" I hesitated.

"Her voice is full of money," he said suddenly.

That was it. I’d never understood before. It was full of money—that was the inexhaustible charm that rose and fell in it, the jingle of it, the cymbals’ song of it… High in a white palace, the king’s daughter, the golden girl…

Tom emerged from the house, wrapping a quart bottle in a towel, followed by Daisy and Jordan wearing sleek, tight hats made of metallic fabric and carrying light capes over their arms.

“Shall we all go in my car?” Gatsby suggested. He felt the hot, green leather of the seat. “I should have parked it in the shade.”

"Is it manual transmission?" demanded Tom.

"Yes."

"Well, you take my coupe and let me drive your car to town."

The suggestion was repulsive to Gatsby.

“I don’t think there’s much gas,” he objected.

“Plenty of gas,” said Tom loudly. He checked the fuel gauge. “And if it runs out, I can stop at a convenience store. You can buy anything at a convenience store nowadays.”

A pause followed this seemingly irrelevant comment. Daisy looked at Tom, her brow furrowed, and an indescribable expression, both completely new and oddly familiar, as if I had only heard about it, crossed Gatsby’s face.

"Come on, Daisy," said Tom, nudging her with his hand toward Gatsby's car. "I'll take you in this circus wagon."

He opened the door, but she stepped away from his embrace.

"You take Nick and Jordan. We'll follow you in the coupe."

She walked close to Gatsby, touching his coat with her hand. Jordan, Tom, and I climbed into the front seat of Gatsby's car. Tom cautiously navigated the unfamiliar gears, and we sped off into the oppressive heat, leaving them out of sight behind.

"Did you see that?" demanded Tom.

"See what?"

He looked at me sharply, aware that Jordan and I must have been in on the secret from the start.

"You think I'm not very smart, don't you?" he proposed. "Maybe I'm not, but I have—almost a sixth sense, sometimes, that guides me on what to do. You might not buy into that, but science—"

He paused. The pressing situation caught up with him, pulling him back from the brink of speculative danger.

"I've done some digging into this guy," he continued. "I could have looked further into it if I'd known—"

"Do you mean you've consulted a psychic?" Jordan asked, jokingly.

"What?" Confused, he looked at us as we laughed. "A medium?"

"About Gatsby."

"About Gatsby! No, I haven't. I said I've been doing a little digging into his past."

"And you discovered he went to Oxford," said Jordan, offering a helpful nudge.

"An Oxford man!" He was incredulous. "No way! He wears a pink suit."

"Nevertheless, he's an Oxford man."

"Oxford, New Mexico," Tom scoffed with disdain, "or something like that."

"Listen, Tom. If you're such a snob, why did you invite him to lunch?" Jordan asked sharply.

"Daisy invited him; she knew him before we were married—God knows where!"

We were all getting cranky now, the buzz from our drinks wearing off, and knowing it, we drove in silence for a bit. Then, as we approached the dilapidated billboard featuring the eyes of Doctor T. J. Eckleburg, I recalled Gatsby's warning about fuel.

"We have enough gas to make it to the city," said Tom.

"But there's a gas station right here," Jordan countered. "I don't want us to break down in this scorching heat."

Tom slammed on the brakes impatiently, and we skidded to an abrupt, dusty stop under Wilson's sign. After a moment, the proprietor emerged from the depths of his establishment and looked hollow-eyed at the car.

"Fill it up with gas!" Tom shouted roughly. "What do you think we stopped for—to admire the view?"

"I'm sick," said Wilson, without moving. "Been sick all day."

"What's wrong?"

"I'm completely exhausted."

"Well, should I just help myself?" Tom demanded. "You sounded fine on the phone."

With an effort, Wilson left the comfort and support of the doorway and, breathing heavily, unscrewed the cap of the gas tank. In the sunlight, his face looked pale and sickly.

"I didn't mean to interrupt your lunch," he said. "But I'm really in need of money, and I was wondering what you were planning to do with your old car."

"How do you like this one?" Tom asked. "I bought it last week."

"It's a nice yellow one," said Wilson, as he struggled with the handle.

"Interested in buying it?"

"Big chance," Wilson smiled weakly. "No, but I could make some money on the other."

"What do you need money for, all of a sudden?"

"I've been here too long. I want to get away. My wife and I want to go West."

"Your wife does," exclaimed Tom, startled.

"She's been talking about it for a decade." He paused for a moment against the pump, shielding his eyes. "And now she's going whether she likes it or not. I'm going to take her away."

The coupe sped past us, kicking up a cloud of dust and a glimpse of a waving hand.

"What do I owe you?" Tom asked sharply.

"I just realized something odd in the last two days," remarked Wilson. "That's why I want to leave. That's why I've been pestering you about the car."

"What do I owe you?"

"A dollar twenty."

The relentless heat was beginning to confuse me, and I had a bad moment before I realized that his suspicions hadn’t yet focused on Tom. He had discovered that Myrtle had a life apart from him in another world, and the shock had made him physically ill. I looked from him to Tom, who had made a similar discovery less than an hour before—and it struck me that there was no difference between men, in intelligence or race, as profound as the difference between the sick and the well. Wilson was so ill that he appeared guilty, unforgivably guilty—as if he had just impregnated some poor girl.

“I’ll let you have that car,” said Tom. “I’ll send it over tomorrow afternoon.”

That area was always unsettling, even in the bright light of afternoon, and now I turned my head as if I had been warned of something behind. Over the ash-heaps, the giant eyes of Doctor T. J. Eckleburg kept their vigil, but I realized, after a moment, that other eyes were watching us with peculiar intensity from less than twenty feet away.

In one of the windows above the garage, the curtains had been slightly pulled aside, and Myrtle Wilson was peering down at the car. She was so absorbed that she didn't realize she was being watched, and various emotions crossed her face like images appearing in a developing photo. Her expression was oddly familiar—it was one I had often seen on women's faces, but on Myrtle Wilson's face, it seemed without purpose and baffling until I understood that her eyes, wide with jealous terror, were not fixed on Tom, but on Jordan Baker, whom she mistook for his wife.

There's nothing quite like the confusion of a simple mind, and as we drove away, Tom was seized by a sudden panic. His wife and his mistress, until an hour ago secure and untouched, were now quickly slipping from his grasp. Instinctively, he pressed down on the accelerator, aiming both to catch up with Daisy and to leave Wilson far behind, and we sped toward Astoria at fifty miles an hour. Soon, amidst the intricate steel framework of the elevated tracks, we caught sight of the relaxed blue coupé.

"Those big movies around Fiftieth Street are cool," suggested Jordan. "I love New York on summer afternoons when everyone's away. There's something very sensuous about it—overripe, as if all sorts of funny fruits were going to fall into your hands."

The word "sensuous" had the effect of further disquieting Tom, but before he could invent a protest, the coupe came to a stop, and Daisy signaled us to draw up alongside.

"Where are we going?" she cried.

"How about the movies?"

"It's so hot," she complained. "You go. We'll drive around and meet you after." With an effort, her wit rose faintly. "We'll meet you on some corner. I'll be the one smoking two cigarettes."

"We can't argue about it here," Tom said impatiently, as a truck honked angrily behind us. "Follow me to the south side of Central Park, in front of the Plaza."

Several times he turned his head and looked back for their car, and if the traffic delayed them, he slowed up until they came into sight. I think he was afraid they would dart down a side street and out of his life forever.

But they didn’t. And we all took the less explicable step of booking the parlor of a suite in the Plaza Hotel.

The prolonged and tumultuous argument that ended by herding us into that room eludes me, though I have a sharp physical memory that, in the course of it, my underwear kept climbing like a damp snake around my legs and intermittent beads of sweat raced cool across my back. The notion originated with Daisy’s suggestion that we rent five bathrooms and take cold baths, and then assumed more tangible form as “a place to have a mint julep.” Each of us said over and over that it was a “crazy idea”—we all talked at once to a baffled clerk and thought, or pretended to think, that we were being very funny …

The room was spacious and suffocating, and, even though it was already four o'clock, opening the windows only let in a blast of hot foliage from the Park. Daisy went to the mirror and stood with her back to us, adjusting her hair.

"It's an impressive suite," whispered Jordan respectfully, and everyone laughed.

"Open another window," Daisy ordered, without turning around.

"There aren't any more."

"Well, we'd better call for an axe—"

"The thing to do is to forget about the heat," said Tom impatiently. "You make it ten times worse by complaining about it."

He unwrapped the bottle of whiskey from the towel and placed it on the table.

“Why not leave her be, old sport?” Gatsby remarked. “You’re the one who wanted to come into the city.”

A moment of silence followed. The phone book slipped from its nail and dropped to the floor, at which Jordan whispered, “Excuse me”—but this time, no one laughed.

"I'll get it," I offered.

"I've got this." Gatsby examined the broken string, muttered "Hmm!" with interest, and tossed the book onto a chair.

"That's a favorite phrase of yours, isn't it?" said Tom sharply.

"What's that?"

"All this 'old sport' talk. Where did you get that from?"

"Now listen, Tom," said Daisy, turning away from the mirror, "if you're going to start with the personal attacks, I won't stay here a second longer. Call and order some ice for the mint julep."

As Tom picked up the phone, the stifling heat burst into sound, and we found ourselves listening to the ominous strains of Mendelssohn’s Wedding March coming from the ballroom below.

"Can you imagine getting married in this heat?" Jordan exclaimed in despair.

"Yet—I got married in the middle of June," Daisy recalled. "Louisville in June! Someone passed out. Who was it that passed out, Tom?"

"Biloxi," he replied tersely.

"A guy named Biloxi. 'Blocks' Biloxi, and he made boxes—that's true—and he hailed from Biloxi, Tennessee."

"They brought him into my house," Jordan added, "because we lived just two doors down from the church. And he stayed three weeks, until my dad told him he had to leave. The day after he left, my dad passed away." After a pause, she added, as though she might have sounded disrespectful, "There wasn't any connection."

"I used to know a Bill Biloxi from Memphis," I remarked.

"That was his cousin. I knew his whole family history before he left. He gave me an aluminum putter that I use today."

The music had quieted down as the ceremony started and now a long cheer drifted in through the window, followed by intermittent shouts of "Yea—ea—ea!" and finally by a burst of jazz as the dancing started.

"We're getting old," said Daisy. "If we were young, we'd get up and dance."

"Remember Biloxi," Jordan warned her. "Where did you meet him, Tom?"

"Biloxi?" He focused with an effort. "I didn't know him. He was a friend of Daisy's."

"He was not," she denied. "I'd never seen him before. He arrived in the private car."

"Well, he claimed he knew you. He said he grew up in Louisville. Asa Bird introduced him at the last minute and asked if we could make room for him."

Jordan smiled.

"He was likely hitchhiking his way home. He told me he was president of your class at Yale."

Tom and I exchanged puzzled glances.

"Biloxi?"

"First off, we didn't have any president—"

Gatsby's foot tapped a short, restless rhythm, and Tom looked at him sharply.

"By the way, Mr. Gatsby, I hear you went to Oxford."

"Not exactly."

"Oh, yes, I understand you went to Oxford."

"Yes—I did go there."

A pause. Then Tom's voice, incredulous and mocking:

"You must have shown up around the same time Biloxi headed to New Haven."

Another pause. A waiter knocked and entered with crushed mint and ice, but the silence remained unbroken by his "thank you" and the gentle shutting of the door. This significant detail was finally going to be clarified.

"I told you I went there," said Gatsby.

"I heard you, but I'd like to know when."

"It was in 1919, I only stayed five months. That's why I can't really call myself an Oxford man."

Tom glanced around to see if we shared his skepticism. But we were all looking at Gatsby.

“It was an opportunity they offered to some of the officers after the armistice,” he continued. “We could attend any of the universities in England or France.”

I wanted to stand up and give him a supportive pat on the back. I had one of those moments of renewed complete faith in him that I’d experienced before.

Daisy rose, her smile subtle, and approached the table.

"Open the whiskey, Tom," she commanded, "and I'll mix you a mint julep. Then you might not feel so foolish... Look at the mint!"

"Hold on," Tom snapped, "I have another question for Mr. Gatsby."

"Continue," Gatsby said courteously.

"What kind of trouble are you trying to stir up in my house anyway?"

They were finally out in the open, and Gatsby was satisfied.

“He isn’t causing a scene,” Daisy looked desperately from one to the other. “You’re causing a scene. Please, show some self-control.”

“Self-control!” Tom repeated, incredulous. “I guess the new trend is to just sit back and let Mr. Nobody from Nowhere flirt with your wife. Well, if that’s the plan, count me out… These days, people start by mocking family life and its values, and next thing you know, they’ll be tossing everything aside in favor of mixing races.”

Flushed with his impassioned nonsense, he saw himself as the lone defender on the final frontier of civilization.

"We're all on the same side here," murmured Jordan.

"I know I'm not the life of the party. I don't throw massive parties. I guess you have to turn your house into a complete mess if you want to have any friends—in today's world."

As angry as I was, as we all were, I couldn't help but feel tempted to laugh whenever he spoke. The shift from a free-spirited person to a stickler was just so absolute.

"I've got something to tell you, old sport—" began Gatsby. But Daisy guessed at his intention.

"Please don't!" she interrupted, feeling overwhelmed. "Please, let's all go home. Why don't we all go home?"

"That's a good idea," I stood up. "Come on, Tom. Nobody wants a drink."

"I want to know what Mr. Gatsby has to say to me."

"Your wife doesn't love you," Gatsby declared. "She's never loved you. She loves me."

"You must be out of your mind!" Tom blurted out without thinking.

Gatsby leaped up, alive with excitement.

"She never loved you, do you understand?" he shouted. "She only married you because I was broke and she was tired of waiting for me. It was a huge mistake, but deep down, she never loved anyone but me!"

At this moment, Jordan and I attempted to leave, but Tom and Gatsby demanded with equal intensity that we stay—as if neither had anything to hide and it would be an honor to indirectly experience their emotions.

"Sit down, Daisy," Tom's voice groped unsuccessfully for a paternal tone. "What's been happening? I want to hear all about it."

"I told you what's been happening," said Gatsby. "Happening for five years—and you didn't know."

Tom turned to Daisy sharply.

"You've been involved with this guy for five years?"

"Not involved," said Gatsby. "No, we couldn't meet. But both of us loved each other all that time, old sport, and you were clueless. I used to laugh sometimes"—but his eyes were void of any laughter—"thinking about how clueless you were."

"Oh—that's it." Tom tapped his thick fingers together like a clergyman and leaned back in his chair.

"You're out of your mind!" he burst out. "I can't talk about what happened five years ago, because I didn't know Daisy then—and I'll be damned if I understand how you got close to her unless you were delivering groceries to the back door. But everything else you've said is a complete lie. Daisy loved me when she married me and she loves me now."

"No," said Gatsby, shaking his head.

"She does, though. The problem is that sometimes she gets silly notions in her head and doesn't realize what she's doing." He nodded wisely. "And what's more, I love Daisy too. Sure, occasionally I lose control and embarrass myself, but I always return, and deep down, I love her all the time."

"You're disgusting," said Daisy. She turned to me, and her voice, dropping an octave lower, filled the room with thrilling scorn: "Do you know why we left Chicago? I'm surprised they didn't share the story of that wild escapade with you."

Gatsby walked over and stood beside her.

"Daisy, that's all in the past," he said earnestly. "It doesn't matter anymore. Just tell him the truth—that you never loved him—and it's all erased forever."

She looked at him without seeing. "Why—how could I ever love him—possibly?"

"You never loved him."

She hesitated. Her gaze landed on Jordan and me with a kind of plea, as if she finally understood what she was doing—and as if she had never, from the start, planned to do anything at all. But it was done now. It was too late.

"I never loved him," she admitted, her voice tinged with a noticeable hesitation.

"Not even at Kapiolani?" Tom pressed, his question sharp and unexpected.

"No."

From the ballroom below, muted and stifling chords floated up on hot currents of air.

“Not that day I carried you down from the Punch Bowl to keep your shoes dry?” His voice carried a husky tenderness... “Daisy?”

“Please don’t.” Her voice was cold, yet devoid of bitterness. She turned to Gatsby. “There, Jay,” she said—but her hand trembled as she attempted to light a cigarette. Suddenly, she tossed the cigarette and the lit match onto the carpet.

"Oh, you're asking for too much!" she exclaimed to Gatsby. "I love you now—isn't that enough? I can't change what's happened." She began to sob uncontrollably. "I did love him once—but I loved you too."

Gatsby's eyes opened and closed.

"You loved me too?" he echoed.

"Even that's a lie," Tom said fiercely. "She didn't know you existed. Why—there are things between Daisy and me that you'll never understand, things that neither of us can ever forget."

The words seemed to physically wound Gatsby.

"I want to talk to Daisy alone," he insisted. "She's all worked up now—"

"Even when I'm alone, I can't say I never loved Tom," she admitted in a pitiful voice. "It wouldn't be true."

"Of course it wouldn't," agreed Tom.

She turned to her husband.

"As if it mattered to you," she said.

"Of course it matters. I'm going to take better care of you from now on."

"You don't understand," said Gatsby, with a touch of panic. "You're not going to take care of her anymore."

"I'm not?" Tom widened his eyes in surprise and laughed. He felt he could relax now. "Why's that?"

"Daisy's leaving you."

"Nonsense."

"I am, though," she said, clearly straining.

"She's not leaving me!" Tom's voice suddenly bore down on Gatsby. "Definitely not for a common swindler who'd have to steal the ring he put on her finger."

"I can't handle this!" cried Daisy. "Oh, please, let's go."

"Who are you, anyway?" Tom burst out. "You're part of that crowd that hangs out with Meyer Wolfshiem—that much I know for sure. I've done some digging into your business—and I'll dig deeper tomorrow."

"You can do as you please with that, old sport," Gatsby replied calmly.

"I discovered what your 'drugstores' really were." He turned to us and spoke quickly. "He and this Wolfshiem acquired a bunch of small-time drugstores here and in Chicago and sold grain alcohol right over the counter. That's one of his schemes. I pegged him as a bootlegger the first time I laid eyes on him, and I wasn't far off."

"What about it?" said Gatsby, with politeness. "I suppose your friend Walter Chase wasn't too proud to get involved."

"And you abandoned him, didn't you? You let him end up in jail for a month over in New Jersey. God! You should hear Walter talk about you."

"He came to us completely broke. He was very happy to make some money, old sport."

"Don't you call me 'old sport'!" yelled Tom. Gatsby remained silent. "Walter could have reported you for the gambling laws too, but Wolfshiem intimidated him into silence."

That unique yet familiar expression returned to Gatsby's face.

"That drugstore scheme was just petty cash," Tom went on slowly, "but you're involved in something now that Walter's too scared to even mention to me."

I glanced at Daisy, who was staring terrified between Gatsby and her husband, and at Jordan, who had begun to balance an invisible but absorbing object on the tip of her chin. Then I turned back to Gatsby—and was startled at his expression. He looked—and this is said with all contempt for the baseless rumors of his parties—as if he had “killed a man.” For a moment, his face could be described in just that surreal way.

It passed, and he began to talk excitedly to Daisy, denying everything, defending his reputation against accusations that had not been made. But with every word, she was drawing further and further into herself, so he gave that up, and only the dead dream fought on as the afternoon slipped away, trying to touch what was no longer tangible, struggling unhappily, without despair, toward that lost voice across the room.

The voice begged again to go.

"Please, Tom! I can't take this anymore."

Her frightened eyes revealed that any intentions, any courage she once had, were completely vanished.

"You two start heading home, Daisy," said Tom. "In Mr. Gatsby's car."

She looked at Tom, now visibly alarmed, but he insisted with a generous display of contempt.

"Go ahead. He won't bother you. I think he understands that his bold little fling is finished."

They left, without a word, vanished suddenly, turned into something accidental, isolated, like ghosts, beyond even our sympathy.

After a moment, Tom stood up and started wrapping the unopened bottle of whiskey in the towel.

"Do you want any of this? Jordan? ... Nick?"

I didn't respond.

"Nick?" He asked again.

"What?"

"Want some?"

“No … I just remembered that today’s my birthday.”

I was thirty. Before me stretched the portentous, menacing road of a new decade.

It was seven o’clock when we got into the coupe with him and started for Long Island. Tom talked incessantly, exulting and laughing, but his voice was as remote from Jordan and me as the foreign clamor on the sidewalk or the tumult of the elevated train overhead. Human sympathy has its limits, and we were content to let all their tragic arguments fade with the city lights behind. Thirty—the promise of a decade of loneliness, a dwindling list of single friends to know, a dwindling briefcase of enthusiasm, thinning hair. But there was Jordan beside me, who, unlike Daisy, was too wise ever to carry well-forgotten dreams from age to age. As we passed over the dark bridge, her wan face fell lazily against my coat’s shoulder and the formidable stroke of thirty died away with the reassuring pressure of her hand.

So we drove on toward death through the cooling twilight.

The young Greek, Michaelis, who ran the café beside the ash-heaps, was the main witness at the inquest. He had slept through the afternoon heat until after five, when he walked over to the garage and found George Wilson sick in his office—truly sick, as pale as his own light hair and trembling all over. Michaelis suggested he go to bed, but Wilson refused, arguing that he'd lose too much business if he did. While his neighbor was trying to convince him, a loud commotion erupted upstairs.

"I've got my wife locked up there," explained Wilson calmly. "She's going to stay there until the day after tomorrow, and then we're going to move away."

Michaelis was astonished; they had been neighbors for four years, and Wilson had never seemed remotely capable of such a statement. Generally, he was one of those worn-out men: when he wasn't working, he sat on a chair in the doorway and watched the people and cars that passed along the road. When anyone spoke to him, he invariably laughed in an agreeable, colorless way. He was his wife's man and not his own.

So naturally, Michaelis tried to find out what had happened, but Wilson wouldn't say a word—instead, he began to throw curious, suspicious glances at his visitor and ask him what he'd been doing at certain times on certain days. Just as the latter was getting uneasy, some workers came past the door heading for his restaurant, and Michaelis took the opportunity to leave, intending to come back later. But he didn't. "Beat me!" she screamed. "Throw me down and beat me, you coward!"

Moments later, she burst into the twilight, flailing her arms and yelling—before he could even step out, it was all over.

The "death car," as the media dubbed it, didn't stop; it emerged from the dim light, swerved tragically for an instant, then vanished around the next curve. Michaelis and the man got to her first, but as they ripped open her blouse, still damp with sweat, they saw her left breast was hanging free like a flap, and it was clear there was no need to check for a heartbeat. "The damn coward!" he whimpered. "He didn’t even stop his car."

"I'll call a taxi to take you home, and while you're waiting, you and Jordan should go into the kitchen and have them prepare you some supper—if you're hungry." He opened the door. "Come in."

I'd be damned if I went in; I'd had my fill of them all for the day, and suddenly that included Jordan too. I sat for a few minutes, head in hands, until I heard the phone picked up inside and the butler's voice summoning a taxi. "What are you doing?" I asked.

It all happened so fast, but it seemed to me that she wanted to talk to us, thinking perhaps we were someone she recognized. Initially, Daisy swerved away from the woman towards the other car, but then she panicked and swerved back. The moment my hand touched the wheel, I felt the impact—it must have killed her instantly.”

A new perspective occurred to me. Suppose Tom discovered that Daisy had been driving. He might see a connection in it—he might think anything. I looked at the house; there were two or three bright windows downstairs and the pink glow from Daisy's room on the ground floor.

"You wait here," I said. "I'll check if there's any sign of a commotion."

I walked back along the edge of the lawn, moved quietly across the gravel, and tiptoed up the veranda steps. The curtains of the living room were open, and I saw that the room was empty. Crossing the porch where we had dined that June night three months earlier, I came to a small rectangle of light which I guessed was the pantry window. The blind was drawn, but I found a gap at the sill.

Daisy and Tom were sitting opposite each other at the kitchen table, with a plate of cold fried chicken between them, and two bottles of beer. He was talking intently across the table at her, and in his earnestness, his hand had fallen upon and covered her own. Once in a while, she looked up at him and nodded in agreement.

They weren’t happy, and neither of them had touched the chicken or the beer—and yet they weren’t unhappy either. There was an unmistakable air of natural intimacy about the scene, and anyone would have said that they were conspiring together.

As I tiptoed from the porch, I heard my taxi navigating the dark road toward the house. Gatsby was waiting where I had left him in the driveway.

"Is everything okay up there?" he asked anxiously.

"Yes, it's all quiet." I hesitated. "You should head home and get some sleep."

He shook his head.

"I want to wait here until Daisy goes to bed. Good night, old sport."

He put his hands in his coat pockets and turned back eagerly to his scrutiny of the house, as if my presence tarnished the sanctity of his vigil. So, I walked away and left him standing there in the moonlight—watching over nothing.

I couldn’t sleep all night; a foghorn was groaning incessantly on the Sound, and I tossed, half-sick, between grotesque reality and savage, frightening dreams. Toward dawn, I heard a taxi go up Gatsby’s drive, and immediately I jumped out of bed and began to dress—I felt that I had something to tell him, something to warn him about, and morning would be too late.

Crossing his lawn, I saw that his front door was still open and he was leaning against a table in the hall, heavy with dejection or sleep.

"Nothing happened," he said weakly. "I waited, and around four o'clock she came to the window, stood there for a moment, and then turned off the light."

His house had never seemed as vast to me as it did that night when we searched the grand rooms for cigarettes. We moved aside curtains that felt like grand tents, and groped along countless feet of dark wall for light switches—once, I accidentally hit the keys of a spectral piano, creating a ghostly echo. There was an unexplainable amount of dust everywhere, and the rooms smelled musty, as if they hadn't been opened for days. I found the cigar box on an unfamiliar table, with two stale, dry cigarettes inside. Opening the French doors of the living room, we sat smoking, looking out into the darkness.

"You should leave," I suggested. "It's almost certain they'll figure out it was your car."

"Leave now, my friend?"

"Head to Atlantic City for a week, or maybe up to Montreal."

He wouldn't think of it. He couldn't possibly leave Daisy until he knew what her next move would be. He was holding onto some final hope, and I couldn't bring myself to snap him out of it.

It was on this night that he shared with me the peculiar tale of his youth with Dan Cody—shared because "Jay Gatsby" had shattered like glass against Tom's unyielding malice, and the long-hidden spectacle had come to an end. I believe he would have confessed to anything at that point, without holding back, but he wanted to talk about Daisy.

She was the first "nice" girl he had ever met. In various undisclosed roles, he had encountered such people, but always with an invisible barrier between them. He found her incredibly desirable. He visited her home, initially with other officers from Camp Taylor, then on his own. It astonished him—he had never been in such a magnificent home before. But what gave it an aura of thrilling intensity was the fact that Daisy lived there—it was as commonplace to her as his tent out at camp was to him. There was a rich mystery about it, a suggestion of bedrooms upstairs more elegant and cool than any others, of lively and radiant activities happening through its halls, and of romances that weren't stale and stored away but fresh and alive, fragrant with the scent of this year's sleek cars and of dances whose flowers had barely begun to fade. It thrilled him, too, that many men had already loved Daisy—it added to her allure in his eyes. He felt their presence throughout the house, filling the air with the remnants and echoes of still vivid emotions.

But he knew his presence in Daisy's house was a monumental fluke. No matter how splendid his future as Jay Gatsby might be, he was currently a penniless young man without a past, and at any moment the invisible cloak of his uniform might slip from his shoulders. So, he seized the moment. He took what he could, greedily and without scruples—ultimately, he took Daisy one still October night, took her because he had no real right even to touch her hand.

He might have despised himself, for he had certainly misled her under false pretenses. I don’t mean that he had lied about his phantom millions, but he had deliberately given Daisy a sense of security; he let her believe that he came from a background similar to her own—that he was fully capable of taking care of her. In reality, he lacked such means—he had no supportive family backing him, and he was at the mercy of an impersonal government, vulnerable to being sent anywhere around the world.

But he didn’t despise himself, and things didn’t turn out as he had imagined. He had probably intended to take what he could and leave—but now he found that he had committed himself to the pursuit of a dream. He knew Daisy was extraordinary, but he hadn’t realized just how extraordinary a “nice” girl could be. She disappeared into her luxurious house, into her rich, full life, leaving Gatsby with nothing. He felt bound to her, that was all.

When they met again, two days later, it was Gatsby who was breathless, who felt somehow betrayed. Her porch was bright with the purchased luxury of starlight; the wicker of the settee squeaked stylishly as she turned toward him and he kissed her curious and lovely mouth. She had caught a cold, making her voice huskier and more charming than ever, and Gatsby was overwhelmingly aware of the youth and mystery that wealth traps and preserves, of the allure of numerous outfits, and of Daisy, shining like silver, secure and proud above the heated struggles of the less fortunate.

“I can’t describe to you how surprised I was to find out I loved her, old sport. I even hoped for a while that she’d break up with me, but she didn’t, because she was in love with me too. She thought I was so knowledgeable because I knew different things from her… Well, there I was, far from my ambitions, falling deeper in love every minute, and all of a sudden, I didn’t care. What was the point of achieving great things if I could have a better time sharing my dreams with her?”

On the last afternoon before he went abroad, he held Daisy in his arms for a long, silent time. It was a chilly fall day, with a fire warming the room and her cheeks rosy. Now and then she shifted, and he adjusted his arm slightly, and once he kissed her dark, shining hair. The afternoon brought them a tranquil moment, as if to grant them a deep memory for the long separation the next day would bring. They had never been closer in their month of love, nor shared more deeply with one another, than when she quietly pressed her lips against his coat’s shoulder or when he touched the tips of her fingers, gently, as though she were asleep.

He performed exceptionally well in World War I. He was promoted to captain before being deployed to the front lines, and after the battles in the Argonne, he earned a promotion to major and took command of the division's machine guns. After the armistice, he desperately tried to return home, but due to some complication or misunderstanding, he ended up at Oxford instead. He was now worried—Daisy's letters were filled with a nervous despair. She couldn't understand why he couldn't come back. She felt the pressure of the outside world and longed for his presence to reassure her that she was indeed making the right choice.

For Daisy was young, and her superficial world was filled with the scent of orchids, cheerful elitism, and orchestras that dictated the year's rhythm, capturing the melancholy and suggestiveness of life in their new melodies. All night, the saxophones lamented the futile sentiment of the "Beale Street Blues," while a hundred pairs of golden and silver shoes scattered the glittering dust. During the dim tea hour, there were always rooms pulsating with this gentle, sweet fever, as fresh faces floated around like rose petals, carried by the mournful tunes across the dance floor.

Through this twilight universe, Daisy began to move again with the season; suddenly, she was once more juggling multiple dates a day with various men, and falling asleep at dawn with the beads and chiffon of her evening dress entangled among wilting orchids on the floor beside her bed. And all the while, something inside her was screaming for a decision. She wanted her life to take shape now, immediately—and the decision had to be made by some force—be it love, money, or undeniable practicality—that was within reach.

That force took shape in the middle of spring with the arrival of Tom Buchanan. There was a reassuring solidity to both his presence and his status, and Daisy was charmed. No doubt, there was a mix of conflict and relief. The letter reached Gatsby while he was still at Oxford.

It was dawn now on Long Island, and we went about opening the rest of the windows downstairs, filling the house with grey-turning, gold-turning light. The shadow of a tree fell abruptly across the dew, and ghostly birds began to sing among the blue leaves. There was a slow, pleasant movement in the air, scarcely a wind, promising a cool, lovely day.

“I don’t think she ever loved him.” Gatsby turned around from a window and looked at me challengingly. “You must remember, old sport, she was very excited this afternoon. He told her those things in a way that frightened her—that made it look as if I was some kind of cheap con artist. And the result was she hardly knew what she was saying.”

He sat down gloomily.

"Of course, she might have loved him for just a moment, when they first got married—and even then, loved me more, do you see?"

Suddenly, he shared a peculiar thought.

"In any case," he said, "it was purely personal."

What could you make of that, except to suspect some intensity in his conception of the affair that couldn’t be measured?

He returned from France when Tom and Daisy were still on their honeymoon, and made a miserable but irresistible journey to Louisville with the last of his army pay. He stayed there a week, walking the streets where their footsteps had clicked together through the November night and revisiting the secluded spots they had visited in her white car. Just as Daisy’s house had always seemed to him more mysterious and vibrant than other houses, so his idea of the city itself, even though she was gone from it, was imbued with a melancholy beauty.

He left feeling that if he had searched harder, he might have found her—that he was leaving her behind. The day-coach—he was penniless now—was hot. He went out to the open vestibule and sat down on a folding chair, and the station slid away and the backs of unfamiliar buildings moved by. Then out into the spring fields, where a yellow trolley raced them for a minute with people in it who might once have seen the pale magic of her face along the casual street.

The track curved, now moving away from the sun, which, as it descended, seemed to cast a blessing over the disappearing city where she had lived. Around five o'clock, our procession of three cars arrived at the cemetery and halted in a steady drizzle by the gate—first a hearse, ominously black and soaked, followed by Mr. Gatz, the minister, and myself in the limousine, and shortly after, four or five servants along with the postman from West Egg, crammed into Gatsby’s SUV, all drenched. As we made our way through the gate into the cemetery, I heard a car stop and then the sound of someone hurrying after us across the wet ground. Turning, I saw it was the man with owl-eyed glasses whom I had encountered in awe of Gatsby’s book collection in the library one night three months prior.

I hadn’t seen him since that night. How he learned of the funeral or even his name remained a mystery to me. The rain streamed down his thick glasses, prompting him to remove and wipe them to better see the protective tarp being rolled out over Gatsby’s grave.

I tried to think about Gatsby then for a moment, but he was already too far away, and I could only remember, without resentment, that Daisy hadn’t sent a message or a flower. Dimly, I heard someone murmur, “Blessed are the dead that the rain falls on,” and then the owl-eyed man said, “Amen to that,” in a brave voice.

We hurried down quickly through the rain to the cars. Owl-eyes spoke to me by the gate.

“I couldn’t get to the house,” he remarked.

"Neither could anybody else."

"Go on!" He was startled. "Why, my God! They used to flock there by the hundreds."

He removed his glasses and wiped them again, both outside and inside.

"The poor son-of-a-bitch," he said.

One of my most vivid memories is of coming back to the West from prep school and later from college during the Christmas season. Those of us who traveled beyond Chicago would meet up in the old, dimly lit Union Station at six o’clock on a December evening, alongside a few Chicago friends already swept up in their holiday festivities, to bid them a quick farewell. I remember the fur coats of the girls coming back from various exclusive schools and the chatter of frozen breath, the hands waving overhead as we spotted familiar faces, and the swapping of party invitations: “Are you going to the Ordways’? The Herseys’? The Schultzes’?” and the long green tickets held tightly in our gloved hands. And finally, the dimly lit yellow cars of the Chicago, Milwaukee, and St. Paul railroad, looking as cheerful as Christmas itself on the tracks beside the gate.

When we pulled out into the winter night and the real snow, our snow began to stretch out beside us and twinkle against the windows, and the dim lights of small Wisconsin stations moved by, a sharp wild brace came suddenly into the air. We drew in deep breaths of it as we walked back from dinner through the cold vestibules, unutterably aware of our identity with this country for one strange hour, before we melted indistinguishably into it again.

That’s my Middle West—not the wheat or the prairies or the lost Swede towns, but the thrilling returning trains of my youth, and the street lamps and sleigh bells in the frosty dark and the shadows of holly wreaths thrown by lighted windows on the snow. I am part of that, a little solemn with the feel of those long winters, a little complacent from growing up in the Carraway house in a city where dwellings are still called through decades by a family’s name. I see now that this has been a story of the West, after all—Tom and Gatsby, Daisy and Jordan and I, were all Westerners, and perhaps we possessed some deficiency in common which made us subtly unadaptable to Eastern life.

Even when the East excited me most, even when I was most keenly aware of its superiority to the bored, sprawling, swollen towns beyond the Ohio, with their interminable inquisitions which spared only the children and the very old—even then it had always for me a quality of distortion. West Egg, especially, still figures in my more fantastic dreams. I see it as a night scene by El Greco: a hundred houses, at once conventional and grotesque, crouching under a sullen, overhanging sky and a lustreless moon. In the foreground four solemn men in dress suits are walking along the sidewalk with a stretcher on which lies a drunken woman in a white evening dress. Her hand, which dangles over the side, sparkles cold with jewels. Gravely the men turn in at a house—the wrong house. But no one knows the woman’s name, and no one cares.

After Gatsby’s death, the East became a haunted place for me, its reality twisted beyond my ability to see it clearly. So, when the air filled with the blue smoke of burning leaves and the wind made the hanging laundry snap and crackle, I decided it was time to go back home.

There was one last thing I needed to do before leaving, a difficult and unpleasant task that might have been better left undone. But I wanted to leave everything tidy, not relying on the indifferent sea to wash away my troubles. I met with Jordan Baker to discuss what had happened between us and what had unfolded for me afterward. She listened intently, motionless in a large chair.

She was dressed for golf, and I remember thinking she looked like a perfect illustration, with her chin tilted up slightly, her hair the color of autumn leaves, and her face matching the brown hue of the glove resting on her knee. When I finished, she informed me without any elaboration that she was engaged to another man. I was skeptical, although I knew she had her pick of suitors, but I feigned surprise. For a moment, I questioned if I was making a mistake, but then I quickly reconsidered and stood up to say goodbye.

"Nevertheless, you did break up with me," said Jordan suddenly. "You broke up with me over the phone. I don't care about you now, but it was a new experience for me, and I felt a little dizzy for a while."

We shook hands.

"Oh, and do you remember," she added, "a conversation we had once about driving a car?"

"Why—not exactly."

"You said a bad driver was only safe until they met another bad driver? Well, I met another bad driver, didn't I? I mean, it was careless of me to make such a wrong guess. I thought you were rather an honest, straightforward person. I thought it was your secret pride."

"I'm thirty," I said. "I'm five years too old to lie to myself and call it honor."

She didn’t answer. Angry, and half in love with her, and tremendously sorry, I turned away.

One afternoon late in October, I saw Tom Buchanan. He was walking ahead of me along Fifth Avenue in his alert, aggressive manner, his hands slightly away from his body as if ready to fend off any disturbance, his head moving quickly in various directions, keeping pace with his restless gaze. Just as I slowed down to avoid catching up to him, he stopped and began frowning into the windows of a jewelry store. Suddenly, he saw me and walked back, extending his hand.

"What's wrong, Nick? Do you have a problem with shaking hands with me?"

"Yes. You know what I think of you."

"You're out of your mind, Nick," he said quickly. "Completely out of your mind. I don't know what's gotten into you."

"Tom," I asked, "what did you say to Wilson that afternoon?"

He looked at me silently, confirming my suspicions about those lost hours. As I began to walk away, he stepped closer and grabbed my arm.

"I told him the truth," he declared. "He showed up at the door while we were getting ready to leave, and when I sent down a message that we weren't available, he tried to barge his way upstairs. He was desperate enough to kill me if I hadn't told him who owned the car. He was gripping a revolver in his pocket the entire time he was in the house—" Tom stopped, his tone defiant. "So what if I did tell him? That guy had it coming. He deceived you just as he did Daisy, but he was no saint. He hit Myrtle like she was nothing more than a stray dog and didn’t even stop his car."

There was nothing I could say, except the one unutterable fact that it wasn't true.

“And if you think I didn’t have my share of suffering—look here, when I went to give up that apartment and saw that damn box of dog biscuits sitting there on the sideboard, I sat down and cried like a baby. By God, it was awful—”

I couldn’t forgive him or like him, but I saw that what he had done was, to him, entirely justified. It was all very careless and confused. They were careless people, Tom and Daisy—they smashed up things and creatures and then retreated back into their wealth or their vast carelessness, or whatever it was that kept them together, and let other people clean up the mess they had made…

I shook hands with him; it felt absurd not to, as I suddenly found myself speaking to him as if he were a child. Then he entered the jewelry store to purchase a pearl necklace—or maybe just a pair of cufflinks—freeing myself from my provincial reservations forever.

Gatsby's house remained deserted when I left—its lawn had become as unkempt as my own. One of the cab drivers in the village never passed the entrance gate without pausing for a moment to point inside; perhaps he was the one who had driven Daisy and Gatsby to East Egg on the night of the tragedy, and maybe he had concocted his own version of the events. I didn't want to hear it and made sure to avoid him whenever I disembarked from the train.

I spent my Saturday nights in New York because those gleaming, dazzling parties of his were with me so vividly that I could still hear the music and the laughter, faint and incessant, from his garden, and the cars going up and down his drive. One night I did hear a car there, and saw its lights stop at his front steps. But I didn’t investigate. Probably it was some final guest who had been away at the ends of the earth and didn’t know that the party was over.

On the last night, with my trunk packed and my car sold to the grocer, I went over and looked at that huge incoherent failure of a house once more. On the white steps an obscene word, scrawled by some boy with a piece of brick, stood out clearly in the moonlight, and I erased it, drawing my shoe raspingly along the stone. Then I wandered down to the beach and sprawled out on the sand.

Most of the big shore places were closed now and there were hardly any lights except the shadowy, moving glow of a ferryboat across the Sound. And as the moon rose higher the inessential houses began to melt away until gradually I became aware of the old island here that flowered once for Dutch sailors’ eyes—a fresh, green breast of the new world. Its vanished trees, the trees that had made way for Gatsby’s house, had once pandered in whispers to the last and greatest of all human dreams; for a transitory enchanted moment man must have held his breath in the presence of this continent, compelled into an aesthetic contemplation he neither understood nor desired, face to face for the last time in history with something commensurate to his capacity for wonder.

And as I sat there reflecting on the ancient, undiscovered world, I thought of Gatsby's awe when he first noticed the green light at the end of Daisy's dock. He had traveled a great distance to this blue lawn, and his dream must have seemed so near that he could almost seize it. He didn't realize that it was already behind him, lost in that vast shadowy expanse beyond the city, where the dark fields of the republic stretched out under the night.

Gatsby believed in the green light, the ecstatic future that year by year retreats before us. It escaped us then, but that's irrelevant—tomorrow we will run faster, stretch our arms wider... And one fine morning—

So we continue, like boats against the current, carried back ceaselessly into the past.

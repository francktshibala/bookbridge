She was one of those pretty and charming girls, born, as if by an error of fate, into a family of clerks. She had no dowry, no expectations, no means of becoming known or appreciated, loved and married by a man both prosperous and famous; and she allowed herself to marry a petty clerk in the office of the Board of Education. She dressed plainly because she could not dress well, but she was unhappy as if she had really fallen from a higher station. For women have no caste or class; their beauty, their grace, and their charm serve them in place of birth and fortune.
